        In the summer of 1145, in the half-Christian, half-Muslim
        translation workshops of Toledo, Adelard of Bath was rendering
        al-Khwārizmī's book on Hindu calculation into Latin. The

        Arabic *nisba* ending in Latin, so "Khwārizmī" became
        "Algorismi." This was not a simple transcription error but a
        *trans-continental misreading*, and the whole of Europe
        inherited it.

        Within a century "*algorismus*" had become the technical term
        for calculation with Hindu-Arabic numerals taught in European
        universities. Sacrobosco's *Algorismus vulgaris* (~1230) was a
        foundational textbook in Paris and Oxford; by the fourteenth
        century, mastery of "*algorisma*" procedures was a requirement
        for graduation in the German universities.

        Somewhere in this trajectory, the name was severed from the
        man. In Adelard's Latin, "Algorismi" still designated a person.
        A century later, "algorismus" had become the name of a
        *method* — no one was thinking of the person any longer. The
        geographical suffix of a man from Khwārizm had seeped into
        Europe's shared technical vocabulary; but the seepage cost him
        his face.

      en: |
        The *Bayt al-Ḥikma* founded by Caliph al-Maʾmūn (r. 813–833)
        was not merely a translation bureau but a *workshop of
        synthesis*. al-Khwārizmī arrived there, probably in his
        thirties, as an astronomer-mathematician summoned from the
        provinces to the capital. The caliph's patronage gave him
        access to three distinct traditions in their original textual
        forms: the Greek *Megístē* (Almagest), the Persian *zīj*
        tradition, and the Indian *siddhāntas*, including the
        *Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta*.

        Between roughly 825 and 833 he wrote three landmark books. The
        first was *al-Kitāb al-Mukhtaṣar fī Ḥisāb al-Jabr wa-l-Muqābala*:
        the work that gave the discipline of algebra its name, written
        for practical problems of inheritance, commerce, and land
        surveying. The second was *Kitāb al-Ḥisāb al-Hindī*: the
        treatise that introduced the Indian system — the decimal
        place-value system, including *zero* — to the Arab world. The
        third was *Zīj al-Sindhind*: a set of astronomical tables
        drawing on Indian sources. None of these was a book of
        *transmission*; each was a book of *synthesis*.

        Documents from the period present him not only as a writer but
        as a *librarian* and a *measurer*: at the caliph's command he
        took part in geodesic measurements in the Sinjār desert, and
        updated Ptolemy's geography for a new world map (*Ṣūrat
        al-Arḍ*). He was, in other words, no detached theorist but a
        measure-and-apply man — which means that the very concept his
        name would later carry, the *algorithm* as a *sequence of
        executable steps*, was already alive in his own practice.

      en: |
        al-Khwārizmī's work in Baghdad did not happen in a vacuum.
        Three distinct intellectual streams converged in the
        caliphate's library at the start of the ninth century — and
        when they converged, al-Khwārizmī was the man positioned to
        turn convergence into synthesis.

        The first stream was India. Brahmagupta's
        *Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta* (628) contained the decimal
        place-value system, zero, operations on negative numbers, and
        primitive forms of algebraic equations. This text reached
        Baghdad in the mid-eighth century when an Indian envoy
        presented it to Caliph al-Manṣūr; it was translated into
        Arabic under the name *Sind Hind*.

        The second stream was Sasanian Persian: astronomical tables
        (*zīj*es) accumulated over centuries, carrying the Babylonian
        observational legacy. Post-Sasanian Persian scholars rendered
        these tables into Arabic for the caliphate.

        The third was the Hellenistic world: Ptolemy's *Megístē*,
        Euclid's *Stoiḫeîa*, Diophantus's *Arithmetica*. These passed
        into Arabic at the start of the ninth century through the Bayt
        al-Ḥikma's translation programme.

        three sources together but *to discover the relations between
        them*: why the *six canonical equations* of algebra suffice;
        why the Indian numerals fit so naturally with Greek geometry;
        why astronomical reckoning could be written in the same

        had to answer.
