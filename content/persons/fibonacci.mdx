        The popular descent of the *Liber Abaci* runs, from the mid
        thirteenth century, through the *scuole d'abaco* (abacus schools)
        set up in the Tuscan cities — Florence, Siena, Pisa, Lucca,
        Pistoia — for merchants' sons. These schools taught not in Latin
        but in Tuscan vernacular; the master-arithmeticians (*maestri
        d'abaco*) adapted the long problem-section of the *Liber Abaci*

        Tuscan abacus manuscripts. By the 1320s Florence alone hosted

        reports in the 1330s that 1,000 to 1,200 students were attending
        them in the city. The modernisation of Europe's commercial hand
        ran *from below, not from above* — through the merchant quarters,
        not the universities — and the *Liber Abaci* is the key text of
        that lower channel.

      en: |
        Leonardo returns to Pisa around 1200 and completes the *Liber
        Abaci* a few years later (first edition 1202, revised second
        edition 1228 — dedicated to Michael Scot, in fifteen chapters;
        all modern editions descend from this line). The structure:
        introduction of the *novem figuras Indorum* together with zero;
        the rules of positional notation; the four operations; ratios
        and fractions; problems of commercial arithmetic (alloying,
        share-allocation, profit-and-loss, interest, insurance);
        algebraic methods. The book's distinction is not the knowledge
        itself — most of its elements were already reaching Europe by
        other channels — but the *organisation*: a model, in 600 pages,
        of how an arithmetic can be written as a textbook. Many
        civilisations can reach the raw knowledge of a technology; its
        propagation depends on its being organised into a teachable
        format. The *Liber Abaci* is the first text that succeeds at
        this for Latin Europe.
