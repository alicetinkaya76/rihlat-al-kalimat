      en: |
        The cornerstones of modern mathematical trigonometry — sine (*jayb*), cosine (*jayb al-tamām*), tangent (*ẓill*), secant (*quṭr al-ẓill*) — reached modern science as the tables calculated in the workshop of a Battan-born astronomer, in the observatory on the bank of the Euphrates at Raqqa, over the forty years between 877 and 918. Al-Battānī's masterpiece *Zīj al-Ṣābiʾ* (the Sabian Table) — the first great Islamic astronomical work to go beyond the Greek Ptolemy's *Almagest* — expressed the annual-seasonal movement of the stars with precise mathematical tables, in a form that is the ancestor of the modern radian-centred formulation. The book was translated into Latin by Plato of Tivoli in 1116 as *De motu stellarum*, and for seven centuries it was a European astronomical reference-work.

        Copernicus, in his 1543 *De revolutionibus*, cites al-Battānī twenty-three times; especially on the precise measurement of the durations of the solar year (the seven-hundred-and-fifty-year interval between al-Battānī's observations and Ptolemy's was a golden data-source for precision calculation), on the drift of planetary apsides, and on the asymmetry of the lengths of the seasons. Tycho Brahe and Kepler too read al-Battānī systematically. Modern historiography of science (Otto Neugebauer in 1957, Edward S. Kennedy's 1956 doctoral thesis, Régis Morelon's 1987 modern Arabic edition) positions al-Battānī as *the real starting point of modern mathematical astronomy*; under the foundation of the so-called "Latin European Renaissance after the Greek-Byzantine dark age", the tables of the Raqqa workshop lie. A concrete case of how the numerical data that came from the hand of one man could form the infrastructure of the Newtonian mechanics a thousand years later.

      en: |
        Plato of Tivoli (Plato dello Tivoli) — the Italian translator working at Toledo at the beginning of the twelfth century — translated al-Battānī's *Zīj al-Ṣābiʾ* from Arabic into Latin in 1116. The Latin title of the work is *De motu stellarum* (On the Motion of the Stars), the name chosen by Plato. The work became one of the principal textbooks of the European astronomical curriculum: in the thirteenth century, Sacrobosco's astronomical textbook *De Sphaera Mundi* uses data-tables sourced from al-Battānī; in the fourteenth century the *Almagestum Parvum* (the Little Almagest, the standard form of Arab astronomy in Latin Europe) takes Plato's translation as its base.

        The astronomical literature of the early Renaissance reads and cites al-Battānī systematically: Regiomontanus (1436-1476) at Nuremberg sought to update al-Battānī's table-data; Tycho Brahe (1546-1601) used al-Battānī as a calibration source in his star observations; Copernicus (1473-1543) cited him systematically as described above. An important detail in the word-history: Europe Latinised al-Battānī's name as *Albategnius* / *Albatenius*; this form was standardised in modern Latin astronomical literature until the seventeenth century. History pulled this name out of general use, but in the astronomical literature of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries the formula *Albategnius dixit* ("Albategnius said") works at the same layer of debate as the *Ptolemaeus dixit* formula of the Almagest.
