      en: |
        In the modern sociology of science the type that ibn ʿIrāq
        instantiates — the aristocratic patron, the productive
        scientist and the first-rate teacher in one — is rare.
        In the modern university lab these three roles are
        institutionalised as three different people: the dean or
        foundation that secures the funding (the patron); the
        postdoc or principal investigator running the daily
        experiments (the scientist); the faculty member who gives
        the lectures (the teacher). The combination of the three
        roles is seen in the modern period only under *very
        specific conditions* — Crick's role at Cambridge, say,
        or Feynman's at Caltech. Ibn ʿIrāq instantiates this role
        in its purest form in eleventh-century Khwārazm: the
        family inheritance supplied the observatory and the library
        (the patronage), he wrote the mathematics books (the
        scientist), and he raised the young al-Bīrūnī from the age
        of eighteen (the teacher). For the modern reader, ibn
        ʿIrāq's significance is this: the scientist's economic
        independence is the precondition of *creative freedom*.
        Independent of patrons rather than of one patron — the
        combination of aristocratic income and scientific curiosity
        — was made possible in the eleventh century.

        Werke* — a single-volume biographical catalogue of the

        with a list of twenty-five works in Arabic titles. In the

        list, brought ibn ʿIrāq's small treatises out one by one

        recognising ibn ʿIrāq — moving him out of the side-note
        status of *al-Bīrūnī's teacher* and treating him as a
        figure in his own right — only becomes possible in the
        1980s, with the revival of Arabic history of science by
        Roshdi Rashed and Régis Morelon's Paris-CNRS school.

      en: |
        Around the 990s ibn ʿIrāq began his own mathematical
        production at the court of Gurganj in Khwārazm — the capital
        of the Ma'munid dynasty, the northern successor to the
        Sāmānids. His main interest was the revision of the Arabic
        readings of the Greek mathematical heritage. The *al-Shakl
        al-Mughnī* (the Sufficient Figure) — ibn ʿIrāq's re-derived
        version of Menelaus's theorem on the spherical triangle —
        took spherical trigonometry one step forward. He revised
        the Arabic edition of Apollonius's *Conics*; he and Ibn
        al-Haytham (his contemporary, at Cairo) read the same
        source text in two different cities. The more than
        twenty-five small treatises ibn ʿIrāq wrote — on spherical
        astronomy, trigonometry, geometric problems — are the
        richest example we have of the lost eleventh-century
        Khwārazmian mathematicians. But his real legacy is his
        court practice: he made the court of Gurganj into the
        equivalent of a school for young men who wished to study
        mathematics — al-Bīrūnī, and others. The modern history
        of science names this school *the Khwārazmian mathematical
        tradition*; it was distinct from the "House of Wisdom"
        school of Baghdad, on the northern Iran-Transoxianan axis,
        and read the Greek mathematical sources not at Baghdad's
        mediation but directly.

      en: |
        In the 960s ibn ʿIrāq was, on his mother's side, of the
        Banū ʿIrāq princely dynasty of Khwārazm; on his father's
        side, of a smaller princely dynasty in what is now the
        Gilan region. In childhood he was raised as a prince:
        military training, dynastic politics, court etiquette. For
        a young man born aristocratic to study mathematics was, in
        the eleventh-century Islamic world, rare — aristocrats
        studied politics and the military arts; science was the
        business of students *of a class below the salaried class*.
        Ibn ʿIrāq broke this expectation: in his late twenties, while
        retaining the title of prince, he turned to mathematical
        production. His choice makes him, for the modern reader,
        a figure of the Renaissance kind: aristocratic but
        scientifically curious. His aristocratic capital financed
        his scientific production (he had his own observatory and
        library); yet that capital also limited his educational
        options — he was no spectator-scientist who had inherited
        from his father, but a self-taught autodidact. One of the
        conditions of his raising al-Bīrūnī was this: he knew how
        to transmit the type of the *self-taught* to the next
        generation.
