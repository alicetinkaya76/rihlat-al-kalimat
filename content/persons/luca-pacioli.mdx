      en: |
        In 1494 at Venice, Pacioli has the *Summa de Arithmetica,
        Geometria, Proportioni et Proportionalita* printed at the
        press of Paganino Paganini — six hundred pages, mostly in
        Tuscan vernacular Italian, with some Latin. This work
        has three distinct significances in the history of
        science. **First**, the book itself is the first printed
        comprehensive mathematics text — Europe's first
        *mathematical reference book*. Pacioli openly declares
        that this is not his own contribution: the *Summa* is the
        written compilation of the Tuscan abbaco school tradition
        (Fibonacci 1202 → Dell'Abbaco c. 1340 → Pacioli 1494),
        blended with Pythagoras, Euclid and Islamic algebra
        (indirectly from al-Khwārizmī). **Second**, chapter 11
        (the *Particularis de Computis et Scripturis*) is the
        first printed systematic exposition of modern double-entry
        accounting (twenty-seven pages). **Third**, chapter 12
        gives an exposition of the *regola della cosa* (algebra) —
        the last great summary of rhetorical algebra before the
        symbolisation of modern algebraic notation. The *Summa*'s
        impact may be summarised in a sentence: in writing the
        history of mathematics after 1494, the boundary between
        *before the Summa* and *after the Summa* is a meaningful
        historical line. The flow of mathematical knowledge in the
        print era begins with this book.
