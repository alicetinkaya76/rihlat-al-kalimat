        Modern akademik tarihçilik (Gabriel Ferrand 1921-28 modern Fransız basımları, G. R. Tibbetts 1971 İngilizce çevirisi *Arab Navigation in the Indian Ocean before the Coming of the Portuguese*) Ibn Mājid'i Hint Okyanusu Arap denizciliğinin altın çağının son büyük belgeleyicisi olarak konumlandırır. *Kitāb al-Fawāʾid fī uṣūl ʿilm al-baḥr wa-al-qawāʿid* (Deniz Biliminin Esasları ve Kurallarındaki Yararlar Kitabı, c. 1490) Arap denizci-pilot literatürünün (*rahnāmag* türü) en sistematik özetidir: yıldız-yön belirleme, mevsim-rüzgarı tabloları, kıyı-haritası kuralları, liman-girişi protokolleri, gemi-mühendisliği prensipleri.

        Sözcüğün modern akademiye eklediği bir tartışma: 1498 Vasco da Gama'nın Hint Okyanusu seferinin Malindi-Calicut son ayağı için Arap-pilot rehberliği geleneksel olarak Ibn Mājid'e atfedilir. Bu tartışma 16. yy Portekiz tarih yazarı João de Barros (1552) ve 18. yy Arap kaynak Quṭb al-Dīn al-Nahrāwālī'nin (1577 yazıt) belirsizliğine dayanır. Modern akademik tartışma (Ibrahim Khoury 1971, Vincent Cornell 1998) Portekiz pilot-belgelerinde *Cana / Canaqua* / *Malemo Cana* adının tek Ibn Mājid'i ve değil bir başka Arap pilotu (Sevahili Mancha) gösterdiğini savunur. Tarihsel kesinliği önemsiz: Ibn Mājid'in *Kitāb al-Fawāʾid*'i Hint Okyanusu Arap denizci geleneğinin teknolojik özetidir, ve Portekiz Indian Ocean ticareti bu geleneğin pratik mirasından doğrudan beslendi — Ibn Mājid kişisel rehberlik etmiş olsa da olmasa da.

      en: |
        Modern academic historiography (Gabriel Ferrand's 1921-28 modern French editions, G. R. Tibbetts's 1971 English translation *Arab Navigation in the Indian Ocean before the Coming of the Portuguese*) positions Ibn Mājid as the last great documenter of the golden age of Indian-Ocean Arab navigation. *Kitāb al-Fawāʾid fī uṣūl ʿilm al-baḥr wa-al-qawāʿid* (The Book of Useful Things in the Principles and Rules of the Sea-Science, c. 1490) is the most systematic summary of the Arab navigator-pilot literature (the *rahnāmag* genre): star-direction-finding, the seasonal-wind tables, the rules of coast-mapping, the protocols of port-entry, the principles of ship-engineering.

        A debate that the word adds to modern scholarship: the Arab-pilot guidance of the Malindi-Calicut final leg of Vasco da Gama's Indian-Ocean voyage of 1498 is traditionally attributed to Ibn Mājid. This debate rests on the ambiguity of the sixteenth-century Portuguese historian João de Barros (1552) and of the eighteenth-century Arab source Quṭb al-Dīn al-Nahrāwālī (the writing of 1577). Modern academic discussion (Ibrahim Khoury 1971, Vincent Cornell 1998) argues that the name *Cana / Canaqua* / *Malemo Cana* in the Portuguese pilot-documents points not necessarily to Ibn Mājid alone but to another Arab pilot (Swahili Mancha). The historical certainty does not matter: Ibn Mājid's *Kitāb al-Fawāʾid* is the technological summary of the Arab maritime tradition of the Indian Ocean, and Portuguese Indian-Ocean trade was directly fed from the practical inheritance of that tradition — whether or not Ibn Mājid personally provided the guidance.

        UNESCO has placed the Arab maritime tradition of the Indian Ocean on its protection list twice: "Pearl-diving in the Gulf" in 2011 and "Al-Khateera dhow building" in 2017. This recognition is the practical proof that the thousand-year-old navigator culture that Ibn Mājid documented finds its place as a category of modern world heritage.

      en: |
        April 1498: Vasco da Gama's Portuguese voyage (1497-99) arrived at the port of Malindi on the East African coast. The Portuguese-European crew, entering Indian-Ocean waters for the first time, needed the guidance of an Arab pilot who knew the waters, the winds and the star-map of the Indian Ocean for a safe passage to Calicut (on the Indian Malabar coast). The sultan of Malindi assigned to the European ship an Arab pilot — the man named, in the Portuguese documents, *Malemo Cana* / *Canaqua*. This pilot brought the Portuguese ship through the 27-30-day Malindi-Calicut passage on a safe route. On 20 May 1498 Vasco da Gama arrived at Calicut. The direct sea-trade link Europe-Indian Ocean was established.

        Who that pilot was is one of the longest debates of modern historiography. João de Barros's *Décadas da Ásia* (1552) names him *Cana / Canaqua / Malemo Cana*; this Latinised name comes directly from the Arabic *muʿallim* (teacher, pilot); but *Cana* is ambiguous — does it come from the initials of Ibn Mājid's Arabic name, or is it a distorted sound-form of another name like *Mancha* (Swahili-Arab *manjā*)? In the 1960s and '70s Arab scholarship (Ibrahim Khoury in 1971, Anwar Abdel-Malek in 1973), assuming that Ibn Mājid died around 1500 and so could still have been active at that age in 1498, supported the hypothesis. Later studies (Vincent Cornell in 1998, Sayyed Hossein Nasr in 2007) weakened the hypothesis by showing that the identification could not bear a documentary basis. History leaves the matter open; oral-practical tradition keeps it under the name of Ibn Mājid.

        The real content of this legend is this: the direct sea-trade between Europe and Asia took place through the practical-transfer of the technological accumulation of Arab-Indian-Ocean navigation. Vasco da Gama's ship reached Calicut not by its own knowledge but by the knowledge of the Indian-Ocean pilot. The modern European "discovery of the East" narrative (the heroic story of Henry the Navigator, Vasco da Gama, Magellan) tends to render this practical-transfer process invisible; the so-called "Europe discovered Asia" — when in fact Asia had been navigating its own waters for centuries. The figure of Ibn Mājid is a name for that transfer-point under the invisibility.

      en: |
        Ibn Mājid's practical navigator-pilot career began at the age of thirty (around 1462) — the training taken in his own father Mājid ibn Muḥammad's workshop; on the grandfather's side already a pilot-family (a three-generation pilot tradition). Over nearly thirty years of navigator-practice he systematically recorded the star-coordinates, seasonal-wind tables, port-entries and water-currents of each of these regions.

        *Kitāb al-Fawāʾid fī uṣūl ʿilm al-baḥr wa-al-qawāʿid* (The Book of Useful Things in the Principles of the Sea-Science, around 1490) is the systematic summary of this thirty years of practical knowledge. The book is divided into four parts: (i) *al-qawāʿid* (rules) — the mathematics of star-direction-finding, the seasonal-wind tables; (ii) *al-aqālim* (climates/regions) — the practical-geography of each coast of the Indian Ocean; (iii) *al-mawāqit* (timings) — the protocols of the right-time-to-voyage (the harmony with the monsoons); (iv) *al-ṭuruq* (routes) — specific route-descriptions. The book is not written in prose but in the *urjūza* style of the Arab navigator's tradition (a verse-format made to be memorised) — a mnemonic-organisation of the coastal-pilot's readings suited to their practical purpose.

        Ibn Mājid's other works: he wrote about forty short *urjūza*s (navigator-poems); some on practical port-knowledge (for example *Urjūza fī siyar al-shams*, The Poem of the Sun's Course), some on navigator-philosophy (for example *Urjūza fī siyar wa-al-fawāʾid*, The Poem of Navigation and the Useful Things). Modern studies of navigator-literature (Khoury 1971, Sezgin 2007) have catalogued all of Ibn Mājid's *urjūza*s and analysed them philologically. These studies place Ibn Mājid's navigator-writing practice as the most authoritative example of the integration of the Arab-Greek-Indian-Persian astronomical tradition into the practical-application of the Indian Ocean.

Erken örnekler: Sulaymān al-Mahrī'nin *Tuḥfat al-fuḥūl fī tamhīd al-uṣūl* (10. yy), Bāqir al-Yamānī'nin denizci tablolarının kitabı (12. yy), İbn Mājid'in büyük-amcası Sulaymān al-Mahrī'nin (farklı kişi, aynı ad) 15. yy başı *Mahmūdiyya*'sı.       en: |
        After the seventh-century Islamic conquest, Arab navigation established an eight-century dominion over the Indian Ocean (seventh-fifteenth centuries). This process emerged from the merging of the pre-Islamic Arab maritime tradition (Sabaʾ, Ḥimyar, Magan) with the economic-political infrastructure of the Islamic-Arab world. Seventh-ninth century: Basra-Sohar-Aden as the initial ports; ninth-eleventh century: the Baghdadi-Abbasid-centred trade's Indian-Ocean infrastructure (Sīrāf, Kīsh, Aden, Hormuz); eleventh-fifteenth century: the golden age of the spice-and-silk trade on the Egypt-Aden-Kerala-Quanzhou axis.

        Through these eight centuries the *rahnāmag* (coastal-pilot's book) literature developed. Early examples: Sulaymān al-Mahrī's *Tuḥfat al-fuḥūl fī tamhīd al-uṣūl* (tenth century), Bāqir al-Yamānī's navigator-tables book (twelfth century), Ibn Mājid's great-uncle Sulaymān al-Mahrī's (a different person, the same name) early-fifteenth-century *Maḥmūdiyya*. This literature joined Greek-Arab mathematical astronomy (the Aristotle-Ptolemy-Alexandrian-Baghdadi tradition) with practical navigator-pilot experience.

        Ibn Mājid's *Kitāb al-Fawāʾid* is the LAST GREAT synthesis of this eight-century tradition — *the last* because the European Indian-Ocean presence (Portuguese 1498, Dutch 1602, English 1600 East India Company) eroded the economic-political power of Arab navigation in seven or eight generations. The modern reader should read Ibn Mājid as *a man giving his work at the end of an Arab maritime golden age*: both the summit of the tradition and the last representative of the tradition. The late entry of the word into English Europe (around 1799, the British colonial period) reflects this: the Arab maritime tradition was no longer dominant by then; Ibn Mājid's work entered Europe as an academic museum-catalogue, not as a practical knowledge-infrastructure.
