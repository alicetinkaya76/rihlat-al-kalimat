    bilim tarihçisinin gözünde Râzî, Orta Çağ Arap-İslam dünyasının en

  en: |
    The nisba *al-Rāzī* ("of Rey") attaches him to the city of Rey, south
    of modern Tehran, in the province of Jibāl; Rey was one of the
    richest medical-philosophical centres of the post-Islamic Persian
    Sasanian inheritance. As a student, al-Rāzī first turned to music and
    alchemy, and only in his thirties to medicine — under the influence
    of the last great representative of the Persian medical tradition, Abū
    Zayd al-Balkhī. His medical training is unusual: he learns the Galenic
    medicine not from the classical texts but from the observation of the
    patients lying in the hospital-bed. His principal work, emerging from
    this practice, is the *Kitāb al-Ḥāwī* ("the All-Containing") — a
    clinical case-series in twenty-three volumes — which becomes the
    canonical foundational book of Arab-Islamic medicine. In character:
    religious scepticism (in *al-Makhāriq fī al-Anbiyāʾ* on the impossibility
    of prophecy by reason), opposition to Galenic dogma (in *al-Shukūk ʿalā
    Jālīnūs* — "Doubts about Galen"), clinical empiricism (the noting-down
    of cases). In the eye of the modern historian of science, al-Rāzī is
    the most "modern" scientist of the medieval Arab-Islamic world; the
    epistemic character of his thinking is not that of the nineteenth-century
    European physician but of the seventeenth-century Baconian medical-empiricism.

        Modern tıp tarihi er-Râzî'yi üç temel katkıyla anar: (i) *Kitāb al-Ḥāwī*'da kayıt altına alınan klinik vaka serileri — Avrupa'da bu pratik 17. yüzyıla kadar yeniden ortaya çıkmaz; Râzî hastane yatağı başında her vakanın notunu tutar, semptomları zaman içinde takip eder, etkili ve etkisiz tedavileri kayıt eder. (iii) *al-Shukūk ʿalā Jālīnūs* — Galenik tıbbî dogmaya akademik eleştiri; epistemik öneme sahiptir çünkü Galen'in 1500 yıllık otoritesinin sorgulanması ilk kez bir İslam dünyası hekimi tarafından yapılır. Vesalius'un (Padua, 1543) Galen'in anatomik hatalarına dair sistematik düzeltmesinden 600 yıl önce. Modern tıp tarihçisinin sıkça yaptığı söz: "Râzî, Vesalius'tan 600 yıl önce Vesalius gibi düşündü."
      en: |
        Modern medical history records al-Rāzī for three foundational contributions: (i) the clinical case-series put on record in the *Kitāb al-Ḥāwī* — a practice that does not re-emerge in Europe until the seventeenth century; al-Rāzī keeps the note of every case at the hospital bedside, tracks the symptoms over time, records the effective and the ineffective treatments. (ii) the *Kitāb fī al-Jadarī wa al-Ḥaṣba* ("On Smallpox and Measles") — the first text to lay out systematically the clinical distinction between two diseases (the onset of symptoms, the morphology of the rash, the prognosis); the epistemic ancestor of the nosology of modern infectious diseases. A medical classic with more than forty European editions between 1498 and 1866. (iii) *al-Shukūk ʿalā Jālīnūs* — an academic critique of Galenic medical dogma; this is of epistemic importance because the questioning of Galen's fifteen-hundred-year authority is undertaken, for the first time, by a physician of the Islamic world. Six hundred years before Vesalius's (Padua, 1543) systematic correction of Galen's anatomical errors. The remark the modern historian of medicine often makes: "al-Rāzī thought like Vesalius six hundred years before Vesalius."

      en: |
        Gerard of Cremona, around 1170 in Toledo, translates the *Kitāb al-Manṣūrī* into Latin under the name *Liber Almansoris*; in the same period the *Kitāb al-Ḥāwī* passes in pieces into Latin, and is then translated in its entirety as the *Continens* in 1279 by the order of Charles I of Anjou, king of Sicily, by Faraj ibn Salim (the Sicilian Jewish physician). The *Continens* in twenty-five volumes is the largest medical work translated into Latin in its period; it becomes the standard author of the curriculum at the medical faculties of Salerno-Montpellier-Paris-Padua-Oxford. Between 1486 and 1542 it goes through eighteen printed editions (according to modern bibliographic records). Al-Rāzī's *Kitāb fī al-Jadarī wa al-Ḥaṣba* (Smallpox-and-Measles), in a separate translation line (Gerard's translation around 1170, then the first Padua print in 1498), becomes the foundational book of European nosology of infectious diseases. In the Latin literature al-Rāzī is named *Rasis* or *Albubather*; "Rasis" is the Latinised form of *al-Rāzī*, "Albubather" the Latin distortion of the kunya *Abū Bakr*. Until the fourteenth century al-Rāzī, together with Ibn Sīnā, is one of the two principal Arab authors of European medical education. *The physician of a Persian city becomes the skeleton of the medical curriculum of Christian Europe.*

      en: |
        After 905 al-Rāzī returns to Rey and becomes chief physician of the Rey *bīmāristān* (the hospital of Rey). The systematic gathering of his clinical notes is done in these twenty years; after his death his students compile the notes into the thirty-three-section *Kitāb al-Ḥāwī*. The structure: each section ("*bāb*") is devoted to a disease; first the relevant passages from al-Rāzī's Galenic medical corpus (Galen, Oribasius, Aetius), then the views of the physicians of the Islamic world before him (translations of Hippocrates, the physicians of the Persian-Sasanian Gundēshāpūr school), then — the critical addition — al-Rāzī's own individual clinical case-notes. In the structure "I observed in a patient that... and he was healed by this treatment," it is the first great medical book in which the recorded case-series is kept as the example. The term known in modern infectious-disease literature, the "case series" — what al-Rāzī did in the Bīmāristān of Rey is exactly that. The epistemic meaning of the clinical method: the case is not subject to the authority-text; the case corrects the authority-text. *In the hospital of a Persian city, six or seven hundred years before Europe, the epistemology of Baconian empiricism begins to work in practice.*

      en: |
        Around 880 al-Rāzī moves to Baghdad, and there — with the support of the Abbasid court — becomes chief physician of the city's largest hospital, the Manṣūriyya *bīmāristān*. This twenty-five-year Baghdad period is the period of writing of the foundational works *Kitāb al-Manṣūrī* ("the Dedication to al-Manṣūr," dedicated to the Samanid governor al-Manṣūr ibn Isḥāq, a short medical compendium in ten books) and the *Kitāb fī al-Jadarī wa al-Ḥaṣba* ("Smallpox-and-Measles"). The Baghdad *bīmāristān* is the organisational model of ninth-and-tenth-century Islamic medical practice: separate wards (eye, internal, surgical, mental disease), men's and women's wards, a library, examination-based physician certification, an apothecary. This is the structure that the later European hospitals (the Salerno hospital, the Florence Santa Maria Nuova) take as their example. The contribution of al-Rāzī to medical training in Baghdad: bedside teaching of students at the hospital bed (the pre-form of the modern "clinical clerkship"); the use, as teaching material, of live cases rather than of canonical texts. *The Baghdad hospital is approximately a modern hospital, by the European hospital model of the 1900s. Al-Rāzī sets up the practical epistemology of the hospital.*

      en: |
        Al-Rāzī's path to medicine is unusual. In his early years — what the sources record as the years of his twenties — his principal interests are music and alchemy: a young alchemist who plays the lute, who knows the Persian song-tradition, and at the same time has been influenced by the Jabirian corpus. After his death the biographers (Ibn Juljul in the tenth century, Ibn Abī Uṣaybiʿa in the thirteenth) touch upon his alchemical past: that one or two of his eyes were damaged during chemical experiments (a variant of cataract, perhaps a steam-burn), and that this incident turned him to medicine. The story is in the form of a biographical myth which cannot be authenticated, but what it indicates is right: al-Rāzī's entry into medicine in his thirties, and with it his laboratory-experimental consciousness grounded in alchemical practice. The systematicity of the clinical case-records — the observation-note-tabulation method — is the transposition of the alchemist's distillation-procedure-notebook practice into the medical field. *The methodological template of a chemist becomes the epistemology of a physician.* As the modern historian of science puts it: "al-Rāzī is a clinician not only because he is a clinical physician, but because he is a former alchemist."
