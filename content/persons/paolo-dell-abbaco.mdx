        the European foundation of the modern accounting system.

      en: |
        From around 1340 until his death in 1374 — more than thirty
        years — Dell'Abbaco directed the principal abbaco school of
        Florence, near Santa Trinità. His *Trattato d'aritmetica*,
        written in Tuscan, was used as the school's textbook; more
        than a hundred manuscript copies survive today in libraries
        at Florence, Venice, Paris and the Vatican. Its structure:
        the writing of Hindu-Arabic numerals, the four basic
        operations, fractions, proportions, the *regola del tre*
        (the rule of three), profit-and-loss, compound interest,
        and tables for currency conversion (appendix). This last
        appendix is of particular importance: the Florentine
        florin, the Venetian ducat, the Genoese solidus, the
        Sicilian tarì, the Acre besant — practical conversion
        tables among the currencies of the fourteenth-century
        Mediterranean trade network. The Florentine merchant
        carried this book *in his pocket*. Boccaccio in a letter
        mentions Dell'Abbaco as a personal friend; the
        mathematical-temporal precision of the narrative scheme of
        the *Decameron* (10 × 10 × 10 = 100) may be the literary
        reflection of this abbaco-mind.

      en: |
        Dell'Abbaco was born around 1281 at Prato — northwest of
        Florence, in the central-western belt of Tuscany. This
        birth comes thirty years after the death of Fibonacci (c.
        1250); the *Liber Abaci* (1202) was by then an eighty-
        year-old book, the foundational reference of the Tuscan
        abbaco tradition. The young Paolo took his arithmetic
        education at Prato, then went on to Pisa — Fibonacci's
        own city, the birthplace of the abbaco tradition — for
        further training; this Pisa education brought the
        structure of his *Trattato d'aritmetica* close to
        Fibonacci's *Liber Abaci*. He moved from Pisa to Florence
        around 1320 and stayed there until his death in 1374. The
        epithet *Dell'Abbaco* (the abbaco-master) is the sign of
        his vocational choice: no aristocratic surname, no
        paternal trade — the *abbaco* itself became his name. This
        is an important type in the modern sociology of science:
        the scientist bound to a craft, who takes his identity
        from his teaching. Dell'Abbaco's being called
        "dell'Abbaco" is comparable to Galileo's later being
        called "Galileo": the vocational identity can become the
        scientist's first name.
