    yabancıdır."* Modern okuyucu için: dünyanın en büyük 11. yy
    bilim insanı kendi şehrinde *taşralı/dışlı* sayılmıştı. Bu
    sosyal işaret, onun Hint kültürüne dönük açıklığını —

  en: |
    *al-Bīrūnī* — "the one from the outskirts" — a *nisba* derived
    from the Arabic–Persian *bīrūn* "outside, edge." It marks his
    having been born in the *outskirts* of Kāth, the capital of
    Khwārazm, and the city-court population's having marked him out
    as an "outsider." He himself stresses this epithet in his works:
    *"The name Khwārazm gave me — *al-Bīrūnī* — is unpleasant on my
    tongue; but I am called by it, since the quarter of my birth is
    still a stranger at the court I have reached."* For the modern
    reader: the eleventh century's greatest scientist was counted a
    *provincial outsider* in his own town. This social marking, in
    psychological terms, prepared his openness towards Indian
    culture — his refusal to consider the foreign *foreign*.

      en: |
        Al-Bīrūnī's crossing into Latin Europe is, compared with
        al-Khwārizmī's, a century late and incomplete. Some pieces
        of the astronomical tables in *al-Qānūn al-Masʿūdī* came
        into circulation through Adelard of Bath's Latin
        mathematics-and-astronomy translation programme around
        1145; the sections of the *Tahqīq mā li'l-Hind* dealing
        with Indian mathematics are called on as an indirect source
        in the Toledo translations, but no full translation is
        produced. Modern Europe learns of al-Bīrūnī's existence
        only in the nineteenth century — with Eduard Sachau's
        Berlin edition of the *al-Āthār al-Bāqiya* in 1879. The
        reason for this *delayed reception* is geographical: he
        wrote at Ghazna; Ghazna is five thousand kilometres from
        Toledo by way of Baghdad. The Baghdad-Toledo channel
        served al-Khwārizmī (Baghdad → Sicily → Toledo); but the
        Ghazna-Baghdad channel closed in the thirteenth century,
        on account of the Saljuq-Qarakhanid conflicts and the
        Mongol invasion. Al-Bīrūnī's legacy reached Europe not
        through a single channel but — through the back-reading
        discovery of modern history of science — late. The fruit
        of that *late discovery*: an eleventh-century scientist
        speaks directly with a twenty-first-century reader.
