      en: |
        The English adjective *canonical* — "standard, authoritative, in the syllabus" — descends from *kānūn*, which descends from the Greek *kanōn* (a rod, a measuring stick); but its particular density in the languages of science and medicine settled because one book was taught for six centuries as *the canonical book*: *Kitāb al-Qānūn fī al-Ṭibb*. The Latin *Liber Canonis*, translated in 1187 by Gerard of Cremona, became the chief textbook in the medical faculties of Bologna, Padua, Montpellier, Paris and Salamanca from the thirteenth to the mid-seventeenth century. In medicine *canon* means "the base text presumed correct" because this book *was* that base text. A modern speaker mentioning the *canonical cut* of a film or the *canonical form* of a theory speaks — unawares — the language of the status that a five-book medical encyclopaedia written in Hamadan acquired in Europe. UNESCO commemorated Ibn Sīnā on the millennial anniversary of his birth in 1980, with stamps issued by Pakistan and Iran; Latin Europe stopped reading him in the 1650s, but the adjective derived from his title remains a backbone of academic speech.

        There is an interesting asymmetry: in modern Western medical historiography, from the nineteenth century onwards, the name *Avicenna* is processed as *the representative of the period between Galen and Vesalius* — a "bridge figure". This framing makes it difficult to read him *as a philosopher in his own right*; whereas Dimitri Gutas (in his theses of 1988 and 2014) repositions Ibn Sīnā as *the summit of post-Aristotelian Greek-Arabic philosophy* — a singular synthesiser within the neoplaton-Arabic tradition, not a bridge but a peak. This re-reading is slowly finding a place in twenty-first-century university curricula: at Princeton, since the 2010s, the *Islamic Philosophy* course begins directly from Ibn Sīnā's *Kitāb al-Shifāʾ*; the Sorbonne, Berlin, McGill in the same direction. That the adjective derived from his name is part of the backbone of academic speech is a residue; the reading of *the man himself* is a door newly opened.

      en: |
        In the twelfth-century Toledo translation workshop Gerard of Cremona (d. 1187) rendered *Kitāb al-Qānūn fī al-Ṭibb* into Latin as *Liber Canonis*; in the same workshop Dominicus Gundissalinus and Avendauth carried sections of *Kitāb al-Shifāʾ* into Latin under independent titles — *De Anima*, *Metaphysica*, *Sufficientia*. The effect of these translations on European intellectual life was enormous: in the thirteenth century *Avicennism* — the reading of Aristotle through Ibn Sīnā — became the common frame of the Parisian and Oxonian schoolmen. In Albertus Magnus's (1200-1280) lecture notes the formula "*Avicenna says*" appears with the same frequency as the well-known "*Philosophus dicit*" for Aristotle. On the medical side: *Liber Canonis* — five books, (I) general theory, (II) simple drugs, (III) diseases of the body organ by organ, (IV) general illnesses and wounds, (V) compound drugs — was the principal textbook of medicine at Bologna, Padua, Montpellier, Paris and Salamanca from the thirteenth century to the mid-seventeenth. It was printed at Padua in 1493; in 1593 its Arabic was printed in Rome by the Medici Oriental Press — the first Arabic book printed in Europe. Its time in the curriculum: roughly six centuries. A comparison: in the same period Galen, Hippocrates and Avicenna were read side by side; in the order of names that modern medical history writes, it is hard to see the one in the middle.

        The most mature product of these six centuries was the correction of the text itself: Andrea Alpago of Belluno's new Latin translation, finished in 1527 (printed posthumously), was the first serious attempt to revise the *Liber Canonis* text by direct comparison with the Arabic manuscripts — it revealed that the twelfth-century text contained thousands of errors. The Padua School (fifteenth-sixteenth centuries) emerged as the centre where Ibn Sīnā was read most rigorously: Pietro d'Abano's *Conciliator* (1310-1316) on the *Qānūn*, the commentaries of Gentile da Foligno and Cristoforo da Grado (the great Italian strata). The framework broke at three points succeeding one another within a century: Vesalius's 1543 *De humani corporis fabrica* exposed the anatomical errors in the *Qānūn* (most of them inherited from Galen); William Harvey's 1628 theory of the circulation of the blood broke the larger Galen-Avicenna synthesis; the disintegration of the Galen-Avicenna medical authority around 1650 in the new clinical medicine. In a hundred years *Liber Canonis* passed from the curriculum to the museum — not because it was wrong, but because the larger framework in which it lived was being displaced.

        Around 1015 Ibn Sīnā arrived at Hamadan; he became physician — then vizier — to the Buyid emir Shams ad-Dawla (r. 997-1021). This exceptional position — a man of science loaded down by court administrative duties of the first rank — is the central paradox of his biography. The writing of *Kitāb al-Qānūn* began in these years; when he was dismissed from the vizierate he was thrown into prison, escaped, and went on to Isfahan to ʿAlāʾ ad-Dawla; the five books of the *Qānūn* were written in pieces, often by a night's work, often between court duties. His student al-Jūzjānī tells the scene in his *Sīrat al-Shaykh*: an evening the Master would teach, then take wine, then dictate to al-Jūzjānī a section of the Canon — the morning might continue from there. *Kitāb al-Shifāʾ* (The Book of Healing) — in four parts, logic, natural science, mathematics, metaphysics — the broadest single-author philosophical encyclopaedia of medieval Islam — was completed in the Isfahan years. The writing reached such a pace that lost sections were rewritten later (on one journey the horses' load fell, and a portion of the books was lost; Ibn Sīnā went back and dictated them from memory). Philosophy and medicine were written in the same court, on the same night, in the same minutes; the modern reader's word "specialization" had no place in an eleventh-century court.

        The detail of the prison scene summarises this paradoxical life best: around 1023, after the death of Shams ad-Dawla, Ibn Sīnā was imprisoned for four months in the fortress of Fardajān (near Hamadan, by order of Tāj ad-Dawla) — as a political bargaining chip. In the fortress he wrote *Kitāb al-Hidāya* (The Guidance), *Kitāb al-Qūlanj* (on colic — on the pain he himself was experiencing), and two Sufi treatises in poetic form, one of them — *Risālat al-Ṭayr* (The Treatise of the Bird) — a foundational text of the history of Sufi mysticism. After the prison he escaped to Isfahan to ʿAlāʾ ad-Dawla (in disguise). His death came in 1037 in Hamadan: during a crisis of colic pain he applied the treatment he himself had described, but did not recover; al-Jūzjānī records the scene in the *Sīra*: "When the doctor could no longer heal himself, he accepted death; with his last breath he asked: *bury me with a single dirham and twelve silver pieces, my son read for me the *Metaphysics* of the Shifāʾ*." One of the largest intellectual lives in the medieval Islamic world ended at the age of fifty-seven with the failure of his own medical authority.

        In Ibn Sīnā's twenty-second year the Qarakhanids brought down the Samanid state (999-1005); the royal library at Bukhara was dispersed, his father died. The following thirteen years were the years of a wandering court physician: at Gurganj (Khwārazm), in the library of ʿAlī ibn Maʾmūn; at Gurgān, in the court of Qābūs ibn Wushmgīr (it was while there that the *Qānūn* received its note "begun in Gurgān in the 1010s"); at Rayy, in the physicianship of the Buyid Majd ad-Dawla. Each transition was a crisis: politics shifts, the court changes hands, the physician-vizier must turn to a new patron. A substantial part of works like *Kitāb al-Mabdaʾ wa-l-Maʿād* and *Kitāb al-Najāt* was either written or planned in these unsettled years. In the history of science the question of what happens when the "library-holding state" falls has one answer here: an encyclopaedia that goes on being written on the back of its author's horse. The eastern-Iranian counterpart, two centuries later, of Baghdad's *Bayt al-Ḥikma* was a court-science infrastructure dependent on the state; when the state fell, the man of science set out on the road.

        The court of Gurganj in these years was the second densest centre of science in the medieval Islamic world (after Baghdad): under the patronage of ʿAlī ibn Maʾmūn (1009-1017) and Maʾmūn ibn Maʾmūn (1017-1017), al-Bīrūnī, Abū Sahl al-Masīḥī, Abū al-Khayr al-Khammār and Ibn Sīnā worked at the same court-academy — these four polymaths are the circle that the modern history of science calls "the Khwārazm ring". Ibn Sīnā's correspondence with al-Bīrūnī took place years later, when the two were at different courts; their written dialogue on ten questions of Aristotelian philosophy (*Asʾila wa-Ajwiba*, *Questions and Answers*, before 1015) is a rare example of a polymath polemic in the modern history of philosophy. The Khwārazm ring was dispersed in 1017: Maḥmūd of Ghazna (r. 998-1030) killed Maʾmūn ibn Maʾmūn and plundered the court; Ibn Sīnā — together with the other three he had been summoned to Maḥmūd's court at Ghazna but did not wish to go — fled to Qābūs at Gurgān, in disguise. Maḥmūd's pressure raises the question of *the portable independence of a man of science*: in the eleventh-century Khwārazm-Khorāsān world the *choice of patron* was a part of historical personhood, not merely employment.

        Ibn Sīnā was born in 980 at Afshana, a village near Bukhara; his father ʿAbd Allāh was a Samanid official, the family probably Ismaili Shia. At ten he had memorized the Qurʾan and mastered Arabic literature; at twelve, logic and Euclidean geometry; at sixteen, the full Aristotle. In his *Sīrat al-Shaykh* — the scene he himself recalled — he writes that he had read Aristotle's *Metaphysics* forty times without understanding, and then bought, in the Bukhara market for three dirhams, al-Fārābī's (d. 950) short commentary *Aghrāḍ mā baʿd al-ṭabīʿa*, and the text "lit up" for him. At seventeen he succeeded in the treatment of the Samanid emir Nūḥ ibn Manṣūr; the physicianship of the court came, with it the full free access of the Samanid royal library. In his own words: *"By the time I was eighteen, I had already completed all the branches of knowledge."* That sentence is not a confirmation of a scientist's knowledge; it is an anecdote of what kind of knowledge-density was possible in the triangle of court, library and patronage of an age. The Bukhara library was burnt in a later fire — according to some sources deliberately — burned; the contemporary accusation was that the fire had been set to leave Ibn Sīnā's book-stock without rival (we do not know the truth of the accusation; but even the anecdote reveals the knowledge-density paranoia of a century).

        His early teachers represent three different disciplines: Abū ʿAbd Allāh al-Nātilī (a wandering man of science travelling from Baghdad to Bukhara) gave him his first lessons from Euclid's *Elements* and Ptolemy's *Almagest*, and soon enough saw that Ibn Sīnā could read them by himself; Ismāʿīl al-Zāhid (a local jurist) the principles of jurisprudence and of Islamic law; Abū Sahl al-Masīḥī (a Christian Syriac physician, later to be met again in the Khwārazm ring) the first lessons of Hippocratic-Galenic medical theory. This triple formation — Greek science + Islamic jurisprudence and theology + Syriac medical tradition — is a paradigmatic answer to the modern history of science's question of "how a man of science was trained in the medieval Islamic world": not monolingual and not single-channel, but **three-layered**. The four or five years spent with these teachers (roughly 992-997) coincide with pre-adolescence and early adolescence; when the Bukhara court physicianship began (997), Ibn Sīnā at the ages of seven to seventeen rested on this three-layered foundation.

        وُلِدَ ابنُ سينا سنةَ 980م في أَفشَنَة — قَريةٍ قُربَ بُخارى — وَأَبوه عبدُ اللهِ مُوَظَّفٌ سَامانيّ، وَالعائلةُ على الأَرجَحِ إسماعيليّةٌ شيعيّة. وَفي العاشِرةِ كان قد حَفِظَ القُرآنَ وَأَتقَنَ الأَدَبَ العَرَبيّ؛ وَفي الثانيةَ عَشرَةَ المَنطِقَ وَالهَندَسةَ الإقليديّة؛ وَفي السادسةَ عَشرَةَ أَرسطو كامِلاً. وَفي *سيرَةِ الشَّيخِ* — المَشهَدُ الذي يَستَرجِعُه هو نَفسُه — يَكتُبُ أنّه قَرأَ *ميتافيزيقا* أَرسطو أَربعينَ مَرّةً دونَ أَن يَفهَمَها، ثمَّ اشتَرى في سوقِ بُخارى بثَلاثةِ دَراهِمَ تَفسيرَ الفارابيِّ (المُتوفّى 950م) القَصيرَ *أَغراضُ ما بعدَ الطَّبيعة*، فـ"أَضاءَ" له النَّصّ. وَفي السابعةَ عَشرَةَ نَجَحَ في عِلاجِ الأَميرِ السَّامانيِّ نوحِ بنِ مَنصور؛ فَجاءَتْ طِبابةُ البَلاطِ، وَمعَها الوُصولُ الحُرُّ الكامِلُ إلى خِزانةِ القَصرِ المَلَكيّ. وَبكَلِماتِه نفسِها: *"حين بَلَغتُ الثامِنةَ عَشرَةَ كُنتُ قد أَتمَمتُ جميعَ فُروعِ المَعرِفة."* وَهذه الجُملةُ ليسَتْ تَوكيدًا لمَعرِفةِ عالِم؛ بل هي حِكايةٌ عن نَوعِ كَثافةِ المَعرِفةِ التي كانتْ مُمكِنةً في المُثَلَّثِ — البَلاط، الخِزانة، الرِّعاية — من عَصرٍ من العُصور. وَاحتَرَقَتْ خِزانةُ بُخارى لاحِقًا في حَريقٍ — بحَسبِ بَعضِ المَصادِرِ مُتَعَمَّدٍ — وَالاتِّهامُ الذي رَدَّدَه المُعاصِرونَ كانَ أنَّ النَّارَ أُضرِمَتْ لتَترُكَ مَخزونَ كُتُبِ ابنِ سينا بلا مُنازِع (وَلا نَعرِفُ صِدقَ الاتِّهام؛ لكنَّ الحِكايةَ وَحدَها تُظهِرُ بارانوياَ كَثافةِ المَعرِفةِ لِقَرنٍ من القُرون).

        تَجدُرُ الإشارةُ إلى أَنَّ بُخارى نَفسَها في القَرنِ العاشِرِ كانتْ مَركَزًا فِكريًّا من الدَّرَجةِ الأُولى: عاصِمةَ السُّلالةِ السَّامانيّةِ (819-999م)، حاضِنةَ تَجَدُّدِ الفارِسيّةِ الأَدَبيّةِ (الرودكيُّ ت. 941م، الفِردَوسيُّ تَلميذُ القَرنِ التّالي)، مَوطِنَ عُلَماءَ من أَمثالِ الفارابيِّ السَّامانيِّ (مَرّ ذِكرُه) وَأَبي الوَفاءِ البوزجانيِّ الفَلَكيِّ. الـ*مَدرَسةُ السامانيّة* — كَما يُسَمّيها مُؤَرِّخو العِلمِ الحَديثُ — أَنتَجَتْ في جيلِ ابنِ سينا نَفسِه أَيضًا أَبا نَصرٍ ابنَ عِراق (مُعَلِّمَ البيرونيِّ) وَالبيرونيَّ نَفسَه (973-1048م، صاحِبَ *تَحقيقِ ما لِلهِنْد* وَمُعاصِرَ ابنِ سينا الذي راسَلَه في مَسائلَ فَلسَفيّةٍ). فالنُّضجُ المُبَكِّرُ لابنِ سينا ليسَ مُعجِزةً فَردِيّةً بل ثَمَرةُ شَبَكةٍ ذِهنيّةٍ كَثيفةٍ — كانَ في أَرضِ بُخارى ما يَجعَلُ صَبيًّا في الثَّامِنةَ عَشرَةَ يَجِدُ المُعَلِّمَ وَالنَّصَّ وَالحامِيَ في خَطٍّ واحِد.
