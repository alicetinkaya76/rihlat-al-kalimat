    edilmiş bir kolektif imza. *Geber* Latin Avrupası'na bu kolektif imza
    olarak ulaşır; 13. yy'da Paul of Taranto'nun yazdığı *Summa Perfectionis*
    de aynı adı taşır ama tarihsel Câbir ile organik bağı yoktur — modern

  en: |
    The nisba *al-Azdī* attaches him to the Arab tribal stock of Yemenite
    Azd, but Jābir's historical identity is uncertain: some sources place
    him as Sabian (of Ḥarrān), others as Persian (of Ṭūs), others as a
    Shia-affiliated. The Jabirian corpus consists of some three thousand
    treatises recorded under Jābir's name; as the work of modern philology
    (Paul Kraus, 1942) has shown, this corpus is the work not of a single
    author but of various authors between 730 and 960 — a collective
    signature championed under one name. *Geber* reaches Latin Europe as
    this collective signature; the *Summa Perfectionis* written in the
    thirteenth century by Paul of Taranto bears the same name but has no
    organic connection with the historical Jābir — what the modern
    historian distinguishes as "Geber Latinus."

      tr: |
        Modern kimya tarihinde Câbir bin Hayyân — eğer tek bir tarihsel kişi olsaydı — \"Arap simyasının babası\" olarak adlandırılır: dört Aristoteles elementinin (toprak-su-hava-ateş) cıva-kükürt teorisi (metallerin iç bileşimi) çerçevesinde yeniden formüle edilmesi, terazi-sayısı (*ʿilm al-mīzān*) teorisinin geliştirilmesi, sülfat-sodyum-amonyak gibi inorganik kimyasalların ilk sistematik tanımları. Modern bilimsel tarihçinin söyledikleri farklıdır: Câbir adıyla yazılan korpus 3000 civarında risale içerir ki bu kadar üretkenlik tek bir hayatta mümkün değildir; korpus 8.-10. yy'ın çeşitli simyacı-yazarlarının eseridir, *Câbir* adı kolektif bir imza, bir ekolün ismi. Yine de — kim yazdığından bağımsız — bu korpus simya pratiğine üç temel katkı yapar: (i) deneysel yaklaşımın ilk sistematik betimi (sıvı destilasyonu, kalsinasyonu, kristallendirme); (ii) maddenin nicel teorisi (terazi-sayısı); (iii) inorganik kimyasallar için ilk Arapça-temelli isimlendirme sistemi. *Latin Geber* daha geç (1280 dolayları) ortaya çıkan ayrı bir korpustur — Paul of Taranto'nun yazdığı, mineral asit (HCl, H₂SO₄) keşfini erken belgeleyen, ama tarihsel Câbir'in elinden geçmemiş.
      en: |
        In the modern history of chemistry, Jābir ibn Ḥayyān — were he a single historical person — is named the "father of Arab alchemy": the reformulation of Aristotle's four-element theory (earth-water-air-fire) within the framework of a mercury-sulphur theory (the inner composition of metals), the development of the theory of the balance-number (*ʿilm al-mīzān*), the first systematic descriptions of inorganic chemicals such as sulphate, sodium, ammonia. What modern scientific history says is different: the corpus written under Jābir's name contains some three thousand treatises, an output not possible in any single lifetime; the corpus is the work of various alchemist-authors of the eighth-tenth centuries, *Jābir* the collective signature, the name of a school. Even so — regardless of authorship — this corpus makes three foundational contributions to alchemical practice: (i) the first systematic description of an experimental approach (the distillation, calcination, crystallisation of liquids); (ii) a quantitative theory of matter (the balance-number); (iii) the first Arabic-based nomenclature for inorganic chemicals. *Latin Geber* is a separate corpus emerging later (around 1280) — written by Paul of Taranto, early-documenting the discovery of mineral acids (HCl, H₂SO₄), but without having passed through the historical Jābir's hand.

      tr: |
        Câbir korpusunun parça-parça Latinceye geçişi 12. yy ortalarından başlar: Cremonalı Gerard ve Sevillalı Yuhanna'nın çevirileri kaynak adını *Geber* olarak Latinleştirir. Modern filoloji bu külliyatı *Pseudo-Geber* (Geber-Latinus) olarak ayırır, çünkü içerik Câbir'in 8.-9. yy bağlamında olamayacak buluşları (örneğin Mineral asit damıtımı — HNO₃, HCl — 13. yy Avrupa simya keşfi) içerir. Paradoks şudur: Latin Avrupası kendi simya keşfi olan mineral asitleri Câbir'in (yani Arap simyasının) hanesine kaydeder, sanki bu keşif 9. yy Bağdat simyacısının elinde yapılmış gibi. *Bir adın altında dört yüz yıl yazıldıkça orijinal-fail kaybolur, geriye sadece ad kalır.* Modern kimya tarihi 200 yıl boyunca (1500-1700) bu Latince Geber'i Câbir sanır; ayrım Paul Kraus'un 1942-43 *Jābir ibn Ḥayyān, contribution à l'histoire des idées scientifiques dans l'Islam* monografisinde yapılır. Filolojinin tarihi bilime hediye ettiği en büyük netleştirmelerden biri.
      en: |
        The piecemeal passage of the Jabirian corpus into Latin begins in the mid-twelfth century: the translations of Gerard of Cremona and John of Seville Latinise the source's name as *Geber*. The Latin Geber corpus proper emerges around 1280: the four foundational treatises written by Paul of Taranto (d. after 1310) — *Summa Perfectionis Magisterii* ("the Summary of the Perfection of the Art"), *De Investigatione Perfectionis*, *De Inventione Veritatis*, *Liber Fornacum* — are works under Jābir's name but without original Arabic counterparts. Modern philology distinguishes this corpus as *Pseudo-Geber* (Geber-Latinus), because the content contains discoveries impossible in Jābir's eighth-ninth century context (for example the distillation of mineral acids — HNO₃, HCl — a thirteenth-century European alchemical discovery). The paradox: Latin Europe registers its own alchemical discovery of the mineral acids on the ledger of Jābir (that is, of Arab alchemy), as if the discovery were made by the ninth-century Baghdad alchemist's hand. *When for four hundred years one writes under a name, the original-author is lost, only the name remains.* Modern history of chemistry, for two hundred years (1500-1700), takes this Latin Geber for Jābir; the distinction is drawn in Paul Kraus's 1942-43 monograph *Jābir ibn Ḥayyān, contribution à l'histoire des idées scientifiques dans l'Islam*. One of the great clarifications philology has given to the history of science.

      en: |
        The Jabirian corpus is classified into five principal groups: *al-Mawāzīn* (the balance-numbers, 144 treatises), *al-Miʾāt* ("the Hundreds," 112 treatises), *al-Sabʿīn* ("the Seventies," 70 treatises), *al-Khams Miʾa* ("the Five Hundreds," 500 treatises, lost), *al-Mukhtār al-Rasāʾil* ("the Selected Treatises"). In the 1942-43 monograph, by internal dating — the cross-references of the treatises, the development of the terminology they contain, the supposed astronomical dates — Paul Kraus shows that this classification has the structure not of a single author but of a school's corpus written over the ninth and tenth centuries. The heart of the corpus is the *mercury-sulphur theory*: the inner composition of metals is determined by the ratios of two primary principles, mercury (feminine, fluid) and sulphur (masculine, combustible); to turn a metal into gold is to adjust the ratio of these two principles. This theory does not contradict the classical Aristotelian four-element theory but interprets it at a deeper level; this is the theoretical frame learned by Latin alchemy (Albertus Magnus, Roger Bacon). And also *ʿilm al-mīzān* ("the science of the balance") — every substance carries a quantitative-qualitative "weight"; these weights are subject to mathematical calculation; the meta-physical ancestor of the stoichiometry of modern chemistry. *The Jabirian corpus gives a theoretical infrastructure to alchemical practice; the bridge between practice (the laboratory) and theory (the Pythagorean quantitative thinking).*
