  en: |
    The nisba-laqab *al-Ḥafīd* ("the grandson") is given to distinguish him
    from the fame of his grandfather Abū al-Walīd Muḥammad ibn Rushd al-Jaddī
    (d. 1126) — the Mālikī chief qadi of Córdoba, author of authoritative
    *fiqh*-texts within Mālikism; same name, same city, same profession, the
    last representative of a three-generation legal family. Ibn Rushd is a
    *faqīh* (jurist of the Mālikī *fiqh* school) by his father, a qadi by
    his grandfather, and himself makes the high point of Andalusi
    philosophy — on two sides of the indefinite boundary between *fiqh*
    and philosophy. The *Faṣl al-Maqāl* ("the Decisive Treatise"), in which
    he takes up the philosophy-and-revelation conflict, speaks the last
    word of the discussion of the relation of revelation to reason in the
    Andalusi Muslim intellectual world; his commentaries on the Aristotelian
    corpus at three different scales (the *muḫtaṣar* epitome, the *talkhīṣ*
    middle, the *tafsīr* long-commentary) build the template of the
    Aristotelianism of Latin Europe from the twelfth century to the
    seventeenth. *Aristotle comes to Latin Europe through Ibn Rushd's
    spectacles*; the epithet *the Commentator* summarises a historical fact.

        Modern history of philosophy places Ibn Rushd on two levels: (i) within Islamic philosophy, the high point of Andalusi Aristotelianism — after Ibn Bājja (Avempace) and Ibn Ṭufayl (Abubacer), the one whose reply *Tahāfut al-Tahāfut* ("the Incoherence of the Incoherence") to al-Ghazālī's *Tahāfut al-Falāsifa* ("the Incoherence of the Philosophers," c. 1095) speaks the philosophy-side last word of the philosopher-and-theologian debate; (ii) within the philosophy of Latin Europe, as "the Commentator" of Aristotle, the standard commentator of the Aristotelian corpus from the twelfth to the seventeenth century — for the thirteenth-century European intellectual (Aquinas, Albertus Magnus, Siger of Brabant, Dante in *Inferno* IV: "averroes che 'l gran comento feo," "Averroes who made the great commentary") he is the canonical reference. The condemnation of 219 theological theses issued by Étienne Tempier, the bishop of Paris, in 1277 — many of them with explicit reference to Ibn Rushd — fixes the technical expression of "Latin Averroism"; and modern epistemology inherits from this condemnation the so-called "double-truth" thesis. Modern Aristotle scholarship (Aubenque, Burnett) re-evaluates the lacunae and the contributions of Ibn Rushd's reading of the Greek Aristotle (which he read from Arabic translations). *The one who preserved philosophy in the Islamic world and transmitted it to Christian Europe.*

      en: |
        Within thirty years of Ibn Rushd's death (1198) — by the decade of intensive translation between 1220 and 1240 — the three-level commentary (*muḫtaṣar*, *talkhīṣ*, *tafsīr*) on the Aristotelian corpus is translated into Latin by Michael Scot (the Scotsman-and-Jew working at Toledo, 1217-1235) and afterwards by Hermann the German (Toledo, 1240-1256). By 1240 these translations reach the universities of Paris and Bologna. The Aristotle of Latin European philosophy is now read no longer directly from the Greek but in the form Latinised-via-Ibn-Rushd's-Arabic-commentary; the technical language of Latin academic philosophy (potentia, actus, intellectus possibilis, materia prima — the Aristotelian-Averroist category in Latin) is the Latinisation of Ibn Rushd's Arabic equivalents (*quwwa*, *fiʿl*, *al-ʿaql al-hayūlānī*, *al-mādda al-ūlā*). "Latin Averroism" — Siger of Brabant (Paris, c. 1270), Boethius of Dacia, the philosophers of Padua of the fourteenth-sixteenth century (Pietro d'Abano, Pomponazzi) — inherits the thesis that "the intellect is one and shared" from Ibn Rushd.         Ibn Rushd's philosophical ground is the three-generation line of Andalusi Aristotelianism: (i) **Ibn Bājja** / Avempace (d. 1138, Saragossa-Fez) — the first great Aristotelian in al-Andalus, the *Tadbīr al-Mutawaḥḥid* ("the Governance of the Solitary") the first Andalusi work of politico-philosophical thought. The importance of Andalusi Aristotelianism: the Eastern Islamic philosophy (Ibn Sīnā, al-Ghazālī, al-Fārābī) was an Aristotelianism inwoven with Neoplatonist foundations; the Andalusi line is the project of a return to the classical Aristotle, cleared of the Neoplatonist layers. This "pure Aristotle" is precisely what Latin Europe wishes to receive in the thirteenth century. *The last heir of a tradition is the one who has filtered the previous steps of the tradition and offers its latest, ripened form.* Ibn Rushd, through his commentaries on Aristotle, hands the Andalusi line over to Latin philosophy; the line becomes the Aristotle of European Scholasticism. *The end of one tradition is the beginning of another.*
