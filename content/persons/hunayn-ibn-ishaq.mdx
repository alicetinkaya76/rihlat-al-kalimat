        Roughly thirty percent of Galen's surviving Greek corpus is known today only by back-translation from Ḥunayn's Arabic versions; the Greek originals are lost. *That is to say: a present-day historian writing on Galen still reads, in part, translations from Ḥunayn ibn Isḥāq.* This particular paradox is a quiet support-beam of the history of medicine: Galen → Ḥunayn (Arabic translation) → Constantinus Africanus (Latin re-translation) → 21st-century editor (re-translation into modern language). The modern-readable form of a Greek-originated text was, for these works, built up by back-translation through Baghdad. Ḥunayn's name does not appear in modern medical curricula (Hippocrates, Galen do); but the passage of Hippocrates and Galen from late-antique Greek to modern language ran, largely, *across his desk*. The invisibility of the translator is a rule in the history of science: the one who lays down the term leaves a trace, the one who transmits the term disappears.

Çevirilerinin önemli bir bölümü Huneyn'in Arapça eserlerinden Latinceye taşımadır: Galen'in *Ars Parva* özeti (*Isagoge Johannitii*) Avrupa tıp eğitiminin ana giriş metni oldu.       en: |
        At the end of the eleventh century Constantinus Africanus (d. 1087) crossed from Tunisia to the monastery of Monte Cassino and began the project of translating the Arabic medical corpus into Latin. A substantial part of his translations are renderings of Ḥunayn's Arabic works into Latin: the *Isagoge Johannitii*, Ḥunayn's epitome of Galen's *Ars Parva*, became the principal introductory text of European medical education. This text was the first work read at Bologna and Salerno, later at Paris and Oxford, at the head of the *Articella* anthology — from the twelfth century to the fourteenth. The first book of European medical education was, that is, *the Latin translation of an Arabic epitome of a Greek text*. Three layers of mediation, three signatures, at the head of all teaching. The medical school of Salerno (tenth to thirteenth century) was the single densest institution through which Arabic medical literature crossed into Latin by this channel; the *Articella*'s adoption as the standard in European universities runs from 1250 to 1450.

Onun çevresinde — oğlu İshak ibn Huneyn, yeğeni Hubeyş ibn al-Hasan, talebesi İsa ibn Yahya — bir aile-okulu kuruldu; Galen'in Arapça'ya geçen yüz yirmiye yakın tıp risalesinin önemli bir bölümünü bu çekirdek ekip çevirdi. Huneyn'in 856 dolaylarında patronlarından Ali ibn Yahya'ya yazdığı *Risāla fī mā tarjamahu min kutub Jālīnūs* (Galen'den çevirdiklerim üzerine risale) bilim tarihinin elinde olağanüstü bir belge: Galen'in 129 risalesini tek tek listeler, hangi Yunan elyazmalarını topladığını, bunların ne kadarının önceden Arapça çevirisinin olduğunu, kendi yöntemini Süryanice yardımcı çevrimi nasıl kullandığını anlatır. Bilim tarihçisi Gerrit Bos bu metni *Antik geç dönem ve İslam Ortaçağı çağında bilimsel çeviri pratiği üzerine en zengin tek kaynak* olarak değerlendirir. Çevirinin teorisi ile pratiği aynı el yazısında.
      en: |
        After the death of caliph al-Ma'mūn (d. 833) and through the reign of al-Mutawakkil (d. 861), Ḥunayn rose to the position of chief director of translation activity at the Bayt al-Ḥikma. Around him — his son Isḥāq ibn Ḥunayn, his nephew Ḥubaysh ibn al-Ḥasan, his student ʿĪsā ibn Yaḥyā — a family-school took shape; this core team translated a substantial portion of the roughly 120 medical treatises of Galen that crossed into Arabic. Ḥunayn's letter of around 856 to his patron ʿAlī ibn Yaḥyā, *Risāla fī mā tarjamahu min kutub Jālīnūs* (Epistle on what he has translated of Galen's books), is, in the hands of the history of science, an extraordinary document: it lists Galen's 129 treatises one by one, says which Greek manuscripts he had collected for each, says how much of each had already been translated into Arabic, and explains his own method including the use of intermediary Syriac drafts. The historian of science Gerrit Bos describes this text as *the single richest source on the practice of scientific translation in late antiquity and the Islamic Middle Ages*. The theory and the practice of translation in one hand.

        To find a Greek text to translate in Baghdad was not simple: ninth-century Byzantium itself preserved Greek classical manuscripts in limited number, most of them scattered through monastic libraries. Ḥunayn travelled repeatedly to Syria, Palestine, Egypt and Byzantium; under al-Mutawakkil's court protocol he opened channels with successive Byzantine emperors. A scholar travelling two or three years to find a single copy of some Galenic treatise — an early-medieval version of what the modern reader knows as "research travel".       en: |
        Ḥunayn was born in 809 at Hireh — close to the last Sasanian frontier, an old Christian Arab town. His mother tongue was Syriac; his family belonged to Hireh's Christian Arab *ʿIbād* stratum, in the apothecary's trade. His early education came through Syriac medical texts; at eleven or twelve he travelled to Baghdad to the side of the eminent physician Yūḥannā ibn Māsawayh — the known anecdote is that he asked too many questions in class and was sent away. Ḥunayn went to Alexandria, or to Gundēshāpūr (the last centre of the Sasanian medical academy tradition), and built his Greek there; he returned two or three years later, made his peace with his teacher, and entered the Bayt al-Ḥikma. He arrived in Baghdad, that is, in command of three languages — Syriac, Arabic, Greek — and would spend the next forty years building that command into a methodical practice. The childhood *expulsion* turned out to be the cause that founded the theory-practice institution.
