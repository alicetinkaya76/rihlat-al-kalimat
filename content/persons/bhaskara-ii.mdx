      en: |
        Around 1150 at the Ujjain observatory — successor to the
        observatory where Brahmagupta had worked — Bhāskara II
        finishes the *Siddhāntaśiromaṇi* ("Crown of Science"):
        1,450 verses in four chapters — *Līlāvatī* (arithmetic,
        277 verses), *Bījagaṇita* (algebra, 213 verses),
        *Grahagaṇita* (planetary motions), and *Golādhyāya*
        (spherical astronomy). The definition of division by zero
        he gives in the *Bījagaṇita* is a significant turning
        point in the modern history of mathematics: *"When a
        quantity is divided by zero, the quantity that results is
        called* khahara *(infinity). Its property is that any
        addition or subtraction to it does not change it — just as*
        para-brahman *(the Absolute) does not change across the
        ages of creation and destruction."* Brahmagupta had left
        division by zero *indeterminate* in 628; five hundred years
        later Bhāskara II gives it a name and philosophical status:
        khahara, "the zero-denominator." Modern analysis's symbol
        ∞ was found by John Wallis in the seventeenth century — but
        the concept was named in twelfth-century Indian mathematics.
        Bhāskara II's choice of metaphor is striking
        (para-brahman): mathematical infinity is set beside
        theological absolute — *modern philosophy of mathematics
        will take seven centuries (until Cantor) to re-find the
        comparison*.

        *Līlāvatī* (the playful/the beautiful) — the arithmetic book

        traditional account, Bhāskara astronomically calculated the

        at the exact hour of the wedding ceremony, Līlāvatī's
        hairpin fell and blocked the opening of the hourglass,
        the marriage-hour was missed, and the girl could not marry.

        within a poetic story* — is the foundation stone of modern
        Indian mathematical pedagogy. The problems are presented
        in a weave of "bird, flower, bee, elephant"; algebra
        becomes an art of storytelling. One of *Līlāvatī*'s famous
        problems: *"Five-ninths of a swarm of bees alighted on the
        lotus, a third on the kaṇḍila, and the one remaining bee
        flew between the jasmine and the lotus — tell me the total
        number of the swarm."* The answer: fifteen. The prehistory
        of the modern algebraic equation: the *riddle-arithmetic*
        Bhāskara wrote for his daughter.

      en: |
        Bhāskara II was born in 1114 at Vijjaḍaviḍa — a small town
        in the modern Bīdar district of Karnataka. His father,
        Maheśvara, was also an astronomer-mathematician; Vijjaḍaviḍa
        a centre of the Brahmin scholarly tradition. Bhāskara took
        his mathematical education from his father — typical
        biography of the twelfth-century Indian scientific
        families: knowledge passes from father to son, the
        pen-craft tradition runs for three generations. His son
        Lakṣmīdhara also grew up as a mathematician; his grandson
        Cāngadeva wrote a commentary on his grandfather's works
        (1207). This *three-generation scientific-family* motif
        is rare in the modern sociology of science — the modern
        academy in its *institutional* form breaks the family
        chain; whereas in twelfth-century Ujjain the directorship
        of the observatory could be left in the family. The young
        Bhāskara moved to Ujjain after his father's death — he was
        *appointed* to the directorship, an ancestral form of the
        modern university chair. Before that appointment, at
        Vijjaḍaviḍa, in the period when he was still learning at
        his father's side, he wrote his first essays in
        poetry-mathematics; the style of the *Līlāvatī* (algebra
        as a riddle) ripened in this early Vijjaḍaviḍa period.
