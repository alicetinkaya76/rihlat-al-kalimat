      en: |
        The single model laid down in Ibn al-Haytham's *Kitāb al-Manāẓir* (Book of Optics, c. 1028) — that light comes to the eye by *reflection* from objects, not by emanation from the eye — is the starting-point of modern optical science. His experimental refutation of the two-thousand-year-old "extramission" theory of Aristotle and Euclid was supported by *camera obscura* experiments: the measurement of how light through a small aperture produces an inverted image on the opposite wall. A thousand years later, every digital camera, every mobile-phone lens, every telescope is a variant of that dark room — the sensor receives an inverted image; software flips it. When UNESCO declared 2015 the International Year of Light, the figure selected from the Muslim world's scientific past was Ibn al-Haytham — *a thousand years of optics*. The disciplinary name *optics* (French *optique*, German *Optik*, Turkish *optik*) is etymologically from the Greek *optikē*, but the text by which the discipline as we know it was constituted was written in Arabic.

      en: |
        In 1572 Friedrich Risner published in Basel a Latin volume in which Ibn al-Haytham's *Kitāb al-Manāẓir* was printed alongside Witelo's *Perspectiva*: *Opticae Thesaurus*. The edition became a scientific-revolutionary instrument in the hands of Johannes Kepler (1571-1630). Kepler's 1604 *Ad Vitellionem Paralipomena* is already, on its title page, an extension of Ibn al-Haytham and Witelo; Kepler identified the retina as image-receiver and brought Ibn al-Haytham's *intromission* model into its modern mathematical form. The 1611 *Dioptrice* established lens theory — the theoretical foundation of Galileo's telescope. *Nothing was new* in this work: it was the final discussed step of Ibn al-Haytham's six-hundred-year-old Arabic text. European optics had been building on him throughout, sometimes knowing, sometimes not.

      en: |
        Around the beginning of the thirteenth century — likely in the orbit of the Toledo translation school, the translator unidentified — *Kitāb al-Manāẓir* was rendered into Latin as *De aspectibus* (or *Perspectiva*). Three principal Latin disseminators followed: Roger Bacon (*Opus Maius*, 1267, books IV-V), John Pecham (*Perspectiva communis*, c. 1280), and above all the Polish Witelo (*Perspectiva*, c. 1270). These three works constituted the optical curriculum of European universities for the next three hundred years — *perspectiva* was a pre-doctoral subject at Padua, Bologna, Oxford. Manuscripts of *De aspectibus* in the late Middle Ages run into the hundreds; the modern editor David Lindberg completed a two-volume critical edition in 1996. For these seven hundred years Europe knew the optician's name as *Alhazen* — like al-Khwārizmī, the man forgotten, the work remaining.

      en: |
        Summoned to the court of the Fatimid caliph al-Ḥākim bi-Amr Allāh (r. 996-1021), al-Ḥasan ibn al-Haytham promised a scheme for the control of the Nile flood; but on going down to Aswan he saw that the plan was infeasible at that level of technology. To shield himself from the caliph's wrath he withdrew — by genuine or feigned madness — and was kept in Cairo under what amounted to house arrest for some ten years until the caliph's death (1021). It was during this enforced solitude that he wrote the seven books of *Kitāb al-Manāẓir* — the single deepest medieval Arab-Islamic text on optics. The same period produced *Maqāla fī al-Marāyā al-Muḥriqa* (On Burning Mirrors), *al-Daw'* (On Light), and dozens of mathematical treatises. The count of works attributed to him exceeds ninety; some seventy survive. In the historian of science A. I.       en: |
        Ibn al-Haytham was born in Basra around 965, in the post-Abbasid Iraq. His early education is likely to have included jurisprudence and theology; the biographical sources say he found religious synthesis unsatisfying and turned to the natural sciences and mathematics. He is known to have worked, in his youth, on engineering tasks in and around Basra — hydraulic projects, surveying. He read Aristotle, Euclid, Ptolemy and Galen in Arabic translation (the Greek-into-Arabic movement had peaked two centuries before). His summons to Egypt before 1010 was attributed to this engineering reputation; the Nile-control promise had its source here. That is to say: the actual condition that produced *Kitāb al-Manāẓir* was not a natural-philosophical curiosity about optics (textbooks on optics had been written before); it was the leaving of an engineer alone with the deepest questions of philosophy and mathematics by enforced stillness. The paradox in which the history of science could take form: an enforced solitude.
