Since Andalusi Islamic-agriculture is the technological foundation of the agricultural-infrastructure of modern Spain, terms like fanega are concrete indicators of the "agricultural-technology-inheritance". The word's passage to Latin America was with sixteenth-century Spanish colonial expansion: in the lands conquered by Cortés and Pizarro, together with Andalusi peasant-practice, the fanega-measurement-system was also transferred. The great majority of the modern Spanish-speaking agricultural world still uses traditional measurement-units like *fanega* instead of or alongside the decimal-system (litres-metres).

      en: |
        During the Reconquista (the Christian Spain re-conquest of Muslim Spain in the eleventh-fifteenth centuries), the Muslim agricultural-practice in the conquered Andalusi lands was largely continued as it was — the new-Christian administration, in order to govern without disrupting agricultural-productivity, preserved the practical knowledge and standardised measurement-systems of the Mudéjar (the remaining Muslim) peasantry. The word *fanega* is the philological-signature of this process: the Arabic *al-fanīqa* (with the article dropped) settled as Spanish *fanega*, integrated into the official measurement-vocabulary of the Castile-Aragon kingdoms.

        The 1252 document *Cuentas de Sevilla* of Alfonso X the Wise (the Seville-market accounts of Alfonso X) is the first standardisation of *fanega* as an official measure in Christian-Latin written records. Throughout the fourteenth and fifteenth century in the Castile practical-market, 1 fanega = 12 *celemines* (another Arab-origin measure), 1 celemín = 4 *cuartillos* — the mathematical-integrity of the Andalusi grain-market infrastructure was preserved in the Christian economy. The imperial-edict of Philip II in 1568 legalised the *fanega castellana* (the Castile-fanega) as the official measurement-standard of the Spanish Empire.

        The passage of the word into English in the late sixteenth-seventeenth century, through England-Spain commercial contact: in 1604 English merchant-documents use the word *fanega* for grain-quantities imported from Spain. In modern English *fanega* lives as a Spanish-specific measurement-unit, but in more limited use — as the international agricultural-market passed to the decimal metric-system, fanega became marginal in English.

Bu dönüşümde sulama-altyapısı (*aljibe*, *acequia* — Arap-orijinli su-yönetimi terimleri), yeni-ürünler (pirinç, pamuk, şeker-kamışı, narenciye), pazar-organizasyonu (*sūq*, *almojarife* — Arap-orijinli pazar-yönetimi terimleri), ve standartlaştırılmış ölçü-sistemi entegre bir teknolojik paket'i oluşturdu. *Fanīqa* (kuru-hububat hacim ölçüsü, 1 fanīqa ~ 55-60 litre) bu paketin pazar-standart-bileşeni.

      en: |
        The Andalusi Islamic world (eighth-thirteenth century) was the centre of the technical agricultural transformation called "the Islamic Green Revolution" (Andrew Watson, 1983) in modern comparative-history literature. In this transformation, irrigation-infrastructure (*aljibe*, *acequia* — Arab-origin water-management terms), new-crops (rice, cotton, sugar-cane, citrus), market-organisation (*sūq*, *almojarife* — Arab-origin market-administration terms), and a standardised measurement-system formed an integrated technological package. *Fanīqa* (a dry-grain volume measure, 1 fanīqa ~ 55-60 litres) is the market-standard-component of this package.

        In the Cordoba Caliphate of the ninth-tenth century (under Abd al-Rahman III and Caliph al-Ḥakam II), the official-legal standard of the word *fanīqa* was set annually by the *qāḍī al-qaḍāʾ* (the chief judge); market-inspectors (*muḥtasib*, the hisbah-officers) audited measurement-accuracy in each market using stamped bronze- or copper standard-sample-vessels carrying the fanīqa-measure. *Ibn ʿAbdūn's Seville-hisbah-manual* (twelfth century) is a detailed documentation of the *fanīqa*-market regulation — a reference-work of modern comparative-Islamic economic-history literature.

        The economic-meaning of the word: the smallest linguistic-signature of the most concrete agricultural-inheritance Andalusia left to modern Europe (the irrigation-practice, the grain-crops, the standard-market-infrastructure). The *fanīqa*-measurement-vessels used in twelfth-century Cordoba-Granada-Seville markets can still be found in modern archaeological-finds (the Seville Museum of Arts, the Granada Alhambra Museum); the concrete material-inheritance of Islamic-agriculture practice. The modern word *fanega* is the global linguistic-tail of this practical-inheritance.

        Pers *p-* sesi Arap-fonolojisinde mevcut değildir (Arap alfabesinde *p* harfi yoktur); Pers *p-* sözcükleri Arapçaya geçtiğinde sistematik olarak *f-* ile değiştirilir (alfabet-mismatch örnekleri: Pers *pīl* → Arap *fīl* "fil", Pers *parsīkh* → Arap *fārsī* "Farsça", Pers *parīq* → Arap *fārīq* "ayrılan"). Bu *p- > f-* dönüşümü 7.-8. yy Arap-Pers leksikolojik teması'nın standart-fonolojik patternıdır; *panīqa* → *fanīqa* dönüşümü bu pattern'in tarım-leksikolojisindeki örnek-vakası.

        Pers-Arap leksikolojik teması 8.-12. yy boyunca İslam dünyasının teknolojik altyapısının dil-bilimsel iskeletini oluşturdu. Tarım-pratiği (fanīqa), su-yönetimi (qanāt, kāriz), maden-üretimi (būraq), astronomi (zīj), tıbb (bīmāristān) sözlüklerinin hepsinde Pers kökenli sözcükler Arap fonolojik adapte ile İslam-Arap teknik standartlarına entegre oldu. Modern *fanega* sözcüğü bu uzun-vadeli Pers-Arap entegrasyon-pratiğin Endülüs-İspanya kanalı yoluyla modern dünyaya ulaşan canlı bir dilbilimsel-fosilidir.
      en: |
        The Arabic word *fanīqa* (فَنيقة) was borrowed from the Persian word *panīqa* (پنیقه, the storage-vessel, the grain-keeping-pot) into Arabic from the eighth-ninth century onwards. Sasanian Persian (224-651) agricultural-practice had been using standardised grain-storage-vessels since antiquity; the standard-volume of these storage-pots (around 55-60 litres) had turned into a practical market-measurement-unit. After the Arab conquest (637-642 Syria-Egypt, 644-650 Persia), the Persian agricultural-practice became the technical foundation of the Arab-Islamic agricultural-infrastructure; the word *panīqa* was phonologically adapted to the Arabic form *fanīqa*.

        The Persian *p-* sound does not exist in Arabic-phonology (there is no *p* letter in the Arabic alphabet); when Persian *p-* words pass into Arabic they are systematically replaced with *f-* (alphabet-mismatch instances: Persian *pīl* → Arabic *fīl* "elephant", Persian *parsīkh* → Arabic *fārsī* "Persian", Persian *parīq* → Arabic *fārīq* "separating"). This *p- > f-* transformation is the standard-phonological pattern of seventh-eighth-century Arab-Persian lexical contact; the *panīqa* → *fanīqa* transformation is the case-instance of this pattern in agricultural-lexicology.

        The Persian-Arab lexical contact, throughout the eighth-twelfth century, formed the linguistic-skeleton of the technological infrastructure of the Islamic world. In all the vocabularies of agricultural-practice (fanīqa), water-management (qanāt, kāriz), mine-production (būraq), astronomy (zīj), medicine (bīmāristān), Persian-origin words were integrated into the Arab-Islamic technical standards by Arabic phonological adaptation. The modern word *fanega* is a living linguistic-fossil of this long-term Perso-Arab integration-practice, reaching the modern world through the Andalusi-Spanish channel.

      en: |
        In each of the ancient Near-East (Mesopotamia, Egypt, Persia) agricultures, grain-measurement-units had been standardised — as the basic infrastructure of annual-tax-collection, the state's food-reserve-management, market-trade. In the Sumerian-Akkadian vocabulary, *gur* (about 300 litres, a large-measure) and *ban* (10 litres, a small-measure) were the standard grain-measures (3000-2000 BC); in Egypt, *ḥeqat* (about 4.8 litres, a small-measure) and *khar* (about 96 litres, a large-measure); in the Sasanian Persian world, *artaba* (50-65 litres, a medium-measure) — the technical-standard vocabulary of the agricultural-economy of the ancient world.

        The Sasanian Persian *panīqa* (55-60 litres) is the final standard-form of this ancient tradition; ancient Persian agricultural-practice was developed particularly in the directions of grain-production, storage-management and market-standardisation. The ancient Greco-Roman world observed and largely accepted this Persian agricultural-practice; Herodotus's *Historia* (440 BC) describes in detail the technical-infrastructure of the annual-tax-organisation of the Persian Achaemenid Empire.

        The historical flow: ancient Mesopotamian-Egyptian-Persian agricultural-measurement-standards → the early Islamic-Arab agricultural-practice's Persian-phase inheritance (seventh-eighth century) → the integration into Andalusi Islamic-agricultural practice (tenth-twelfth century) → the post-Reconquista Christian-Spanish inheritance (thirteenth-fifteenth century) → the passage to Latin America with Spanish colonial expansion (sixteenth-eighteenth century) → the use continuing in modern Latin America and Spanish agricultural-practice (nineteenth century to the present). Beneath the modern word *fanega* lies the final linguistic-signature of the five-thousand-year ancient agricultural-measurement-practice tradition. The long-term philological-fossil, in world-agricultural-history, of a single Persian storage-vessel word.
