      en: |
        Around 1145 Gerard of Cremona translated al-Battānī's *Zīj al-Ṣābiʾ* in Toledo into Latin. The Arabic word *jayb* (جَيب) bears two distinct senses: (i) in trigonometry "the bowstring", the Arabic-letter form of Sanskrit *jīvā* — the spelling *j-y-b* is nearly identical to that of *jayb*; (ii) in everyday Arabic "a pocket, a bay, a fold of the bosom". Gerard recognised the second, did not know the first, and rendered the word into Latin as *sinus* ("bosom, fold"). The mistranslation is a typical philological accident of the twelfth-and-thirteenth-century Toledan Latin translation school: the translator could not, in the diacritic-less Arabic of his manuscripts, distinguish the two uses of one word.

        From the thirteenth century onward the word settled into European mathematics as *sinus*; in Fibonacci's *Liber Abaci* (1202) and in the later mathematical texts of Boncompagno, Sacrobosco, and Campanus, the terminology of *sinus rectus* (sine) and *sinus versus* (versine) became fixed. Regiomontanus's *De triangulis omnimodis* (1464, printed posthumously in 1533), as the first systematic textbook of European trigonometry, made *sinus* the official term. In the sixteenth century, Copernicus, Tycho and Kepler — the astronomer-mathematicians of the modern scientific revolution — used the word *sine* without ever questioning it. The permanence of the mistranslation: *once the term has settled, even after it has lost its conceptual transparency, the capacity to do mathematics with it keeps it in place.* The concept correct, the word incorrect, the system at work — this is the typical course of modern scientific terminology.

      tr: |
        9. yüzyılda el-Hârezmî Bağdat'ta *Zīj al-Sindhind* (Sindhind Tabloları) adlı astronomi eserini yazdı. *Sindhind*, Sanskritçe *Siddhānta* (Hint astronomi külliyatı) kelimesinden geliyor; eser Brahmagupta'nın *Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta*'sının (628) Arapçaya çevirisini temel alır. Brahmagupta'nın metni trigonometrinin temel niceliği olan **jyā**'yı (Sanskritçe "yay-teli, çember-yay'ının teli") sistematik olarak kullanır: bir çemberin yayının teli, modern sinüsün geometrik karşılığıdır (yarıçapla orantılı). *Jyā* Sanskritçede j-ī-v-ā harflerinden oluşur (uzun ī, kısa ā). 

        Arapça çevirmenler — ya el-Fazârî, ya el-Hârezmî'nin kendisi — bu sözcüğü Arapça harflerle yazmak istediklerinde bir problemle karşılaşırlar: Arapça yazımda kısa ünlüler genellikle yazılmaz; *jīvā* sözcüğü harf-iskeletinde *j-y-v* veya *j-y-b* olarak okunabilir (Arapça'da *v* sesi yok, en yakın eşdeğeri *b*'dir). Yazıya geçirdiklerinde çıkan harf-dizisi *جيب* — *j-y-b* — Arapçada zaten var olan ortak bir kelime: *jayb* "cep, koyun". Sanskrit kavramı, Arapça sözcüğün gövdesine girer; iki yüz yıl boyunca *jayb* hem "yay-teli" hem "cep" anlamına gelir.         In the ninth century al-Khwārizmī wrote in Baghdad his astronomical work *Zīj al-Sindhind* (the Tables of the *Sindhind*). *Sindhind* is the Arabic form of Sanskrit *Siddhānta* (the Indian astronomical corpus); the work is based on an Arabic rendering of Brahmagupta's *Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta* (628). Brahmagupta's text systematically uses, as the fundamental quantity of trigonometry, the term **jyā** (Sanskrit "bowstring, the chord of a circular arc"): the chord of an arc on a circle, the geometric counterpart of the modern sine (proportional to the radius). *Jyā* is written in Sanskrit with the letters j-ī-v-ā (long ī, short ā).

        When the Arabic translators — al-Fazārī, or al-Khwārizmī himself — wanted to write this term in Arabic letters, they ran into a problem: short vowels are usually unwritten in Arabic; the word *jīvā* could be read in its consonantal skeleton as *j-y-v* or *j-y-b* (Arabic has no *v* sound; the nearest letter is *b*). The letter-string they wrote, *جيب* — *j-y-b* — was a word already present in Arabic: *jayb*, "a pocket, a bay". The Sanskrit concept entered the body of the Arabic word; for two centuries *jayb* meant both "bowstring" and "pocket". For the Arab astronomer the context was sufficient — *al-jayb al-mustawī* (the straight bowstring) did not blur with the ordinary *jayb* (the pocket). But for the twelfth-century Latin translator that double meaning was invisible; he found only the common-usage sense in his lexicon, and translated "pocket".

        Al-Battānī (c. 858-929), working in his Raqqa observatory, in writing the *Zīj al-Ṣābiʾ* (the Sabian Tables, c. 900) recomputed the sine tables on a decimal-based radius (a radius of one, not sixty) — the first great step from the sexagesimal system of Greek astronomy toward decimal mathematics. This book was then translated into Latin by Gerard of Cremona at Toledo; and so the word *sine* carried both its computational capacity and its philological accident together into Europe.

        The prehistory of trigonometry is not to be sought in the Greek astronomy of Hipparchus (c. 150 BC) or in Ptolemy's *Almagest* (c. AD 150). The Greeks used, as a trigonometric quantity, the *chord* (the straight distance between two ends of an arc). Book I of the *Almagest* gives a table of chords at sixty-minute intervals. The chord of an angle — the straight line drawn between the two ends of the arc — is geometrically **twice** the modern sine (formally: twice the sine of the half-angle).

        From the fifth century onward Indian mathematicians changed that structure: in place of the chord they used the **half-chord** (Sanskrit *ardha-jyā*, later abbreviated to *jyā*). This is not merely a notational change; it cleans up the mathematics of trigonometry. The conversions between the full chord and the chord of the half-angle require involved formulae; the half-chord — that is, the sine — attaches directly to the geometry of the right-angled triangle. Āryabhaṭa's *Āryabhaṭīya* (499) carries the first systematic *jyā* table: twenty-four values, at intervals of 3°45', up to 90°. Brahmagupta's *Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta* (628) makes that table more accurate. Indian trigonometry is the direct prefiguration of modern trigonometry: the same function, the same tables, only a different name (*jyā* instead of *sine*).

        This Indian trigonometric apparatus enters Arabic astronomy through the Sindhind translation programme of c. 770-810. Ibrāhīm al-Fazārī and Yaʿqūb ibn Ṭāriq, in rendering Brahmagupta's text into Arabic, wrote *jyā* in the Arabic consonantal skeleton as *jayb* — and the Sanskrit "bowstring" was abruptly transmuted into the Arabic "pocket". The concept, of course, is the same: the direct distance of an arc-chord, the trigonometric counterpart of the half-angle.

        The starting problem of trigonometric thinking is astronomy: the mathematical description of the position of a star or planet on the spherical sky. Plane geometry (Euclid) is insufficient for this; what is needed is spherical geometry. Hipparchus (Rhodes, c. 150 BCE) computes a chord table for the solution of right-angled spherical triangles of the celestial sphere — the earliest trigonometric tool known to the ancient world. Hipparchus's own texts are lost; but Ptolemy's *Almagest* (c. AD 150) preserves and develops his work. Book I, chapter 11 of the *Almagest*: chords at every 30 of arc on a radius of 60.

        Indian mathematicians most likely learned this Greek-Hellenistic trigonometry through Alexandrian-Indian trade-and-science contact in the first to third centuries AD. The Roman-Indian Ocean trade (first century AD) carried Greek astronomical texts to India; the Indian mathematicians took this Greek chord-trigonometry in hand and transformed it through the innovation of the **half-chord (jyā)**. In Āryabhaṭa, in the fifth century, this new form is established.

        The Indian *jyā* is not merely a word but a conceptual revolution: working with the half-chord instead of the full chord is the moment of birth of modern trigonometry. For the modern sine — the ratio of the side opposite to an angle to the hypotenuse — is identical with the geometric definition of *jyā*. The Greek chord is a step further away (formally: `chord(α) = 2 · sin(α/2)`). That this half-chord discovery is not a chance geometric simplification but **the birth of the modern form of trigonometry** has, however, won its full value only with the twentieth-century history-of-science work on Indian mathematics (David Pingree, Kim Plofker).
