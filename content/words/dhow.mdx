      en: |
        In the modern maritime vocabulary, *dhow* is the general name for the traditional wooden boats still in use in the Indian Ocean basin (East Africa, the Arabian Peninsula, the Indo-Pakistani coast); the sub-types *baghla*, *boum*, *sambuq*, *jalboot* are gathered under one category. In the twenty-first century, dhow-type boats still navigate the Indian Ocean waters — despite post-oil economic transformation and modern tankers, they are active in fishing and local-trade between Kerala, Bahrain, Mombasa and Zanzibar. UNESCO added the "Pearl-diving and Pearl-Trade in the Gulf" in 2011 and "Al-Khateera dhow building" in 2017 to its Intangible Cultural Heritage list.

        In the modern technical use of the word, the *lateen* (triangular) sail-type is the defining element: this sail-engineering was optimised for the seasonal winds (monsoons) of the Indian Ocean; it is known as an ancient invention of Arab navigator-engineering. Modern yacht-engineering — the Bermudan sail-form — is the direct technological descendant of this Arab *lateen* sail-principle.

      en: |
        The English word *dhow* was born around 1799-1810, during the British Empire's Indian-Ocean sea-exploration; it was taken from the Swahili-Arab maritime lingua franca. The Swahili form *daw* / *dauw* (derived in the East-African coastal tongue from Arabic *dāw*) was written by British navigators and colonial officers in their records as *dhow*; the initial *dh-* spelling is a traditional British-colonial transliteration rule for marking the Swahili-Arab *d-* sound rather than the English *th-*. From the mid-nineteenth century, the British Navy's operations against the slave-trade *dhow*s widened the word's circulation in the European press: *dhow chase*, *anti-slavery dhow patrol*. From the 1820s to the 1880s the British Indian Navy pursued dhows with steam-powered ships in Indian Ocean waters.

        The passage of the word into English-Europe is not contemporary with the classical Arab-Islamic maritime period (eighth-fifteenth centuries) but in a very late colonial-contact context. This places the etymology-value of the word in modern English in its correct philological position: it comes not from the Mediterranean lingua franca (twelfth-fifteenth centuries) but from the nineteenth-century British-Indian-Ocean colonial contact. This is a different philological transit-point from words like *cable* and *admiral* with their older Mediterranean-Crusader channels.

      tr: |
        Arap denizcilik altın çağı (8.-15. yy) Hint Okyanusu'nun ekonomi-politik egemenliğini tek başına yönetti. Çin (Tang-Song-Yuan dönemleri Quanzhou'su), Hindistan (Malabar sahili Calicut'u), Doğu Afrika (Kilwa-Mombasa-Zanzibar), Arap yarımadası (Aden, Sohar, Julfar, Hormuz), Pers Körfezi — hepsi Arap-Müslüman denizcilerin ortak ticaret-ağıydı. Bu sürecin teknolojik omurgası *dāw* / *dhow* tekne tipiydi: tek veya çoklu direkli, ahşap karkaslı (*sunbuk* tipi: Arabistan kıyısının dikdörtgen-pruvalı tipi; *baghla* tipi: yüksek-pruvalı uzun-tekne), üçgen lateen yelkenli, 50-200 ton kargo taşıyabilen pratik gemi.

        Ahmed bin Mâcid (1432-1500), Hint Okyanusu Arap denizciliğinin en büyük belgeleyicisi, *Kitāb al-Fawāʾid fī uṣūl ʿilm al-baḥr wa-al-qawāʿid* (Deniz Bilim Esasları ve Kuralları Kitabı) eserinde dhow seyrüseferinin teknik kurallarını sistemli olarak yazdı: yıldız-navigasyon, mevsim-rüzgarları, kıyı-haritalama, liman-girişi protokolleri. Bu Arap deniz-bilim geleneği (rahnāmag literatürü) Avrupa'nın 15. yy sonu deniz-keşiflerinde dolaylı olarak kullanıldı; Vasco da Gama'nın 1498 Calicut seferinin Malindi-Calicut son ayağında bir Arap denizci pilotu (efsaneye göre Ibn Mājid kendisi, ama belgelerden netleştirilememiş) Portekiz gemisini güvenli geçişte yönlendirdiği rivayetiyle tarihe geçti.
      en: |
        The Arab maritime golden age (eighth-fifteenth centuries) ruled the economic-political dominance of the Indian Ocean on its own. China (Quanzhou under the Tang-Song-Yuan dynasties), India (Calicut on the Malabar coast), East Africa (Kilwa-Mombasa-Zanzibar), the Arabian peninsula (Aden, Sohar, Julfar, Hormuz), the Persian Gulf — all were a shared commercial network of Arab-Muslim navigators. The technological backbone of this process was the *dāw* / *dhow* ship-type: single- or multi-masted, wood-framed (the *sunbuq* type: the rectangular-bowed type of the Arabian coast; the *baghla* type: the high-bowed long-boat), with triangular lateen sails, capable of carrying 50-200 tons of cargo.

        Ahmad ibn Mājid (1432-1500), the greatest documenter of Arab Indian-Ocean navigation, in his *Kitāb al-Fawāʾid fī uṣūl ʿilm al-baḥr wa-al-qawāʿid* (The Book of Useful Things in the Principles and Rules of the Sea-Science), systematically set down the technical rules of dhow navigation: star-navigation, the seasonal winds, the coast-mapping, port-entry protocols. This Arab maritime-science tradition (the *rahnāmag* literature) was indirectly used in Europe's late-fifteenth-century sea-explorations; the story passed into history that, on the Malindi-Calicut final leg of Vasco da Gama's 1498 voyage, an Arab navigator-pilot (in legend, Ibn Mājid himself, but not confirmed in documents) guided the Portuguese ship to safe passage.

      en: |
        The lateen sail (the triangular-form sail, mounted not on a vertical-upright but on an inclined yard) is the characteristic technological invention of Arab Indian-Ocean navigation: it offered a manoeuvring-ability against the monsoon winds, a much more refined technological development than the old Mediterranean square-sail of the Greco-Roman world. Etymologically, the word *lateen* in modern European languages (Old French *latine*, Latin *latina*) is connected with the ninth-to-eleventh-century process of the Mediterranean Muslim/Arab navigators' transfer of the sail-type into European Latin — ironically, the name "Latin (sail)" is Arab-sourced. The lateen spread to the Mediterranean from the Indian Ocean, along Arab navigation, through the ninth and tenth centuries.

        The modern *dhow* ship-type is the packaged form of Arab maritime-engineering: the lateen sail + the wood-framed technique + monsoon-wind navigation + star-direction-finding + dhow-specific port-organisation. European wind-engineering adopted the Mediterranean half of this package (the lateen + the manoeuvring-technique) in the eleventh-to-thirteenth century; the remaining Indian-Ocean-specific elements (the monsoons, the spice-trade routes) opened to Europe only from the late fifteenth century (the Portuguese-Dutch Indian-Ocean trade period). That is why the word *dhow* enters Europe late, by the British colonial route in the nineteenth century: Europe had already taken in only one part of the dhow-technology (the lateen), much earlier.

      en: |
        The pre-Islamic antique history of Arab navigation reaches back to 1500 BC - AD 600. The Persian Gulf (Dilmun, Magan = modern Bahrain-Oman), the Red Sea (Saba, Himyar), the Yemen-Aden coast (the Punt-route, the spice-and-gold trade with ancient Egypt) — every side of the Arabian peninsula is a practical-master of ancient sea-trade. Sumerian economic tablets (2500 BC) include the lists of trade in copper, ship-mast timber, oils etc. with Magan and Dilmun. The Greek *Periplus Maris Erythraei* (first century AD) describes the port-organisation of the Arab coast in detail.

        The technical foundation of this ancient Arab navigation — the wood-framed boat, the sewn-cord (rather than iron-nailed) ship-construction with palm-fibre, the monsoon-wind navigation — was taken over by the seventh-century Islamic conquest and carried into the golden age of Islamic-Arab navigation. The modern *dhow* ship-type is the continued form of this three-thousand-year-long uninterrupted technological tradition. Each of the modern dhow sub-types — *sunbuq*, *baghla*, *boum* — comes from a different ancient Arab-geographical source region: Bahrain-Oman, Aden-Yemen, the East-African coast. The word (*dāw*), outside the Mediterranean-European philological channel, carries the entire history of a maritime tradition that stands on its own feet.
