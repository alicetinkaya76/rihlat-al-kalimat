      en: |
        In Late Latin, *colpus* (fifth-to-ninth century, borrowed from the Greek *kólpos*) meant "a bay, a coastal-recess"; but Late Latin use remained limited. In the twelfth-and-thirteenth-century Italian maritime republics (Genoa, Venice, Pisa) the word *golfo* (Old Italian *gholfo*) was standardised in the geographical vocabulary of Mediterranean commerce. Two hypotheses for the Italian *g-* initial: (i) a sound-change from Late Latin *colpus* (c → g, hardening of the initial); (ii) a softening of sound under the contact of the Mediterranean trader with the Arabic word *khalīj*.

        Old French *golfe* (thirteenth century) is from Italian; English *gulf* (mid-fourteenth, in Chaucer's *The Knight's Tale*) is from French. The standardisation of the word in the European-Mediterranean geographical vocabulary from the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries on is its settling as a building-block of the navigator-trader's lingua franca. Modern geographers use the word as the classification category for all coastal recesses around the world: "the Gulf of Aden", "the Gulf of Mexico", "the Gulf of Tonkin".

        Arapça *khalīj* (خَليج, kök fiili *kh-l-j* "ayırmak, sızdırmak") — denizden karaya doğru uzanan büyük girinti, ana-deniz-küçük-koy ayırması. Pre-İslamic Arap denizci ve coğrafyacı diline yerleşmişti; *al-Khalīj al-Fārisī* (Pers Körfezi), *al-Khalīj al-ʿArabī* (Arap Körfezi, hâlâ Süveyş bağlamında) klasik Arap coğrafyasında 9. yy itibariyle standart. el-Masʿūdī'nin *Murūj al-dhahab*'ında (c. 947), İbn Khurradādhbih'in *Kitāb al-masālik wa-al-mamālik*'inde (c. 870), İbn Battuta'nın *Riḥla*'sında (c. 1355) sözcük sürekli olarak geçer.

Akdeniz Lingua Franca'sında Arap-Yunan-Latin-Romance tüccarlar günlük olarak *khalīj* / *kolpos* / *colpus* / *golfo* sözcüklerini birbirine geçirerek kullandı. Sözcüğün Avrupa formuna ses-açısı, bu Akdeniz teması bağlamında Arap *kh-* sesinin Romance *g-* sesine kayması ve Yunan *k-* sesinin de Romance *g-* sesine kayması, *birbirini güçlendiren* iki paralel ses-süreç olarak okunmalıdır.
      en: |
        Arabic *khalīj* (خَليج, from the verbal root *kh-l-j*, "to separate, to leak") is a large recess running from the sea into the land, separating a main-sea from a small-bay. It was settled in the pre-Islamic Arab navigator's and geographer's tongue; *al-Khalīj al-Fārisī* (the Persian Gulf), *al-Khalīj al-ʿArabī* (the Arab Gulf, still in the Suez context) became standard in classical Arab geography by the ninth century. The word recurs continuously in al-Masʿūdī's *Murūj al-dhahab* (c. 947), in Ibn Khurradādhbih's *Kitāb al-masālik wa-al-mamālik* (c. 870), and in Ibn Baṭṭūṭa's *Riḥla* (c. 1355).

        Through the centuries that the Arab navigator-trader spent on the Mediterranean, the Red Sea, the Persian Gulf and the Indian Ocean, the word *khalīj* settled as a technical term naming coastal geography. In the Mediterranean lingua franca, Arab-Greek-Latin-Romance traders used the words *khalīj* / *kolpos* / *colpus* / *golfo* in daily mutual passage. The sound-angle of the word's European form is to be read, in the context of that Mediterranean contact, as two parallel sound-processes mutually reinforcing one another: the shift of the Arabic *kh-* to Romance *g-* and the shift of the Greek *k-* to Romance *g-*.

        The pre-Islamic Arab navigators, over centuries on the Mediterranean, the Red Sea and the Persian Gulf, used a shared geographical terminology with the Greek-Roman navigators.       en: |
        In the ancient eastern Mediterranean world, the Phoenician maritime trader (1500-300 BC) worked in parallel with Greek, Egyptian, Mesopotamian and Arab navigators. Though the Phoenician language (a Semitic) and the Greek language (an Indo-European) belong to different language families, the shared sea-experience produced a parallel conceptual evolution: the words for "coastal recess" (Phoenician/Hebrew *ḥālag*, Greek *kolpos*) come from different language families but from similar conceptual-geographical content. The same pattern is observable across all the languages of the pre-modern Mediterranean basin.

        That is, the etymological debate of the modern word *gulf* (Greek *kólpos* or Arab *khalīj*?) is to be re-read in the context of the polyglot maritime-trade of the ancient eastern Mediterranean. In that context the correct word-history is this: Greek and Arabic (and their pre-cognates Phoenician, Akkadian) maritime-traders named the same geographical concept in parallel for a millennium; the European lingua franca in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries is the unification of that multi-naming tradition in a single word-form. *Gulf* comes neither only from the Greek nor only from the Arab; it comes from the shared geographical vocabulary of the Mediterranean-Near Eastern maritime culture.
