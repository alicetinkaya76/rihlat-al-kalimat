  en: "*literally:* the thing thrown down, that which is laid on the ground. Arabic *al-maṭraḥ* (المَطرَح) — from the root *ṭ-r-ḥ* (to throw, to set down) on the *mafʿal* pattern (place-or-instrument-of) — *the place where something is laid*, by extension *a pad laid on the ground for sleep*. The word passed from spoken Arabic — especially the Maghrebi and Andalusian dialects — into the Mediterranean merchant tongue; Sicilian *materazzo / matarazzo* (twelfth-to-thirteenth century), Italian *materasso*, Old French *materas / matelas*, English *mattress*. In each language the word holds its core sense, *a sleeping pad laid on the ground*. The *al-* prefix is lost on the Italian-French line, but the vowel changes between Andalusian and Sicilian Arabic (*maṭraḥ > materazz*) are striking phonetic parallels. The Norman Sicilian court carried a domestic Arabic word into Latin life — like *admiral*, but household rather than military."

Courtly in origin but never courtly in centre: a word democratising itself.

      en: |
        Norman Sicily (1072-1194) preserved, under its own royal roof, the Arabic-speaking population, the legal system, the administrative cadre, and the architectural-and-furniture culture inherited from the Muslim emirate (831-1072). At Roger II's court (r. 1130-1154) Muslim officers with the title *qāʾid* (commander) served, royal decrees were issued in Arabic-Latin-Greek (the *scriptorium* maintained parallel-text documents), and the muqarnas ceilings of the Cappella Palatina were built — the intra-cultural articulation from which *mattress* emerged sits exactly at this point. In the bedchambers of the court *al-maṭraḥ* was an everyday word; Norman lords, administrative clerks and Latin clergy heard it from Arabic-speaking chamber servants and carried it into their own Latin and Norman French. *Materazzo* is documented in Sicilian Latin from the mid-twelfth century onward. The same court from which *admiral* emerges (Arabic *amīr al-baḥr > ammiratus*) — one military hierarchy, the other room furniture; two distinct courtly registers entering the language through a single channel. Mattress + admiral: Sicily's two signatures.

        The Muslim emirate of Sicily was founded by the Aghlabid conquest beginning at Tunis in 831; by the fall of Taormina in 902 the island was wholly under Islamic rule. For three centuries Sicily was an Arabic-speaking region: part of its population was Maghrebi Berber-Arab migrant, part was the local Greek-Latin population Islamised, and the dialect was a close cousin of the Maghrebi Arabic of Tunisia and Algeria. *Al-maṭraḥ* was an ordinary word of this Sicilian Arabic — a pad, a sleeping mat, a thing laid on the ground. High scientific words like those of medicine or astronomy enter Latin via the page in Toledo; everyday furniture words like *mattress* enter Latin life via the household in Sicily. Two different mechanisms of language contact.

      en: |
        The classical Arabic root *ṭ-r-ḥ* means "to throw, to lay down, to strike"; *ṭaraḥa* (the verb), *ṭarḥ* (the verbal noun), *al-maṭraḥ* (the place-noun: the place where something is thrown). In pre-Islamic poetry *maṭraḥ* names *the place a thing is set down* — the spot where an arrow falls in battle, the field on which a caravan settles, the sand into which a camel kneels. In tent life the cover laid for a sleeper is called by the phrase *the thing thrown on the ground* — *maṭraḥ*. The word's *mattress/sleeping-pad* sense rises out of this tent practice; in settled life (village or town) *firāsh* (the bed) is more common, but in nomadic life *maṭraḥ* is more natural. The retention of *maṭraḥ* in the Maghrebi dialects fits this nomadic / semi-nomadic background. When Arab nomadic culture was translated into Mediterranean merchant life, *maṭraḥ* was translated as a word too: first as a guest in Sicily, then in Norman court Latin, then into every European language. *The name of an action, identified with an object, made the world tour.*
