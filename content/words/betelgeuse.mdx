      en: |
        In the Toledo translations (twelfth-thirteenth century), mis-reading errors in the Latinisation of Arabic astronomical texts were a standard-pattern of philology. In Arabic script, the words *yad* (يد, hand) and *bayt* (بيت, house) are visually similar; especially in quickly-written copies, the dot-positions of the *y* and *b* initials are easily confused. The Arabic-reading Christian and Mudéjar Muslim team-members of Gerard of Cremona's workshop probably made this error — in the manuscript, what they read as *yad al-jawzāʾ* (يد الجوزاء) was wrongly segmented as *bayt al-jawzāʾ* (بيت الجوزاء). In transcribing the word into Latin spelling, forms like *Bet-Elgeuze* / *Beit Algeuze* were produced.

        In late-thirteenth-/early-fourteenth-century Latin astronomical texts, many variants of the word are seen: *Bet Algeuze*, *Bedalgeuze*, *Beldengeuze*, *Beteigeuze*. In Bartholomeus de Granate Albo's *Tabulae Resolutae* of 1521, the form *Betelgeuze* was standardised; Bayer's *Uranometria* of 1603 made this form generally accepted in Europe. The spelling of the modern form *Betelgeuse* has been standard since the middle of the seventeenth century. An interesting philological reversal: *Bet* (بيت) means "house" in Arabic; for the modern reader, *Bet-el-jawzāʾ* means "the house of Jawzāʾ", whereas in the original Arab practical-astronomy *yad-al-jawzāʾ* is "the hand of Jawzāʾ" — a shoulder-region indicating an anatomical-position.

        The fossilisation of this mis-transcription is read, in modern comparative history-of-science literature (Paul Kunitzsch 1959, Bernard Goldstein 1965), as the case-instance of "fossil-error" in the Arab-Latin philological transfer. Modern astronomy did not change the word; the error itself was preserved as a historical-evidence; for the same star, modern Arab astronomical literature still uses the correct word *yad al-jawzāʾ*, but it is the Latin-mistaken form that passed into the language of modern science.

      en: |
        In the pre-Islamic Arab astronomical tradition, *al-Jawzāʾ* (الجَوزاء) referred to the region of the modern Orion constellation — the Arabic word was not the name of a specific mythological figure, but in the Arab-practice it was described as an anatomical-image of a "central-region-large, body-prominent" star-figure. In the oral tradition compilations of pre-Islamic Arab clans, *al-Jawzāʾ* lives both as a star-figure and as a mythological woman-image — in some Arab-folk traditions, the image of "a woman sitting at the well-side".

        The trace of this Arab-folk anatomical-approach is still alive in the modern star-names of the Orion constellation: *Betelgeuse* (yad al-jawzāʾ, Jawzāʾ's hand/right-shoulder), *Bellatrix* (Latin "warrior woman", a re-reading of the translation of the Arabic *al-najīd* / *al-murzim*), *Rigel* (Arabic *rijl al-jawzāʾ*, Jawzāʾ's foot), *Saiph* (Arabic *sayf al-jawzāʾ*, Jawzāʾ's sword). A five-star anatomical-figure: hand-shoulder / other-hand / belt / foot / sword. This systematic anatomical naming shows the distinction of the Arab-pre-Islamic desert-star tradition from the Greek-mythological naming: Greek star-names are figurative-mythological (Orion = the Hunter), Arab star-names are anatomical-practical (heart, shoulder, hand, foot).

        In al-Ṣūfī's *Kitāb al-Kawākib al-Thābita* (c. 964) for Orion two picture-descriptions are shown side by side: the Greek-mythological Hunter figure and the Arab-folk Jawzāʾ figure. Al-Battānī's Raqqa-observatory produced minute-accurate position-tables of *yad al-jawzāʾ*. This Arab astronomical data-infrastructure became the basic-source for the twelfth-thirteenth-century Toledo transfer; during the lexical-misreading the modern word *Betelgeuse* was born by the *yad* → *bet* error.
