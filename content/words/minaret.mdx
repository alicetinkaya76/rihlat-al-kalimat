      en: |
        French *minaret* was borrowed from Turkish *minare* in 1606; the typical French diminutive suffix *-et* was added (compare *baronet*, *cabinet*). Because the Turkish form of the word was the standard term of mosque architecture from the Anatolian Saljuq and Ottoman periods (eleventh-twentieth centuries), European travellers (Pierre Belon, Jean Chardin, the Sieur de Thévenot) carried into Europe the form they heard directly on Turko-Ottoman ground. Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's 1716-18 letters from Constantinople introduced the French *minaret* to English letter-readers.

        In the classical-architectural Ottoman period (sixteenth-seventeenth century), the four-minareted silhouette in Sinan's masterworks like the Süleymaniye and the Selimiye was carried into Europe as the Sinan-pattern, depicted in most travellers' travelogues of the seventeenth-eighteenth century. This visual iconography (high cylindrical towers, the domed main mosque, the surrounding garden) became a fundamental component of the modern West's "Islamic architecture" visualisation template. The word *minaret* passed into Europe from the Turkish-Ottoman form, not from the Arabic *manāra* — this small philological difference is the historical proof that, in modern Europe, the word represents the Ottoman visual heritage.

        Sözcüğün Arap mimari sözlüğünde iki form vardır: (i) *manāra* (مَنارة) — "ışık-veren yer", *n-w-r* fiilinden; köken olarak deniz feneri ya da yüksek-işaret-kulesi anlamına çıkar; (ii) *miʾdhana* (مِئذَنة) — "ezan yeri", *adhāna* (ezan okumak) fiilinden türetildi; daha geç (10. yy) ve daha fonksiyon-spesifik bir ad. İslam dünyasının farklı bölgelerinde iki form yan yana kullanılır: Mağrip ve Endülüs'te *manāra* baskın, Doğu İslam dünyasında *miʾdhana*. Türkçe ve İran tarafı *minare/munāre* formunu seçer; bu Türkçe-form Avrupa'ya geçen kelimedir.
      en: |
        In the first century of Islam (seventh century), the call to prayer was simple: the muezzin made the call from a high point on the mosque — the roof-platform that Bilāl used in the Prophet's Mosque in Medina. The construction of the Great Mosque of Damascus (705-715) under the Umayyad Caliph al-Walīd I is the turning point in Islamic history at which the first monumental minarets — four corner-towers — were built. Inspired by the Byzantine basilica or Roman monumental towers, great stone minarets. After Damascus, the minaret became the standard architectural element of Islamic mosques.

        The word has two forms in the Arabic architectural vocabulary: (i) *manāra* (مَنارة) — "the light-giving place", from the verb *n-w-r*; etymologically the sense leads to a lighthouse or a high signal-tower; (ii) *miʾdhana* (مِئْذَنة) — "the place of the call to prayer", derived from the verb *adhāna* (to call to prayer); a later (tenth century) and more function-specific name. In the different regions of the Islamic world the two forms run side by side: in the Maghreb and al-Andalus, *manāra* dominates; in the eastern Islamic world, *miʾdhana*. The Turkish and Iranian side prefers the form *minare/munāre*; this Turkish form is the word that crossed into Europe.

      en: |
        The Semitic verb root *n-w-r* "to give light, to shine" is a basic root of the ancient Semitic languages: Akkadian *nūru* (light), Hebrew *nūr* (light, fire, lamp), Syriac *nūrā* (light), Arabic *nūr* (نور, light). *Manāra* is, from this root, an instrument-or-place noun meaning "the light-giving place" — a lighthouse (the Mediterranean *Pharos* type, the Arabic counterpart of the ancient Alexandrian Pharos), a light-giving watchtower, or, metaphorically, "a guide, a leader". In the ancient Arab period too, the word *manāra* carried the senses "lighthouse, high-signalling-tower".

        Islamic architecture in the eighth century took the word with an architectural specialisation: the meaning of a monumental mosque-tower. The ancient sense-range of the word (lighthouse, watchtower, metaphorical guide) was narrowed by the Islamic architectural usage; in the modern European word *minaret* only the mosque-tower sense remains. This narrowing of meaning is a case of single-sense specialisation: a polysemous ancient Semitic word is frozen into the specific name of an architectural object. The Turkish *minare* still bears the same specialised sense; the Arabic *manāra*, in modern Arabic, still carries both the mosque-tower sense and the lighthouse sense (Egyptian *manārat al-Iskandariyya*, "the lighthouse of Alexandria").
