      en: |
        Giorgio Vasari's *Le Vite de' più eccellenti pittori, scultori e architettori* (The Lives of the Most Eminent Painters, Sculptors and Architects; first edition 1550, expanded second edition 1568) is the first great text of modern art history. The term *arabesco* used by Vasari here names a category in art history: a style of ornament in which the geometry of vegetal motifs, of curving lines and of Kufic-style calligraphy-weave is dominant. Vasari uses this term when he discusses Raphael's (1483-1520) stucco ornament of the Loggia di Raffaello (in the Vatican, 1517-1519) — and positions it as a new category alongside the classical Roman *grotteschi* of Pompeii and the Domus Aurea.

        Through the seventeenth to nineteenth centuries *arabesque* becomes the technical name of the European ornamental aesthetic. Owen Jones's *Grammar of Ornament* of 1856, listing thirty-seven distinct ornamental traditions, devotes separate chapters to Chapter 3 *Mauresque*, Chapter 4 *Arabian*, Chapter 5 *Turkish* — *arabesque* is no longer a single category but the parallel-style cluster of the Muslim ornamental traditions. This classification has been the basis of the vocabulary of modern museology since the 1893 Chicago World's Fair.

      tr: |
        15. yüzyıl Venedik'i Doğu Akdeniz ve Müslüman dünya arasında sürdürdüğü tüccar ağıyla Avrupa'nın Müslüman sanat-eşyalarına talep merkezi haline geldi. Müslüman dünyasından gelen *damaschino* kakma metalwork (Şam'dan), *moresca* desenli ipek kumaşlar (Endülüs ve Magrip'ten), İslamik nakışlar, Hint-İran halıları — hepsi Venedik aristokratlarının saraylarını süslemek için ithal edildi. Yerel Venedik atölyeleri 1450'lerden itibaren bu eşyaların *taklitlerini* yapmaya başladılar: ucuz, yerel pazara yönelik, Müslüman süsleme motiflerini Latin malzeme ve teknikle yeniden üreten *all'arabesca* nesneler.

        İşte tam burada kelime doğar: *arabesco* — *Arap-tarzı*, *Arap-üslubuyla yapılmış*. İlk yazılı kullanım Venedik tüccar envanterinde 1452'de geçer (*una camisa lavorata all'arabesca* — "Arap-üslubuyla işlenmiş bir gömlek"). Anlam taklit ile orijinal arasındaki bilinçli farkı işaretler: gerçek bir Müslüman-Doğu kökenli eser değil, *onun gibi yapılmış* bir Latin ürünü. Bu özel anlam katmanı — *referans-olmadan-üslup* — modern *arabesque*'in çekirdeğidir; Vasari'nin 100 yıl sonra terimi sanat tarihine taşıdığında bu çekirdek dokunulmaz kalır.
      en: |
        Fifteenth-century Venice, through the merchant network it maintained between the eastern Mediterranean and the Muslim world, became Europe's centre of demand for Muslim art-objects. The *damaschino* inlaid metalwork from the Muslim world (from Damascus), *moresca*-patterned silk fabrics (from al-Andalus and the Maghreb), Islamic embroideries, Indo-Persian carpets — all were imported to ornament the palaces of the Venetian aristocrats. From the 1450s onwards the local Venetian workshops began to make *imitations* of these objects: cheap, addressed to the local market, *all'arabesca* objects reproducing Muslim ornamental motifs with Latin materials and techniques.

        It is precisely here that the word is born: *arabesco* — *in the Arab manner*, *made in the Arab style*. The first written use is in a Venetian merchant inventory of 1452 (*una camisa lavorata all'arabesca* — "a shirt worked in the Arab manner"). The meaning marks a deliberate distinction between imitation and original: not an actual work of Muslim-Eastern origin, but a Latin product *made like it*. This particular layer of meaning — *style-without-reference* — is the core of the modern *arabesque*; when Vasari carries the term into art history a hundred years later, this core remains untouched.

      en: |
        When the Christian forces took Toledo in 1085 — then Seville in 1248, Granada in 1492 — a portion of the Muslim population remained. They were called *Mudéjar* (from Arabic *mudajjan* — "those allowed to stay"). The Mudéjar artisans continued to work in the Christian palaces and churches — woodwork, stucco ornament, tile, wood-inlay (*taracea* from Arabic *tarsīʿ*) — in the Andalusian style. The Alcázar of Seville (rebuilt in 1364 for Pedro the Cruel), the Sinagoga del Tránsito of Toledo (1356-1357), the Alhambra of Granada (in the 1370s) — all are technical peaks of the Mudéjar style.

        The most surprising side of this style: one and the same workshop, with the same workman, makes a Kufic inscription for the minaret of a mosque one day, carves vegetal motifs on the capital of a Christian church the next, traces geometry on the *mizraḥ* of a Jewish synagogue the day after that. The style is one; the content and the patron change. The modern *style-without-reference* structure of the word *arabesque* is born in this Mudéjar practice — the ability to detach a style from its original religious-cultural context and carry it into another context existed as a social reality in twelfth- to fifteenth-century al-Andalus. What the Venetian workshops were doing in the fifteenth century was simply the repackaging of this.

      en: |
        Between 936 and 961 ʿAbd al-Raḥmān III, the Andalusian Umayyad Caliph, had a new palace-city built 8 km west of Cordoba called *Madīnat al-Zahrāʾ* — the City of Flowers. This structure is the birthplace of the visual vocabulary of the modern concept of *arabesque*: in the marble and stucco carvings, *aṣbāʿ al-sāḥirāt* (the fingers of the witches — coiled ribbon motifs); *muqarnas* (the stalactite-stalagmite-form vaulted niches); *zalīj* (geometrically cut ceramic mosaic); *khaṭṭ kūfī muzakhrafa* (Kufic inscription interlaced with vegetal motifs); vegetal motifs (vine, acanthus, lotus); geometric panels (Pythagorean compositions, knotted-strapwork). All these elements — every element of the modern *arabesque* — converge here.

        Madīnat al-Zahrāʾ was destroyed during the Berber revolt of 1010-1013; but the visual vocabulary of its court ornament continued as the canon of Andalusian art down to the fourteenth-fifteenth-century Alhambra of Granada. In the marble ornament of the eleventh-century cathedral of Pisa, in the Capilla Palatina of Roger II of Norman Sicily (1140-1170), in San Marco of Venice (in its tenth-to-thirteenth-century phases), the passage of Muslim ornament into Mediterranean Christian art begins. When Vasari discovered the term in 1550, what *arabesco* named was a six-centuries-old visual tradition; the word is a belated naming.
