        Filolojik detay: Latin *Altair* yazılışı Arap *al-ṭāʾir*'in fonolojik adaptasyonudur. Arap *ṭ-* sesi (vurgulu, sert *t*) Latin alfabesinde *t*-sesi olarak alındı; Arap *-ʾ-* (hamza, gırtlak-durması) Latin *i*-sesli ile temsil edildi; Arap *-r* korundu. Aynı yıldız-adı Arabik-orijinli yazımda *Altayr*, *Atair*, *Atayr* gibi varyantlar 13.-16. yy Latin metinlerinde görülür; standartlaşma 17. yy'da *Altair* lehine kesinleşti.       en: |
        As with Vega, the Toledo translators in the Alfonsine Tables (1252-70) compressed the three words of the Arabic phrase *al-nasr al-ṭāʾir* into a single word for practical use. The *al-nasr* (eagle) part was dropped, the remaining *al-ṭāʾir* word passed into the Latin form *Altair* / *Altayr* together with the Arabic *al-* article — unlike Vega, the article was preserved here, as it had been a practical preference for sound-fluency. In the thirteenth-fifteenth-century Latin astronomical texts, the form *Altair* was standardised; Petrus Apianus's *Astronomicum Caesareum* (1551) placed this Latin form as the standard of the star-map.

        Philological detail: the Latin spelling *Altair* is the phonological adaptation of the Arabic *al-ṭāʾir*. The Arabic *ṭ-* (emphatic, hard *t*) was taken as a *t*-sound in the Latin alphabet; the Arabic *-ʾ-* (hamza, glottal-stop) was represented with the Latin *i*-vowel; the Arabic *-r* was preserved. The same star-name, in Arabic-origin writing, shows variants like *Altayr*, *Atair*, *Atayr* in the thirteenth-sixteenth-century Latin texts; standardisation was settled in favour of *Altair* in the seventeenth century. The modern international astronomical-literature standard, the IAU (International Astronomical Union) 2016 official star-name list, defines *Altair* as the canonical name.

      en: |
        The work of carrying the Greek star-list into Arabic at the Baghdad Bayt al-Ḥikma in the ninth-tenth century (translators such as Ḥunayn ibn Isḥāq, Thābit ibn Qurra, al-Ḥajjāj ibn Yūsuf), in bringing the star-catalogue of the Greek Ptolemy's *Almagest* into Arabic, added in parallel to each Ptolemaic-star the pre-Islamic Arab-folk naming. The α-star of Aquila is in Ptolemy's list "Aquilae humerus" (the eagle's shoulder); the same star in the pre-Islamic Arab tradition is *al-nasr al-ṭāʾir* (the flying eagle). The same star came to live in two parallel imagistic-systems.

        In al-Ṣūfī's *Kitāb al-Kawākib al-Thābita* (c. 964), for Altair two picture-descriptions are shown side by side: the shoulder-point of the Greek eagle-figure and the head-point of the Arab flying eagle. This double-image system is, for modern astronomy, historical-archaeological evidence: the Arab astronomers did not abandon their own pre-Islamic visual inheritance, but preferred to synthesise it with the Greek-Hellenistic scientific canon. At al-Battānī's Raqqa-observatory, the position-tables of *al-nasr al-ṭāʾir* were recorded with minute-accuracy; this practical data-infrastructure, together with Vega, became the basic-source of the Toledo transfer.

        Modern comparative history-of-science literature (Paul Kunitzsch 1959, Owen Gingerich 1973) positions Altair as one of the most successful lexical-transfers of the Arab astronomical tradition to Europe: the Arab-folk naming (a zoonym: the flying eagle) passed into the modern language of science and still lives as the canonical-name. By comparison, the Greek-Ptolemaic "eagle's-shoulder" form-description name did not pass; the Arab-folk zoonym did. An instance of the long-term consequences of the aesthetic-practical preferences of word-history.

      en: |
        In the pre-Islamic Arab desert-star tradition, the two bright stars of the Summer Triangle (Vega and Altair) were imaged in two eagle-poses — a dramatic visual-composition of the sky: *al-nasr al-wāqiʿ* (the falling eagle, Vega) closing its wings and swooping on its prey, *al-nasr al-ṭāʾir* (the flying eagle, Altair) flying freely with spread wings. The two stars are observed side by side in the summer sky of the Arabian peninsula, at about 35 degrees' distance; this visual-side-by-side placement strengthens the image of "the two eagles in the same scene".

        Practical use: in the Arabian desert-night navigation, Altair, together with Vega, was part of the east-direction-finding axis. At the same time, Altair's position in the sky is close to the equator-line (declination +9°), which allowed it to be seen from every point of the Arabian peninsula (15°N-30°N) — an "always visible" star the desert-guides relied on. A modern geographical-practical analysis: this visual-accessibility feature of Altair enabled the nomadic-Bedouin clans of the ancient period to use it as a standard reference-star not only in caravan direction-finding but also in season-tracking.

        Oral tradition compilations: in the eighth-ninth century, Arab-Abbasid philologists' Bedouin-poetry compilations (al-Aṣmaʿī, Ibn al-Aʿrābī, and so on) systematically recorded the star-images of pre-Islamic Arab poetry; Altair (*al-nasr al-ṭāʾir*) and Vega (*al-nasr al-wāqiʿ*) were fixed elements in the summer-night image-catalogue of Arabic *qaṣīda* literature. When al-Ṣūfī prepared his later astronomical works, he combined this oral-literary inheritance with the written-scientific standard.

      en: |
        The eagle-image of the Aquila constellation goes back, in Mesopotamia, to the period of 2000-1000 BC: in Sumerian-Akkadian astronomical tablets this star-region was named with the words *Imhullu* (the scorpion-storm eagle, the star-form of the northeastern wind-god) or *Erū* (the eagle). The Mesopotamian eagle-mythology was transferred to ancient Greece; Greek astronomical-mythology (Eudoxus, 400 BC) recorded two distinct mythological explanations for Aquila: (i) Zeus's eagle that carried Ganymede to Olympus, (ii) the eagle that carried Aphrodite's arrows.

        The name *al-nasr al-ṭāʾir* developed by pre-Islamic Arabia for the same star is, independently of the Mesopotamian-Greek eagle-image, a parallel conceptual birth: the same region of the sky was imaged with the eagle-form by three distinct cultural-practical traditions. This parallel-imagery is a rare cultural-phenomenon: the same star-triangle (Lyra-Aquila-Cygnus) is named with the bird-image from three distinct cultural sources (Mesopotamian eagle, Greco-Roman Aquila + Lyra + Cygnus, Arab three-eagle). For modern science an observation: the same visual-pattern (the summer-triangle-position of three bright stars) triggered similar conceptual-responses in different cultures.

        The linguistic-archaeological inheritance carried by the modern word *Altair*: two thousand years of Mesopotamian eagle-star tradition + three thousand years of Greco-Latin Aquila-constellation tradition + a thousand five hundred years of Arab desert-flying-eagle tradition. Three traditions are found in one modern word; the point that it is the Arab one that passed into the European-Anglo language of science is a living instance of the "lost and continued" pattern of modern comparative history of science.
