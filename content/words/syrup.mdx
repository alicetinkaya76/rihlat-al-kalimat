literalMeaning:
  tr: "*birebir anlamı:* içecek, içilen şey. Arapça *sharāb* (شَراب) kök olarak *ş-r-b* (şarbe — içmek) fiilinden; her türlü içecek — su, süt, şarap, meyve suyu, ilaç şurubu — *sharāb* olabilir. Ortaçağ Arap tıbbı ise sözcüğü dar bir teknik anlamda kullandı: *bal veya şekerle koyulaştırılmış ilaç-içeceği*. Bu tıbbi *sharāb* Latinceye *syrupus / siropus* olarak girdi (12.-13. yy Salerno-Constantinus zinciri); oradan Fransızca *sirop*, İngilizce *syrup* — her dilde *ilaç şurubu* sicilinde yerleşik, sonra modern dönemde *meyve şurubu* sicilini de kazandı. **Not:** aynı Arapça *sharāb* kökü farklı bir koridordan *sherbet* olarak da Avrupa'ya geçti (bkz. <em>sherbet</em>); bir kelime, iki kanal, iki anlam-evrimi."
  en: "*literally:* a drink, the thing drunk. Arabic *sharāb* (شَراب), from the root *sh-r-b* (*shariba*, to drink), can denote any beverage — water, milk, wine, fruit juice, medicinal draught. Medieval Arab medicine, however, used the word in a narrower technical sense: *a medicinal drink thickened with honey or sugar*. This medicinal *sharāb* entered Latin as *syrupus / siropus* (the twelfth- and thirteenth-century Salerno-Constantinus chain); from there French *sirop*, English *syrup* — settled in every language in the medicinal register, and only in the modern period extended to the fruit-syrup register. **Note:** the same Arabic root reached Europe through a different corridor as *sherbet* (see <em>sherbet</em>); one word, two channels, two evolutions of sense."
  ar: "*حرفيًّا:* المَشروب، الشَّيءُ المُتَناوَلُ شُرباً. *شَراب* العربيّةُ، من جَذرِ *ش-ر-ب* (*شَرِبَ*)، يُسَمّي كُلَّ مَشروب — ماءً وَلَبَناً وَخَمراً وَعَصيراً وَجُرعةً عِلاجيّة. أَمّا الطِّبُّ العَرَبيُّ الوُسطى فاستَخدَمَ الكلمةَ في مَعنىً تَقنيٍّ أَضيَق: *مَشروبٌ عِلاجيٌّ مُكَثَّفٌ بِعَسَلٍ أو سُكَّر*. وَدَخَلَ هذا *الشَّرابُ* الطِّبِّيُّ اللاتينيّةَ صِيغةَ *syrupus / siropus* (سِلسِلةُ سالِرنو-قُسطَنطين في القَرنَين الثاني عَشَر والثالثَ عَشَر)؛ ومنها *sirop* الفرنسيّةُ، و*syrup* الإنكليزيّةُ — ثَبَتَت في كُلِّ لُغةٍ في السِّجِلِّ العِلاجيِّ، ولم تَمتَدَّ إلى سِجِلِّ شَرابِ الفَواكِهِ إلّا في العَصرِ الحَديث. **مُلاحَظة:** بَلَغَ الجَذرُ العَرَبيُّ نفسُه أوروبّا عَبرَ مَمَرٍّ آخَرَ بصيغةِ *sherbet* (انظر <em>sherbet</em>)؛ كَلِمةٌ واحدة، مَمَرّان، تَطَوُّرانِ في المَعنى."

      en: |
        The *Antidotarium Nicolai*, compiled at Salerno in the mid-twelfth century and present in every European pharmacy from the thirteenth century onward, either in manuscript or in print, standardised hundreds of *syrupus* recipes: *syrupus de absinthio* (wormwood), *syrupus violarum* (violet), *syrupus rosaceus* (rose), *syrupus de papavere* (poppy, an analgesic). In the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries every major European city-state issued its own *antidotarium* (Florence, Cologne, Augsburg, Nuremberg, Lyon), but always took the terminology of the *Nicolai* as its base. In that process the word passed from page to shelf: a 1380 inventory of Florentine apothecaries lists *syropi* in dozens of sub-categories. Even the Humanist medicine of the fifteenth century, with Manuel Chrysoloras's Greek influence, did not drop the Arabicising label *syrupus*; the word was not buried in the Greek-origin (Galenic) layer of the medical lexicon — it kept its own original channel.

        Arab medicine constituted *al-ṣaydala* (pharmacy) as a separate profession in ninth-century Baghdad; the first state *ṣaydala* (state pharmacy) was founded under the Abbasid caliph al-Manṣūr (754-775). Pharmaceutical texts handled *sharāb* as a precise technical sub-category: a *sharāb* is a thick, sweetened, medicinal liquid prescribed by a physician and prepared by a pharmacist. Yūḥannā ibn Māsawayh, Ḥunayn ibn Isḥāq (in his Arabic Galen-Hippocrates), Ibn Sīnā's *Qānūn* (Book V on materia medica and pharmacy), Ibn Buṭlān, Sābūr ibn Sahl's *al-Aqrābādhīn* (a ninth-century pharmacy book) — all of them used the word in the same narrow technical sense. The companion vocabulary moved in the same channel: *rubb* (cooked-down fruit essence), *julāb* (rose-water drink), *maʿjūn* (electuary), *laʿūq* (a licked-sweet medicine). The whole Arab pharmacy workshop would transit to Salerno as a single package.

      en: |
        Classical Arabic *sharāb* covered every kind of drink — *al-māʾ sharāb* (water is a drink), *al-laban sharāb* (milk is a drink), *al-khamr sharāb* (wine is a drink). In the Qurʾān, Sūrat Ṣād (38:42), the verse promising Job water — *hādhā maghtasalun bāridun wa sharābun* (this is a cool washing-place and a drink) — uses *sharāb* for plain drinking water. The Semitic cognates share the same root *sh-r-b*: Hebrew *shātāh* (with a phonetic closure), Aramaic *shtāh*, Akkadian *shatū* — all "to drink". The medicinal sense of *sharāb* was technically narrowed in the ninth-century Baghdad pharmacy workshop; that narrowing is the critical step in the word's world journey. The cognate that reached the Ottoman court as *sharbat* (a cold fruit drink) did not preserve the strict medicinal sense; it settled into the social register of courtly protocol. *Sharāb* and *sharbat* are two historical tracks of the same word: one medicalised (and then Latinised), the other diplomatic-and-table (and then Ottoman-to-European). One word, two histories.
