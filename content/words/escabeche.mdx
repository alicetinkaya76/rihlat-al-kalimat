      en: |
        The Sephardic expulsion of 1492 carried *escabeche* into the Ottoman Mediterranean and North Africa; in the kitchens of Salonica, Izmir, Tangier and Tetuán it has been conservatively preserved into the present, as a religiously important cold dish for Friday night. In the same period the Spanish *conquista* took the dish to the New World: in Peru, the Creole cuisine of Lima rebuilt *escabeche* around the local yellow chilli (*ají amarillo*); in Mexico, the local *escabeche de chiles* reshaped the technique for a different palate. In the Philippines the Manila Galleon (1565-1815) carried the dish — in Filipino, *escabecheng isda* — across the Pacific; the local palate added pineapple and sugar, but the word stayed Arabic. The same *sikbāj* recipe crossed three imperial passages within a generation: Ottoman, Spanish, and Pacific-Spanish. In every place it arrived under the same name.

      en: |
        The anonymous Catalan cookbook <em>Llibre de Sent Soví</em> (c. 1324) is an early attestation of *escabetx* in written Romance, with recipes such as *anguila escabetxada* among its fish entries. Late fourteenth- and fifteenth-century Castilian culinary writing (Ruperto de Nola's <em>Llibre del Coch</em>, printed in the 1520s but transmitting an earlier Castilian tradition) settled <em>escabeche</em> in place as a technique-category: a vinegar-boiled, cold-served marinade for fish or meat. After the *reconquista* the generation that opened these urban kitchens was largely Mozarab (Arabic-speaking Christian) and Mudejar (Muslim under Christian rule) artisans, direct carriers of the older Andalusian culinary tradition. The word entered without phonetic detour from its Arabic source: *as-sikbāj* → *escabetx / escabeche*, the Arabic definite article (here in its sun-letter form *as-*) fused into the Romance lexicon as a first syllable.

        The two great manuscripts of Andalusian cuisine — the Anonymous Andalusian Cookbook (<em>Kitāb al-Ṭabīj fī al-Maghrib wa-l-Andalus</em>, anonymous, thirteenth century) and Ibn Razīn al-Tujibī's <em>Fudālat al-Khiwān</em> (c. 1244) — carry dozens of variants of *sikbāj*: fish sikbāj, chicken sikbāj, lamb sikbāj, duck sikbāj. The recipes share a common core: meat or fish is first fried, then steeped in a mixture of vinegar, honey or sugar, saffron, onion and spices, and cooled before serving. In al-Andalus the dish had both an aristocratic logic (urbane, well-resourced) and a culinary-economic one (vinegar preserves, the dish keeps). *Sikbāj* takes up perhaps five of every hundred recipes in these manuscripts, always under the same heading. Three centuries of dishing in Islamic Iberia register, in the increasing weight of one word in the lexicon, an increasing weight of practice.

        The <em>Kitāb al-Ṭabīkh</em> of Ibn Sayyār al-Warrāq (Baghdad, c. 950) — one of the earliest Arabic cookbooks to survive — lists *sikbāj* among the favourite dishes of the caliph al-Maʾmūn (d. 833); al-Baghdādī's cookbook of the same title (1226) confirms that a generation later the dish was still being made in the Baghdad court. The word is transparent in its parts: Old Persian <em>sik / sikā</em> "vinegar" + the Arabic culinary suffix <em>-aj</em>. In culinary onomastics this is a classical example of Perso-Arabic fusion (the same period brought its siblings <em>fālūdaj</em>, <em>khabīṣ</em>, <em>jūdhāb</em>): a Persian culinary repertoire crossed into Arabic in the Abbasid court, recipe and name together. Through the persistence of this repertoire, the caliph's table was set across three hundred years from Baghdad to Córdoba, and across five hundred years from Baghdad to Lima. The word remained the same word.
