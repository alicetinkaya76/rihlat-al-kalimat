        Among the Toledo translations of Gerard of Cremona between 1150 and 1187, alchemical texts attributed to Jābir ibn Ḥayyān like *Liber de septuaginta* (The Book of the Seventy) and *Liber misericordiae* (The Book of Mercy) were carried into Latin; in these texts the word *al-būraq* was consistently Latinised as *borax* / *boras* (preserving the *al-* article like kiosk, alembic, alkali). The technological-secret of Murano glass-production became European general-knowledge over the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries (Antonio Neri's *L'Arte Vetraria* of 1612 first opened the technology's written documentation to the public). In this process, the word *borace* / *borax* settled in the European craft-lingua franca as a technical standard-term — a precursor to modern chemistry.

Modern European languages dropped *tincal* and only *borax* survived; but in the twelfth-seventeenth-century range both namings were used in parallel.

      en: |
        The natural formation of borax (sodium tetraborate decahydrate) occurs on Earth only in a few desert-lake environments: the high-altitude salt-lakes of the Tibetan plateau (the Changtang basin), the desert-lakes of the Iranian-Persian plateau (Dasht-e Kavir, Dasht-e Lut), the Anatolian volcanic-plateau (Eskişehir-Kütahya), and the Death Valley desert-beds of California. Persian borax (Persian *būrah*), in turn, was carried directly from the Persian plateau to Baghdad-Damascus-Cairo.

        The ancient Greco-Roman world knew borax as a luxury-salt of Persian-Indian origin; in Pliny the Elder's *Naturalis Historia* (AD 77), the term *chrysocolla* appears as the name of a borax-type salt that goldsmiths used. But the Greco-Roman world did not name this salt with the specific word *būrah* / *būraq* — the starting-point of the constitution of the technical standard of the Perso-Arab-Islamic world is after the seventh-eighth-century Arab conquest. The modern word *borax* is the long-term name-inheritance, in the European-American language of science, of the ancient Asian desert-lake economy after it had been standardised in Arab-Islamic workshops.

        A historical irony: the white crystal salt extracted from the Tibetan/Persian/Indian desert-lake circulated, over the three-thousand-year trade-chain, in the forms *būraq* (Arabic), *borax* (Latin), *borace* (Italian), *bourax* (Old French), *Borax* (German); in Mendeleev's periodic table as the symbol B, after Davy and Gay-Lussac had isolated it in elemental purity; and finally extracted from the deserts of Eskişehir-Balıkesir-Kütahya in twenty-first-century Turkey, becomes the backbone of the modern global detergent and glass industries. The extension of a single Persian desert-salt word to the modern global science-industry system.
