      en: |
        The first mosque in Islam was the simple courtyard the Prophet built beside his own house in Medina after the Hijra (622) — *al-Masjid al-Nabawī* (the Prophet's Mosque). The architectural form is plain: a *qibla* wall, a low roof, palm-trunk columns. The word *masjid* appears twenty-eight times in the Qur'an; the usages *al-Masjid al-Ḥarām* (the Mosque of the Kaʿba in Mecca) and *al-Masjid al-Aqṣā* (the Farther Mosque in Jerusalem). These first three mosques (Mecca, Medina, Jerusalem) are the three holy places of Islam.

        Under the reign of the caliph al-Walīd I (705-715), Islamic architecture underwent a monumental mosque-transformation: the Damascus Great Mosque, the reconstruction of the Prophet's Mosque in Medina, the monumental construction of the al-Aqṣā in Jerusalem. In this period the word *masjid* is used in both senses — small local prayer-place and great congregational mosque; by the ninth century the term *masjid jāmiʿ* (the gathering-mosque, the congregational mosque) had differentiated for the great congregational mosques. This Arab architectural-terminological development became the basis for the word sent on to al-Andalus.

      en: |
        The Semitic verb root *s-j-d* (سجد) means, in the ancient Semitic languages, "to bow down to the ground, to prostrate, to put one's forehead to the ground" — a root founded on a physical gesture. Aramaic/Syriac *sgd* (to bow in reverence), Hebrew *sgd* (to bow, to prostrate). *Masjid* is formed on the place-noun pattern *mafʿal*: "the place of prostration". In the pre-Islamic Arab world, in Nabataean and Aramaic inscriptions, *msgd* / *msgdʾ* "a holy place, a place of prostration" appears; it is attested in the inscriptions of Petra (the Nabataean capital), in first-century-CE documents.

        That is, the word *masjid* was used as a name for a holy place in the Arab-Semitic geography even before Islam; it is the holy-place naming of the pre-Islamic Arabian polytheistic religion, and of Christian-Jewish religious communities. Islam took this word over from its existing use and specialised it as the name of a new architectural-religious institution. The form *mosque* of the word in modern European English was born out of a seventeen-hundred-year passage from this ancient Semitic root — a word semantically unchanged (under the physical-gestural meaning "to bow to the ground"), but passing through a great technological transformation in architectural-institutional terms.

      en: |
        In the pre-Islamic Arab religious tradition, the Kaʿba in Mecca (الكعبة, "the cube" — the cubic stone-building) was the centre of a pagan pantheon and at the same time the centre of pilgrimage. The emergence of Islam was a process of re-meaning of this existing holy-place infrastructure: the Kaʿba (to be cleansed of the idols) was renamed for the central-monotheistic God of Islam; *al-Masjid al-Ḥarām* (the Sacred Mosque) became the colonnaded square encircling this Kaʿba. The mosque-concept of Islam is the Abrahamic-monotheistic reinterpretation of this pre-Islamic holy-place infrastructure.

        That is, behind the modern word *mosque* lies the ancient holy-place history of the Arabian peninsula. The Jewish synagogue, the Christian church and the Islamic mosque — the prayer-places of the three Abrahamic religions — went through a parallel architectural-institutional evolution: all are extensions of the pre-Abrahamic ancient Near-Eastern holy-place type; all have a common Semitic lexical root (Jewish *bēt knesset*, "the house of gathering"; Christian *ekklēsia*, "the called-out community"; Islamic *masjid*, "the place of prostration"). The word mosque is, in modern Europe, the architectural-terminological representative of the Islamic-Arab branch of this trio.
