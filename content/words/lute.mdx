  en: "*literally:* a piece of wood, a small branch. Arabic *al-ʿūd* (العُود) in its classical sense names *a piece of wood* or *a branch*; as the name of the instrument it derives from the wooden body, from the *barbat* of Mesopotamian origin that took the name *ʿūd* in Arab culture. Andalusian Arabic *al-ʿūd* crossed into Spanish as *laúd* (twelfth- and thirteenth-century) — the *al-* prefix preserved, the signature of the Andalusian line (cf. *azafrán, almacén, alcázar*). Thence Old Provençal *laüt*, Old French *leüt / lëut*, Italian *liuto*, German *Laute*, Dutch *luit*, English *lute* (Middle English, c. 1361, the *Pearl* manuscript). Turkish received the word along two lines: through Anatolian Greek *laoúto* (λαούτο) as *lavta*, and the Arabic *ʿūd* directly as *ud* — Turkish holds **two indirect channels** of one word in two forms. The classical *lute* became the central instrument of the European Renaissance and Baroque; the *oud* remains the spine of the Arab, Turkish, Greek, Armenian and Syriac traditional music. **The same piece of wood, the histories of two continents' music.**"

      en: |
        The European Renaissance (c. 1450-1600) placed the lute at the head of the list of *principal instruments* from Cesare Tinctoris's *De inventione et usu musicae* (c. 1487) onward. Petrucci's Venetian press produced the first printed lute tablature in 1507 (*Intabulatura de lauto, libro primo*); the sixteenth-century repertoires of Italy (Francesco da Milano), Spain (Luis Milán, parallel to the lute via the *vihuela*), Germany (Hans Newsidler) and England (John Dowland) followed. In the Baroque, the French royal court (Denis Gaultier, Charles Mouton, Robert de Visée — at the court of Louis XIV) and the German lute tradition (Silvius Leopold Weiss, personally acquainted with Bach) installed the lute as an **aristocratic court instrument**. The mid-seventeenth-century French *baroque lute* of eleven, twelve and thirteen courses; in Weiss's hands in Germany, a thirteen-course tonal architecture. Bach's *Lautenwerk* (lute-keyboard) works and the Suite in E minor BWV 996 stand at the summit of the lute repertoire. The European career of the word entered decline in the 1750s (the orchestra-centred aesthetic of the Classical period marginalised the lute in one or two generations); the modern early-music revival begins in the 1970s. **Four hundred years at the centre of court, two hundred years of silence, fifty years of academic revival.**

      en: |
        *Al-ʿūd* crossed from al-Andalus into Christian Europe through two main channels: (a) the court and street music of southern Spain (Toledo, Seville, Valencia), in which the Reconquista left the same instrument in Christian hands — the *Cantigas de Santa María* miniatures of Alfonso X "the Wise" (r. 1252-1284) depict Muslim and Christian lute players side by side; (b) the Mediterranean trade-and-court network — the Provençal-Languedoc troubadours, the Norman court of Sicily, the patrician houses of Florence, Venice and Genoa — adopted the lute in parallel from the twelfth and thirteenth centuries. The thirteenth-century Provençal poet Guiraut Riquier names the *laüt*. The German *Minnesang* tradition and the Hungarian *kobzos* repertoire took the instrument along parallel arms. The European spread of the word **goes together with the spread of the instrument**: the object first, then the word; both from a single point of origin (Cordoba), along the same vector (al-Andalus → northern Spain → southern France → Italy → central Europe). The earliest attestation of *lute* in English is the 1361 *Pearl* poem; Chaucer in the 1380s, *Knight's Tale*: "in many tymes had been at lat. lūt'". The connecting of **two courts** by a single man. *ʿŪd* moved to the centre of Andalusian music; Ziryāb added a fifth string to the instrument (the oud, hitherto four-stringed, was raised to five). The change altered the instrument's acoustic output and the technical capacity of the music theory.       en: |
        The classical Arabic root *ʿ-w-d* has the core sense "to return"; *ʿūd* from the same root is "a piece of wood, a branch, a stick" — wood that, bent, returns upon itself. In pre-Islamic poetry *ʿūd* generally meant *wood* (not the instrument; a very general piece of timber). The instrument as a musical object is of **Mesopotamian origin**: Sumerian *gish-gu* (wood-board), Akkadian *parātu / baratu*, Persian *barbat / barbut* — a pear-shaped stringed instrument already in use in pre-Islamic Arabia. With the coming of Islam, Arabic named the instrument by its own word: from its wooden body, *al-ʿūd*, meaning **the instrument is a piece of wood**. This *naming decision* looks minor at first; but the fact that the word came, ten centuries later, to be the word for the central instrument of Europe shows the decision's force. The word *barbat* disappeared in the Arab world within a century — it gave its place to *al-ʿūd*. When *al-ʿūd* then passed into Europe, *barbat* was never recalled: European musicology, until the 1900s, was unaware of this Sasanian-origin name. **When a word changes the name of an instrument, the whole earlier history of the instrument can be lost beneath a single word.**
