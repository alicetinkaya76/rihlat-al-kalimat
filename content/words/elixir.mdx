      en: |
        Philippus Theophrastus von Hohenheim (1493-1541), the Swiss physician known by the name *Paracelsus*, is the single individual who carried the term *al-iksīr* into early modern European pharmacy. In his *Archidoxis Magica* and *Liber Paramirum* (in the 1530s), he takes *al-iksīr* from the Jābir-Geber channel but treats it as equivalent to the Greek Aristotelian concept of *quintessence* — the fifth essence beyond the four elements. For Paracelsus there is for every disease a specific *elixir*: a mixture of the distilled essences of particular minerals and plants. This approach is the foundation of modern *iatrochemistry* (medicine grounded in chemistry) — a challenge to Galenic four-humours medicine.

        In the seventeenth century Robert Boyle (1627-1691) and Hermann Boerhaave (1668-1738) made the concept of the *elixir* a technical category of the pharmacopoeia: an *elixir* — *a pharmaceutical form presenting one or more active substances in a sweetened alcoholic solution*. This definition passes unchanged into the First Official French Pharmacopoeia of 1789 and the First American Pharmacopoeia of 1820. At this point the mystical semantic layers of the Jabirian corpus (immortality, the making of gold) fall away; what remains is the *form*.

      en: |
        The passage of the Jabirian corpus into Latin happens in two waves. The first wave is the twelfth-century Toledo workshop (Gerard of Cremona and his circle) — works by Jābir such as *Liber Misericordiae* and *Liber Septuaginta* enter Latin; the term *al-iksīr* is first recorded in writing here in the form *elixir*. The second wave is the thirteenth-fourteenth-century pseudo-Geber corpus (in particular the *Summa Perfectionis Magisterii*, c. 1280, attributed to an Italian Franciscan named Paolo of Taranto but written in the Jabirian manner) — this work becomes the basic textbook of all medieval European alchemy and for two centuries continues to feed the concept of *elixir* as a technical term.

        In thirteenth-century Latin writers such as Roger Bacon, Albertus Magnus and Arnaldus de Villa Nova, *elixir* settles as a systematic category. An important move: the Latin writers equate *al-iksīr* with the Greek Aristotelian concept of *quintessence* (the fifth essence, beyond the four elements); this equation forces Jābir's original atomistic theory into Greek elemental theory. In his 2006 *Atoms and Alchemy*, the modern historian of science William Newman: this equation is *not a translation error but a conscious theoretical move*; by articulating Jābir to Aristotle, the Latin alchemists make sure the concept receives *the approval of the Greek philosophers*. Without this move Paracelsus could not have carried *elixir* into pharmacy in the 1530s.

        In Chaucer's *Canon's Yeoman's Tale* (in the *Canterbury Tales*, written around 1386), the word enters the written record of English in the speech of an alchemist's servant: *elixir* is the alchemist's ultimate target-powder, ever sought and never found.       en: |
        The roughly six hundred treatises attributed to Jābir ibn Ḥayyān (c. 721-815) and making up the *Jabirian corpus* are in fact the joint product of various authors over the eighth to tenth centuries; only a part is the work of the historical Jābir (Holmyard's thesis of 1923, Kraus's modern critical study of 1942-1943). This corpus settles *al-iksīr* as a systematic technical term: a *catalytic essence* that can convert one substance into another by correcting the balance among four qualities (hot/cold/dry/moist).

        Etymologically *al-iksīr* comes from Greek *xērion* (dry powder), and enters Arabic in the ninth century from the Syriac-mediated translations of the Galenic medical corpus (the translation circle of Ḥunayn ibn Isḥāq). Galen's *xērion* is a medical powder — applied to a wound, an anodyne. In the Jabirian corpus the word crosses into alchemy and the meaning broadens: *al-iksīr al-aḥmar* (the red elixir, the gold-maker), *al-iksīr al-abyaḍ* (the white elixir, the silver-maker), *al-iksīr al-aʿẓam* (the great elixir, the elixir of immortality). A three-layered system of aims: *transmutation* (the change of metals into gold), *purification* (the cleansing of a substance), and *immortality* (the extension of human life). In his 2006 *Atoms and Alchemy*, the modern historian of science William Newman: these three aims are not *conceptually* independent; they are three distinct applications of the same understanding of a *catalytic essence*. *al-iksīr* is the theoretical kernel of the Jabirian corpus.

        The Greek *xērion* (ξήριον, "the dry one") is originally a technical term in the medical corpus of Galen (129-216 CE): a medicine in the form of a dry powder applied to a wound, in particular a haemostatic (*styptic*). In a work of more than 1500 pages, Galen uses the word *xērion* hundreds of times — for instance *xērion lykōn* (wolf-powder), *xērion eutonon* (strong-powder). This word was part of the technical vocabulary of the Hippocratic-Galenic medical tradition at Alexandria.

        The academy at Gundishapur of the Sasanian Empire (in the fifth to seventh centuries) translated the Greek medical corpus into Syriac — here *xērion* is transliterated as Syriac *iksīrā*. After the Sasanian-Byzantine wars, with the passage of Gundishapur into the Muslim world in the mid-seventh century, the Syriac medical corpus became the first medical source of the Arab world. In ninth-century Baghdad at Caliph al-Maʾmūn's *Bayt al-Ḥikma*, Ḥunayn ibn Isḥāq (809-873) and his son Isḥāq ibn Ḥunayn translated the entire Galenic corpus into Arabic — Ḥunayn's method was to pass through the Syriac intermediate stage; *iksīrā* is taken directly into Arabic as *iksīr*, even with the article *al-iksīr*.

This passage is also the paradigmatic example of how the *Greek-Arab transmission of science* works — for the correct use of the word, not a bilingual but a *trilingual* conceptual circulation is required.

        كَلِمةُ *xērion* اليونانيّةُ (ξήριον، "الجافُّ") مُصطَلَحٌ تَقنيٌّ في الأَصلِ في الذَّخيرةِ الطِّبّيّةِ لجالينوسَ (129-216م): دَواءٌ على شَكلِ مَسحوقٍ جافٍّ يُطَبَّقُ على جُرحٍ، خاصّةً وَقّافُ نَزفٍ (*styptic*). في عَمَلٍ يَزيدُ على 1500 صَفحة، يَستَخدِمُ جالينوسُ كَلِمةَ *xērion* مِئاتٍ من المَرّات — مَثَلًا *xērion lykōn* (مَسحوقُ الذِّئب)، *xērion eutonon* (المَسحوقُ القَويُّ). كانتْ هذه الكَلِمةُ جُزءًا من المُفرَداتِ التَّقنيّةِ لِلتَّقليدِ الطِّبّيِّ الإبقراطيِّ-الجالينيِّ في الإسكَندَريّة.

        تَرجَمَتْ أَكاديميّةُ جُندِيشاپور في الإمبراطوريّةِ السّاسانيّةِ (في القُرونِ الخامِسِ إلى السّابعِ) الذَّخيرةَ الطِّبّيّةَ اليونانيّةَ إلى السُّريانيّةِ — هُنا تُنقَلُ *xērion* صَوتيًّا إلى السُّريانيّةِ *إِكسيرا*. بعدَ الحُروبِ السّاسانيّةِ-البيزَنطيّةِ، مع انتِقالِ جُندِيشاپور إلى العالَمِ الإسلاميِّ في مُنتَصَفِ القَرنِ السّابِعِ، صارَتِ الذَّخيرةُ الطِّبّيّةُ السُّريانيّةُ المَصدَرَ الطِّبّيَّ الأَوَّلَ لِلعالَمِ العَرَبيّ. في بَغدادَ في القَرنِ التّاسِعِ في *بَيْتِ الحِكمةِ* لِلخَليفةِ المَأمونِ، تَرجَمَ حُنَيْنُ ابنُ إسحاقَ (809-873م) وَابنُه إسحاقُ بنُ حُنَيْنٍ كُلَّ ذَخيرةِ جالينوسَ إلى العَرَبيّةِ — كانتْ طَريقةُ حُنَيْنٍ المُرورَ بالمَرحَلةِ السُّريانيّةِ الوَسيطةِ؛ يُؤخَذُ *إِكسيرا* مُباشَرةً إلى العَرَبيّةِ بِوَصفِه *إِكسير*، حتّى مع الأَداةِ *الإِكسير*.

        يُفَسِّرُ هذا العُبورُ ثُلاثيُّ اللُّغةِ التَّحَوُّلَ الدِّلاليَّ لِلكَلِمة: *xērion* جالينوسَ مَسحوقٌ طِبّيٌّ، *الإِكسيرُ* جابِرٍ جَوهَرٌ مُحَفِّزٌ سيميائيٌّ؛ لا يُمكِنُ تَفسيرُ الجِسرِ بَيْنَ المَعنَيَيْنِ دونَ المَرحَلةِ السُّريانيّةِ الوَسيطة. هذا العُبورُ أَيضًا هو المِثالُ النَّموذَجيُّ لكَيفِيّةِ عَمَلِ *النَّقلِ العِلميِّ اليونانيِّ-العَرَبيِّ* — للاستِخدامِ الصَّحيحِ لِلكَلِمة، لا يَلزَمُ تَداوُلٌ مَفهوميٌّ ثُنائيُّ اللُّغة بَلْ *ثُلاثيُّ اللُّغة*.
