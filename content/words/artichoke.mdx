Akdeniz çevresinde yaygın yabani *Cynara cardunculus* (kardunculus, deve dikeni, yabani enginar) çok dikenli, çoğunlukla yapraklarının damarları için yetiştirilen "lif sebzesi"dir; klasik Roma'da *cardo*.       en: |
        Ibn Bassāl (Toledo, c. 1080) and Ibn al-ʿAwwām's *Kitāb al-Filāḥa* (Seville, c. 1180) — the two great works of Andalusi agricultural writing — give *al-kharshūf* systematic instruction. The important thing is not the name given to the fruit, nor the technique of cultivation; what is essential is *how the plant itself is created*. The wild *Cynara cardunculus*, common around the Mediterranean (cardoon, wild artichoke, camel-thistle), is a "fibre vegetable" thorned and mostly cultivated for the midribs of its leaves; *cardo* in classical Roman use. The Andalusi gardeners (over the tenth and eleventh centuries) bred a variety of this wild cardunculus with its thorns softened and its flower-head fleshy and fat — *Cynara cardunculus* var. *scolymus*, the ancestor of the modern artichoke. *That is to say, the word* (al-kharshūf) *comes first, and the new form of the plant follows.* Andalusi horticulture creates, by agronomic intervention, a new biological organism, and a new word along with it. The word reuses for the new garden-plant the name it already had for the wild one (both are scaled, bracted).

      en: |
        The word *al-kharshūf* comes from the well-attested Semitic root *kh-r-sh-f* in Arabic; in its verbal form *kharshafa* it means "to be scaled, bracted, having thin leafy peel." Cognates of the same root include *ḥarshafa* "scale" and *karāf* "rind," and it has cognates across nearby Semitic languages. Abū Ḥanīfa al-Dīnawarī's *Kitāb al-Nabāt* (c. 895, the foundational text of the Baghdad-Mesopotamian botanical tradition) recognises the wild cardunculus by the name *al-kharshūf* (the scale-headed thistle); the cultivated variety that is the ancestor of the modern artichoke does not yet exist, and the name belongs only to the wild Mediterranean thistle. Tenth-century Baghdadi medical literature lists this wild plant's medical uses — the juice of its leaves as a cholagogue, the root as a diuretic. In the Arabic record, that is, the word *kharshūf* is first the name of a wild Mediterranean plant; later the Andalusi gardener applies it retroactively to the new garden-variety. *First the name, then the agricultural organism.* This is the curious asymmetry between the philological order of use of the word and the breeding order of the plant.

      en: |
        The plant *Cynara cardunculus* itself, around the Mediterranean, is attested in the archaeobotanical record going back five thousand years; it appears in Egyptian, Etruscan, Persian, Greek, and Roman records as "the wild thistle." In the Hellenistic period *kynara* (the Greek name for the wild artichoke, derived from a mythological nymph Cynara whom a god turned into a thistle) enters Mediterranean cuisine for the sake of its leaf-midribs; in Rome *cardo* continues to be a leaf-midrib vegetable. Pliny, *Naturalis Historia* XX.96, describes the *cynara* or *carduus* of the Roman table as a luxury, and notes that the flower-heads may also be eaten, like a caper-bud, but the practice is uncommon. The eating of the flower-head as a *garden vegetable*, that is, is a discovery of the Andalusi garden — not of the classical Mediterranean. In the classical Mediterranean the plant is, in its name, a wild plant; the Andalusi gardener takes the name of the wild and with it creates a new cultivated variety. *First the wild plant exists, then its name exists, then last the cultivable garden-variety.* A three-layered chronological order.
