      en: |
        Late Latin *capulum* (the rope-head, the knot at the end of a rope) and *capulus* (a handle, a hilt) come from the classical Latin root *caput* (head); they were used in the Latin maritime vocabulary of the sixth-to-ninth century in the senses of rope-end / thick-rope. Provençal *cable* (twelfth century) and Italian *capo* / *capa* appear to be derived from this Latin root. But in the twelfth-and-thirteenth-century Mediterranean lingua franca, the Arabic word *ḥabl* (rope) was a fundamental terminological unit in the passage of Arab maritime technology to Europe — the word-backbone of Arab-Mediterranean rope-engineering (modular braided ropes, multiple-strand weaving techniques).

        The debate in philology is open: Kahane & Kahane (1958) argue that Old French *cable* / *chable* was born as a result of the interaction, in the Mediterranean lingua franca, of Latin *capulum* and Arabic *ḥabl*. According to some sources the word is entirely Latin-sourced, with no Arab influence; but the word spread through the lingua-franca vocabulary of the Mediterranean trader on the terminological template of Arab rope-technology. The ancestor of modern *cable* is a mixture of a word-form coming from the Latin vocabulary and a semantic content originating in the Arab technology.

      en: |
        Arab navigation in the ninth-to-thirteenth century was the practically-dominant power on the Mediterranean, the Red Sea and the Indian Ocean; Arab navigator-engineering rope-production techniques (palm-fibre lichening, coconut-husk fibre (*qinbar*), the linen-cannabin blend, the cotton-silk braid) became the technical standard for sea-routes from China to Spain. *Ḥabl* (الحَبل) is the basic term of the technical vocabulary of Arab navigation — the ship's anchor-rope, the sail-threads, the cargo-tying rope, the tension-winch ropes. It was inherited from the pre-Islamic Arabian maritime tradition.

        The Arab sea-craft and their rope-configurations were openly under the direct observation of the twelfth-to-fourteenth-century Mediterranean trader. The rope-workshops of Genoa, Pisa and Venice were the practical copyists of Arab-Mediterranean rope-techniques; the technical terminology passed into the lingua franca through that practical-transfer process. Beneath the modern word *cable* lies this Arab-Mediterranean rope-engineering craft-tradition; even if the word-form is Latin-Romance, the technical-practical content the word carries is the passage of the Arab rope-engineering tradition to Europe.
