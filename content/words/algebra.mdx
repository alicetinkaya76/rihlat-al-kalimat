      en: |
        Cardano's *Ars Magna* (1545) solved the general cubic and flirted, half-willingly, with an object that had no name yet — what would later be called the imaginary number. Bombelli, a few years on (1572), turned that flirtation into consistent arithmetic. Viète, in his *In artem analyticem isagoge* (1591), made a decision that would prove decisive: known and unknown quantities should be written as letters; arithmetic now operates not on numbers but on *structure*. He named the practice: *logistica speciosa*. Descartes' *La Géométrie* (1637) fixed the conventions still in use — a, b, c for knowns, x, y, z for unknowns, modern exponent notation. By the time Newton's *Arithmetica universalis* appeared in 1707, the discipline was being addressed by its own name: *algebra*.

        The quiet narrative beneath this shift is that the word's referent had changed. The *al-jabr* of Baghdad named two procedures performed by hand on the page. The *algebra* of Renaissance Europe named the manipulation itself, the act of turning unknowns represented by letters. Beneath the same word, numbers had taken flight; what remained was symbolic structure.

Its purposes were not theoretical but *pragmatic*: partitioning inheritances, settling commerce, solving geometric problems for the day's practitioners. The method, however, was new. It reduced every equation to one of six canonical forms, and gave each form a standard procedure of solution. The key procedures were two. *Al-jabr*: moving a subtracted term from one side of an equation to the other as an addition — filling in what the page presents as missing, *restoring what was broken*. *Al-muqābala*: setting like terms on the two sides against each other and reducing them away.

        The metaphor is surgical. The Arabic verb *jabara* (جَبَرَ) means "to set a broken bone"; in classical Arab society, *al-mujabbir* (المُجبِّر) was a profession — a bone-setter. Al-Khwārizmī attached this surgical resonance to the act of repairing an equation's broken balance. The book's title, with the two procedures standing side by side, would eight centuries later cross into Latin; the title would detach and become the name of the subject. *Al-muqābala* would be lost on the road, but *al-jabr* — *algebra* — would survive in every language it crossed into.

      en: |
        Brahmagupta's *Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta* (628 CE), apart from its decimal arithmetic, sketches an early structural theory of equations. Through the following seven centuries, Indian mathematics developed *bījagaṇita* (equation-arithmetic) as a domain in its own right; Bhāskara II's *Bījagaṇita* of 1150 was one of its peaks. Where Europe was still struggling with negative numbers, with zero, with irrationals, India was already at ease.

        Further back — three centuries earlier — in Alexandria, Diophantus wrote his *Arithmetica* (ca. 250 CE). His method was idiosyncratic, almost private — its own notations, its own tricks — but it carried into Greek mathematics the idea of computing with unknowns. Further back still, two millennia back, Babylonian tablets such as YBC 6967 were solving quadratic problems by standard procedures (not as words, but as flows of computation). Three streams converged on a single Baghdad desk — Indian, Greek-Hellenistic, Mesopotamian — and al-Khwārizmī simply chose to call the act *the restoration of the broken*. The name stuck.
