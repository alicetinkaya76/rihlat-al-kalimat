      en: |

      en: |
        The two ends of the Romance line diverge: Spanish *berenjena* swallows the leading *al-* article, Catalan *albergínia* keeps it. Fourteenth-century Catalan culinary writing (Ramon de Cabrera, later the *Llibre del Coch*, 1520) describes the aubergine in various recipes; in the Castilian of the same period it appears, no longer with hesitation, at the table. Sixteenth-century Italian carries two competing forms: *melangiana* in Florence and *petonciano* in Naples — the first a deformation of Andalusi Arabic (in particular, *mela-* tries to interpret the head of the word as the Italian for "apple," in folk etymology), the second from a different Mediterranean form. The France of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries takes the Catalan form as *aubergine*; the head *aube-* is Provençal in influence, the ending *-gine* is the Catalan terminus (from Latin *-iana*). English *aubergine* is recorded in 1794 in the kitchen-journal of the wife of George III (Charlotte); *eggplant* in American English from the same period, first in eighteenth-century New England, named after the egg-shaped white varieties. *Two separate names of the same plant accepted into two separate continents.*

      en: |
        Ibn Wāfid of Toledo (d. 1075) and Ibn al-ʿAwwām's *Kitāb al-Filāḥa* give *al-bādhinjān* both an agronomic and a medical section; the form of the word consistent in Andalusi Arabic is *al-bādhinjān* (the trailing *-jān* is the Arabic shape of the Persian suffix). Andalusi medicine reformulates the leaves' and fruit's "wind-dispelling" property (the meaning of the original Sanskrit name) within the Galenic frame as a plant of cold-and-dry temperament: the agronomic literature gives detailed instructions for soaking the fruit in olive oil or lemon, and for how to remove its bitterness (the direct ancestor of modern aubergine cookery). An important point: this plant comes to al-Andalus by the Persia-Baghdad axis, brought to al-Andalus from where the Persian court could not bring it to Rome; *the Indian culinary element on the Baghdad court's table passes into Latin Europe by way of the Andalusi courtyard-garden.* It is not a Roman vegetable; it is unknown in Rome, absent from classical Latin botany. Arabic and Persian literature bring it to Europe; this is one of the clearest cases of Andalusi horticulture.

      en: |
        The Sanskrit medical literature — the Charaka Saṃhitā (in strata earlier than c. 100 AD) and the Suśruta Saṃhitā — describes a kitchen plant by the name *vātiṅgaṇa* (वातिङ्गण): *vāta* (wind) + *iṅga* (driving away) + *ṇa* (nominal suffix). "The wind-dispelling plant" — in Ayurvedic temperament theory, a plant of warming and lightening property that remedies imbalances of the *vāta* (wind-ether dosha) in the body. This *vātiṅgaṇa* is exactly the modern *Solanum melongena*; the archaeobotanical and philological correlation is clear. Botanically the plant is endemic to India, bred up in north-western India (the modern Gujarat-Sindh region) from its wild ancestor *Solanum incanum*. *Vāta-dispelling* is a real Ayurvedic clinical observation; but the same plant becomes, at the Persian court, a Galenic plant of "cold and dry" temperament, and in the Andalusi physician a culinary ingredient "whose bitterness is corrected with olive oil." Three different medical-culinary interpretations of one plant within three different systems of thought — Ayurveda, Galenic, modern Mediterranean cuisine. The word's passage from Sanskrit to Middle-Persian Pahlavi: *vātiṅgaṇa* > *bātingān* > *bādingān* — phonemic change in *vā-*, vowel-harmony in *-iṅga-*, single-consonantal *-ṇa* > *-an*. The three linguistic nodes of three medical worlds fossilise in a single word.
