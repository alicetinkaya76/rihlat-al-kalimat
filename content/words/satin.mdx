      en: |
        Quanzhou (Chinese 泉州) is a port city in the Fujian province of south-eastern China. Under the Song dynasty (tenth-thirteenth centuries) and the Yuan dynasty (thirteenth-fourteenth), the principal China-side port of Indian Ocean commerce. From the eleventh century, Arab, Persian, Indian and Jewish traders established colonies there; the city's Arab-Muslim community had several mosques (the Qingjing Mosque of Quanzhou, built in 1009, still standing). The Arabic source of the word: Arab traders first knew the city of Quanzhou by the Chinese adjective *cí tóng* (刺桐, the red-flowering tree, *Erythrina*); *cí tóng* is named in Arabic as *zaytūn* (homophonic with the Arabic for olive-tree).

        The Arab-Chinese silk commerce shows continuity: in the eighth and ninth centuries the Tang period (Canton/Guangzhou the principal port); in the eleventh-to-thirteenth, the Song-Yuan period (Quanzhou/Zaytun the principal port). In Ibn Baṭṭūṭa's *Riḥla* (c. 1355) he visits Zaytun personally and describes it after his trip to Egypt: "In the port of the city, on one occasion, hundreds of great ships were counted." The same commerce, from the mid-fourteenth century, declined with the Ming Chinese period (the ban on sea voyages); the direct European reach (Vasco da Gama in 1498, Magellan in 1521) is the late-fifteenth-and-early-sixteenth-century event.

The Chinese silk-trade with the outside world was systematised by the Han dynasty around 200 BC — the so-called "Silk Road" — the backbone of China's reciprocal commerce with Central Asia and the Mediterranean.
