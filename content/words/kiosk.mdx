      en: |
        In the palace architecture of the Ottoman classical period (fifteenth-seventeenth centuries), *köşk* (Turkish; an architectural function-based variant of the Arabic *qaṣr*) is a detached-residence pavilion on palace grounds, mostly in-garden, for ceremonial-spectacular use. The Bursa-early Ottoman period (early fourteenth century): the adaptation of Anatolian Saljuq pavilion architecture into Ottoman palace-protocol — the garden-pavilion of the Yeşil Cami complex of Bursa, the Muradiye tomb-pavilions. The *köşk* architecture of Istanbul's Topkapı Palace (founded mid-fifteenth century, palace until the mid-nineteenth) offers the most developed examples: the Tiled Pavilion (1473, under Mehmed II), the Baghdad Pavilion (1639, after Murad IV's Baghdad campaign), the Revan Pavilion (1635, in memory of the same period's Revan-Iran conquest), the Circumcision Pavilion (1640).

        The architectural function is three-layered: (i) palace-protection — a private-secluded area inside the walled palace; (ii) ritual-spectacular — a platform for viewing the city and the sea (Topkapı's Bosphorus prospect); (iii) reward-celebration — in memory of a military victory (the Baghdad and Revan pavilions) or for dynastic ritual (the Circumcision Pavilion). This functional richness is the full-spectrum maturation, in the Ottoman classical period, of the Sasanian Persian *kūshk*; the *kiosque* that passed into Europe carried only the "garden-pavilion" aspect; the ceremonial-protocol layer was not transferred.

        The ancient Near-Eastern civilisations (Sumerian, Babylonian, Assyrian, 3000-500 BC) systematised the architecture of the palace-garden. The *Hanging Gardens* of Babylon (under Nebuchadrezzar II, sixth century BC, one of the "seven wonders" of the Hellenistic world) are the most famous ancient instance; on multi-level terrace-gardens, spaces with pavilions.
