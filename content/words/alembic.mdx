      en: |

A hundred and twenty years on, when Robert Boyle's *The Sceptical Chymist* (1661) completed the alchemy-chemistry break on paper, the alembic kept its place on the laboratory bench: the same vessel for the alchemist and for the early modern chemist. The fact that the technical illustrations of Boyle's age label the device "alembic" tells us that the word weathered the disciplinary break unworn; the apparatus survived the crossing, and so did its name.

      en: |
        At Toledo, Gerard of Cremona, and at Salerno, Constantine the African (d. ca. 1099), in moving Arabic alchemical and medical texts into Latin, took *al-inbīq* across as it stood: *alembicum*, then *alambicus*. The word itself is a layered sediment: at the bottom a Greek *ambix* (cup, lidded vessel — from Hellenistic-Egyptian chemical workshops prior to the Sasanian era); above it, Arabic's *al-* article; on top, the Latin termination -*us*. It is among the rarer cases of a tool-name carrying the stamps of three cultures at once. When the Latin text entered a European alchemist's library *in Christo*, the alembic on his bench could not be called Greek or Arab or Latin alone; the answer was all three.

      en: |

The apparatus itself was several hundreds of years old in Egyptian-Greek craft: in the hands of perfumers, of rosewater distillers, of pharmacists. When seventh- and eighth-century Arabic alchemy absorbed the Hellenistic inheritance, the article of Arabic settled at the head of *ambix* — it is hard to say whether what resulted was a new word, or an old word in a new tongue's clothing. By the time Jābir's workshop opened, the word standing before the device was *al-inbīq*; what stood before that, in the lowest layer, was a tradition a thousand years deep.
