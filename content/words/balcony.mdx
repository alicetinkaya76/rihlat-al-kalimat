      en: |
        Italian *balcone* (mid-thirteenth century, first attested in Venetian trade-documents) — the indirect link of the Byzantine-Adriatic palace-architectural tradition to its Perso-Sasanian root. In the ninth-to-twelfth century, Venice was in intense trade-diplomatic contact with Byzantine Constantinople; the "upper-gallery-pavilion" elements of Byzantine palace-architecture (the viewing-pavilions of the Great Palace at Constantinople) became the model for the Venetian aristocracy. In the same period, the *bālā-khāna* type elements of Arab palaces in Norman Sicily (twelfth century) and al-Andalus (tenth-twelfth century) reached the Italian coast through Mediterranean trade-contact channels. Venice's Palazzo Ducale (fourteenth century) and the Grand Canal palace-facades, with their *balcone*s, are the visual expression of this synthesis.

        The debate over the etymological source: some philologists (Devic in 1876, Lokotsch in 1927) defend a direct line from the Arabic *al-balkūnah* / the Persian *bālā-khāna*; the more conservative view (Pellegrini in 1972, Corriente in 2008) prefers the route Germanic *balko-* (a beam, a log) → Late Latin *balcus* → Italian *balcone*. Most modern philologists accept that the two were in interaction in the Mediterranean lingua franca: a Germanic structural-word (a beam, a stone-platform-beam) and a Persian functional-word (an upper-room, a high-space) came together in a single formula in their meaning-overlap. Beneath modern *balcony* lies this Germano-Persian semantic combination.

        French *balcon* (mid-sixteenth century) is borrowed from Italian; English *balcony* in 1618 (in John Florio's Italian-English dictionary as *balcone*) → first practical use in 1645; by this point Shakespeare was dead (1616), and so the description of the "balcony scene" of *Romeo and Juliet* is a late-seventeenth-century editorial addition.

      en: |
        In the period of the Andalusi Caliphate (756-1031), the palace architecture of Córdoba and Madīnat al-Zahrāʾ borrowed the Perso-Abbasid design-heritage of the eastern Islamic world and adapted it to the region. The concept of *bālā-khāna* (Persian "upper-room") was realised in the Andalusi palaces as lattice-screened high-platforms projecting from inner facades — *al-balakūn* / *al-balkūnah* (Arabic-Andalusi pronunciation). In Arab architectural documents (Ibn Ḥayyān in the eleventh century, *al-Muqtabis*) this term appears as a standard element of Andalusi palace-architecture. The *mirador*s (the viewing-pavilions) between the palace-rooms of the Alhambra of Granada (fourteenth century) are the last lexical expression of this Andalusi *al-balkūnah* heritage.

        In Norman Sicily (twelfth century), under Roger II (1130-1154), who conquered the Muslim-Arab palace-architecture and continued at the same time to use it, the Cuba and Zisa pavilions of Palermo carry the same *bālā-khāna* principle: a high space projecting outward from the lattice-screened inner facade. Sicily in the twelfth century was a period when the Mediterranean trade-contact was intense with Pisa, Genoa and Venice; the geographical-channel of the passage of the word *balcone* into Italian is this Norman-Arab palace-architectural stratum. The Persian-Baghdadi-Andalusi-Norman-Venetian-European continuity-line of a millennium-long architectural element is carried in a single word.

      en: |
        In Persian Sasanian architecture, *bālā-khāna* (بالاخانه, Pahlavi *bālāk-xānak*) is a private-secluded space on the upper floor of the palace-structure or on a high platform; the place from which the king watched his palace-garden, incoming envoys, or military-ceremonies — a viewing-space. In the Sasanian Ctesiphon palace, the *bālā-khāna-i shāhī* (the royal-balcony) was one of the important elements of the imperial protocol. The morphology of the word: Persian *bālā* (above, high) + *khāna* (a room, a house, a space) — a compound adjective-noun. The same word in modern Persian (in Iran, Tajikistan and Afghanistan) still carries the senses "upper-room, upper-floor, balcony".

        The Arab conquest (637) brought the Sasanian palace-architecture into the palace-protocol of the Islamic world; *al-balakūn* / *al-balkūnah* (the Arabic-Persian sound-adjustment) was standardised in Abbasid Baghdad (c. 762) and the following periods. Persian *bālā-khāna* → Arabic *al-balkūnah* → Spanish *balcón* (via al-Andalus, thirteenth century) and Latin *balcus* / Italian *balcone* (via the Mediterranean lingua franca, thirteenth century) — two parallel channels of passage into Europe. Modern *balcony* is the final product of the interaction of the two veins.

        The architectural essence of the Persian concept: a space of *horizontal-distance*. The *bālā-khāna*, a platform extending outward from the high-building, creates a transition-zone between out-of-building and in-of-building — for viewing, for taking air, for social-distance-keeping. This functional-logic is the original source of the tragic-romantic narration of the Romeo-Juliet balcony-scene: the balcony = the space of horizontally-distanced communication (from the original Persian concept to the modern editor's reading of Romeo and Juliet).

      en: |
        The ancient Near-Eastern civilisations (Sumero-Akkadian 2500-2000 BC, Babylonian-Assyrian 1800-600 BC, Persian Achaemenid 550-330 BC) built palace-architecture on a high-platform system. The Babylonian ziggurats (Sumerian *é-temen-an-ki*, "the temple of the foundation of heaven and earth") are the earliest examples of high-platform architecture (around 2200 BC); the platform-top temple-space was an indispensable part of the royal-ceremonial procedure. The Assyrians, under Sargon II (c. 720 BC) and Sennacherib (c. 700 BC), built terrace-balconies running along the outer-facade of the Khorsabad and Nineveh palaces — the ancient prototype-example of the modern *balcony* concept.

        The Persian Achaemenid palace at Persepolis (under Darius, c. 515 BC) synthesised this Mesopotamian high-platform tradition: the main-palace entrance-stairway, the *apadāna* (the reception-hall), the platformed pavilions around the palace-gardens. The Persian word *bālā-khāna* is the direct continuation, in the Sasanian period, of this Achaemenid-architectural tradition. That is, beneath the modern word *balcony*: the Sumero-Babylonian ziggurat → the Assyrian palace-terraces → the Achaemenid platformed pavilions of Persepolis → the Sasanian *bālā-khāna* → the Arabic *al-balkūnah* → the Italian *balcone* → modern European *balcony*. A three-thousand-year uninterrupted architectural-lexical line. The modern user drinking coffee on the balcony of a Paris-Haussmannian apartment is the last link of the same architectural-logic as the Mesopotamian king on the ziggurat-platform of 2200 BC.
