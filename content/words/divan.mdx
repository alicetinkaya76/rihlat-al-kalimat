      en: |
        Modern *divan* in international vocabulary lives in three parallel sense-chains: (i) furniture-engineering — a long, cushioned couch on a low base (Turkish *divan*, European *divan / divan-bed*); (ii) Ottoman historiography — *Divan-ı Hümâyûn* is the standard term for Ottoman council-history of the sixteenth-to-nineteenth century, and modern academic historiography (Halil İnalcık, Suraiya Faroqhi) keeps this term; (iii) classical Persian and Turko-Ottoman literature — the standard category for a poet's collected works (*Divan literature*, *Divan poetry*: the Turkish classical-poetry tradition of the fourteenth-to-nineteenth century). The three senses live side by side in the same word, but the modern reader is often unaware of the link between one and another.

        Beneath the three senses lies a single historico-practical logic: the Perso-Abbasid-Ottoman bureaucratic divan-space was arranged around long-cushioned sitting-platforms (sofas); the vizier-katib of clerks sat on these platforms to do their work. The furniture-word was born of the space-word (the council = a type of space + the cushioned sitting-platforms inside it). The poetry-word is a separate parallel sense-type: from a vizier-clerk's record of *dīwān al-shiʿr* (the poetry-divan, the poetry-notebook), it passed into the name of a literary genre. A single Persian word became the name of three distinct institutions — administrative, furniture, and literary.

      en: |
        The first stream: seventeenth-century European-Ottoman diplomatic discourse. In French and English embassy reports *le Divan* / *the Divan* names the Ottoman imperial council (Sieur de Vincennes 1664, Sir Paul Rycaut 1668 *The History of the Present State of the Ottoman Empire*). This political-sense was turned, in Goethe's *West-östlicher Divan* (1819), into a literary metaphor — an East-West poetic-divan dialogue, the infrastructure of the German romanticism under the influence of Ḥāfiẓ. In the same period, the Orientalist writings of Voltaire and Volney use "le divan" as the standard term for the Ottoman imperial institution.

        The second stream: late-eighteenth-to-early-nineteenth-century French aristocratic domestic interior-architecture. With the *turquerie* fashion, when the Turkish-model long-cushioned sitting-platform entered the European drawing-room, it was named *un divan* — the soft, movable furniture version of the original Turkish-sofa. English *divan* is attested in the furniture sense from 1702; after 1830 the "divan bed" (sitting by day, sleeping by night) is an industrial-furniture category. In the twentieth century *divan kanepe* / *divan yatak* are the standard terms of modern domestic furniture. The two distinct social-context streams of the same word flowed in parallel for a hundred years in modern Europe; the word's political-Eastern echo reached the literary-romantic current, its furniture-Eastern echo reached the industrial-consumption current.

        Osmanlı *Divan-ı Hümâyûn* (1453 İstanbul fethinden 1654 Köprülü reformlarına kadar Topkapı'da; sonra Bâbıâli'ye taşındı, 1908 İkinci Meşrutiyet'e kadar) — sadrazam başkanlığında kazasker, defterdar, nişancı, kapıkulu ağaları üyeliğinde haftada dört kez (pazartesi, salı, çarşamba, cumartesi) toplanan imparatorluk meclisi. Toplantı protokolü 16.-17. yy itibariyle son derece detaylı: belirli oturma sırası, belirli giysi kuralları, belirli görüşme akışı (askeri-mali-adli-diplomatik), padişahın *kafes* arkasından gizli-gözetimi.         Bürokratik *dīwān* sözcüğünün anlamı çoklu-kurumsal: aynı kelime aynı zamanda (i) toplantı-mekanı (Topkapı'daki kubbeli salon, *Kubbealtı*), (ii) toplantı-kurulu (üye-sadrazamlar), (iii) kurul-arşivi (kararların yazılı tutulduğu defter), (iv) toplantıda görülen pratik iş (resmi olarak "divan görüşmek"). Modern kullanıcı *divan* dediğinde sadece tek bir anlamı çağırır, ama 17. yy Osmanlı katibi aynı sözcükle dört farklı pratik birim arasında salınırdı. Bu çoklu-pratik anlam Avrupa'ya aktarılamadı; transferde sadece tek-anlam-katmanları geçti.
      en: |
        The Ottoman *Divan-ı Hümâyûn* (from the conquest of Istanbul in 1453 to the Köprülü reforms of 1654, at Topkapı; afterwards it moved to the Sublime Porte, until the second constitutional period of 1908) — the imperial council, presided by the grand vizier, with the kadıaskers, the *defterdar*, the *nişancı* and the *kapıkulu ağaları* as members, meeting four times a week (Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, Saturday). The protocol of the meeting was, by the sixteenth-and-seventeenth century, extremely detailed: a specific seating order, specific dress rules, a specific flow of discussion (military-financial-judicial-diplomatic), the sultan's hidden-supervision from behind the *kafes*. This institutional architecture was distilled from the Abbasid transformation (the Baghdadi *dīwān al-rasāʾil*, ninth century) of the Sasanian Persian *dīvān-i shāhī* (the royal divan) and from the Anatolian Saljuq *divan* heritage (thirteenth century), and reached its full form in the Ottoman classical period.

        The sense of the bureaucratic word *dīwān* is multi-institutional: the same word means at once (i) the meeting-space (the domed hall at Topkapı, the *Kubbealtı*), (ii) the meeting-council (the member-grand-viziers), (iii) the council-archive (the register where decisions are written), (iv) the practical business done in the meeting (officially "to hold a divan"). The modern user, in saying *divan*, calls up only one sense at a time, but the seventeenth-century Ottoman clerk swung between four different practical units with the same word. This multi-practical sense could not be transferred to Europe; only single-meaning-layers passed in the transfer.

      en: |
        The Abbasid caliphate (750-1258) systematically borrowed the Persian Sasanian bureaucratic heritage; the foundation of Baghdad by the Caliph al-Manṣūr (762) was directly modelled on the Sasanian palace, and the *dīwān* was among the central elements of this transfer.         The literary-sense outgrowth of the word: the *dīwān al-shiʿr* (the poetry-notebook) used by Abbasid palace-clerks — the systematic register-book of poems recited by court-poets in the caliph's presence. In the ninth-and-tenth century this practical category broadened to the sense of "a poet's collected works" (e.g. *Dīwān Abī Tammām*, *Dīwān al-Mutanabbī* — the standard category-name in modern Arabic literary historiography).         Pahlavi *dīvān* (Sasanian Persian, third-seventh centuries AD) — the ancient Avesta-Pahlavi word *dipī* / *dipīvar* "writing, scribe" (from the Akkado-Aramaic-Persian lingua-franca root *dpyr*, "scribe") + the Persian plural suffix-adjustment. Its first meaning is "the writing-place, the place where writings are stored" — the royal administrative archive. In Sasanian palace bureaucracy, specific functional categories like *dīvān-i shāhī* (the royal divan), *dīvān-i sarbāz* (the soldiers' divan), *dīvān-i kālāha* (the goods' divan). This system was one of the central elements of the Sasanian administrative model, distinct from Hellenistic-Roman bureaucracy: a more centralised, writing-based form of governance.

        The etymological depth of the word: at the lowest root, the Sumero-Akkadian *dub* (a clay-tablet, a writing-document, around 3000 BC) → Aramaic *dpr* (a scribe, a clerk) → Persian *dipī* / *dipīr* (writing, scribe) → Pahlavi *dīvān* (writing-archive, the council of scribes) → Arabic *dīwān* → Turkish *divan* → European *divan*. A five-thousand-year uninterrupted word-chain of writing-bureaucracy-governance; modern *divan* is the last lexical signature of this long vein. The modern user lying on the divan-bed of a European home is using a word linked to the signature on the clay-tablet of an ancient Mesopotamian scribe.
