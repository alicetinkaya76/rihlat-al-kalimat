      en: |
        The chequered tablecloth on which the Norman-English Exchequer (post-1066) reckoned its accounts was called, in Anglo-Norman, *eschequier* — "a chess-board" — and the method by which it counted resembled the stacking of pieces on such a board. The word in time became the name of the *institution* to which the table belonged: the Exchequer, the Crown's Treasury. From the fourteenth century orders of payment were written on this institution: a *check* or *cheque* — a piece of paper for the *checking* of a payment. In the seventeenth century private London banking standardised the instrument (the Bank of England was founded in 1694). By the mid-nineteenth century *cheque* had been exported to continental Europe: French *chèque* (after the Banque de France of 1865), German *Scheck*, Italian *assegno bancario* (the same semantic in different lexis), Turkish *çek* (in the late-nineteenth-century Tanzimat banking reforms). No one planned that a Persian game-cry should, in six hundred years, become the name of a global payment instrument.

      en: |
        The earliest written trace of chess vocabulary in Latin Europe is the <em>Versus de Scachis</em> (c. 1000, the abbey of Einsiedeln in Switzerland); the poem assigns a Latin name to each piece — *scaccus* (a piece, then the game itself), *scacchi* (the game-name, which eventually settled). From the eleventh century onward Norman Sicily, al-Andalus and the Byzantine intermediary spread the game across Europe in parallel. The Toledan translators did not publish the Arabic *kitāb al-shaṭranj* tradition — al-Adli, al-Sulī, al-Lajlaj — into Latin, but the Mozarabs carried the game as everyday practice into Christian Iberia: Alfonso X's <em>Libro de los juegos</em> (1283) presents chess with illustrations drawn directly from Arabic exemplars. The three Latin spellings of the warning — *scaccus* (Italian), *eschec* (Old French), *jaque* (Castilian) — show how the same Perso-Arabic core softened differently across Romance dialects. The same warning, fixed in three tongues.

      en: |
        Chess entered the Abbasid court from Sasanian Persia around 750 under the name *shaṭranj* (Sanskrit *chaturaṅga* → Persian *chatrang* → Arabic *shaṭranj*). During the reigns of al-Maʾmūn and al-Mutawakkil it became firmly established as a courtly game; its rules, openings and endgame problems acquired a scholarly literature — al-Adli (c. 840), Abū Bakr al-Sūlī (d. 946), al-Lajlaj (d. 970) wrote analyses across decades. The game passed into al-Andalus by the eleventh century via the Córdoban court; it was played on either side of the Christian-Muslim frontier by the same rules. The warning *al-shāh* — "the king is threatened", from Persian "*šāh*" meaning king — settled in the Arabic chess vocabulary; *shāh māt* — "the king is helpless" — named the endgame. By the eleventh century chess was a natural element of courtly language in the Muslim world; the word was less a game-cry than a cultural marker.

      en: |
        The game entered the Sasanian Persian court from the north-west of India under its Sanskrit name *chaturaṅga* — "four-limbed (army)"; the Persian name was a calque, *chatrang*. For the game's "king" piece the natural Persian word was *šāh*: a term for a sovereign already in use, in no way bound to a game. When a piece was threatened, one called out "*šāh*" to the other player — "take care of your king." When the game passed from Persian to Arabic during the conquest (c. 650), the Persian terminology was preserved on the whole: *shaṭranj*, *shāh*, *bayḏaq* (a pawn), *fīl* (an elephant, from a Persian root). The Persian sovereign-term *šāh* entered Arabic as a part of the game, and from there began the millennium-long voyage that lay ahead of it. No one supposed, when a game-piece was named after a king, that what had just happened was a linguistic event without precedent in the world. But that, exactly, is what it was.
