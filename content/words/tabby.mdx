  en: "*literally:* of al-ʿAttab, the al-ʿAttab weave. Arabic *al-ʿAttābī* (العَتّابيّ) takes its name from a quarter of Baghdad — *ḥayy al-ʿAttābī / darb al-ʿAttābī*, the neighbourhood that grew around the estates of the family of *ʿAttāb ibn Asīd*, descendants of the first Umayyad caliph Muʿāwiya, and was Baghdad's silk-weaving centre from the ninth through the twelfth centuries. The watered or striped silk-and-cotton blends woven there were named *al-ʿAttābī* — a textile-pattern name taken from a neighbourhood name. The word reached Spanish as *ataby / atabí* (twelfth-century Toledo), Italian *tabbino* (thirteenth-century Genoa), Old French *atabis > tabis* (fourteenth century), English *tabby* (sixteenth century) — the original *al-* prefix lost on the Italian-French line. In the early seventeenth century English *tabby* shifted to name *a cat with striped or watered markings* — a textile pattern's name becoming an animal's. In modern English *tabby cat* is the dominant sense; the weaving sense has retreated into the expert vocabulary of textile historians."

      en: |
        Modern English *tabby* is dominantly a cat: *mackerel tabby* (parallel thin stripes), *classic tabby* (broad whorled stripes), *spotted tabby*, *ticked tabby* (each hair banded — the Abyssinian). The 2024 estimates from feline pedigree research suggest that **60-70%** of the world's domestic cats carry the tabby gene (the agouti M gene, a dominant gene). The majority of a global population is named for the al-ʿAttab quarter of Baghdad. In Turkish the parallel word is *tekir* (from Ancient Greek *tigris*, "tiger" — a different etymology, same object); but in scientific and international cat pedigrees *tabby* is the standard term. In textile, the *tabby weave* remains a technical term: a one-up-one-down plain weave (modern textile writing tends to prefer *plain weave*). The early-seventeenth-century shift from weave to animal occurred in the moment when European pet culture was being born; aristocratic circles named "a creature with a precious silk pattern" with an aesthetic label. Today that aristocratic label lives on a street cat's back.

      en: |
        In late-sixteenth-century England *tabby* was still a textile word: a 1573 *List of Things to be sold* inventory includes "Tabbie silke"; Marlowe's *Edward II* (1594) has the courtiers "in rich tabby suites" — *tabby* pointing to a fabric's decorative pattern. The metaphor began in the early seventeenth century: a cat's **striped or marbled fur** was compared to the watered pattern of tabby silk. The earliest written attestation of the tabby-cat sense is in Richard Brathwait's *Barnabae itinerarium* (1638): "their cat is a Tabbie too." Edward Topsell's *History of Four-footed Beasts* (1658) gives the detail: "A grey and Tabbye-colour'd Cat." By the mid-seventeenth century *tabby cat* was an established secondary sense; by the end of the century *tabby* alone could name a cat (especially a female one) — *Old Tabby* "old female cat" → "old spinster" as a metaphor. **Neighbourhood name → weave pattern → animal fur pattern**: the three-step chain was complete, and at each step the Arabic origin was buried one layer deeper.

      en: |
        In twelfth-century Toledo, Muslim, Christian and Jewish weavers worked in parallel workshops; *al-ʿAttābī*, through the phonology of Andalusian Arabic, crossed as *ataby / atabí* into Castilian Spanish — the name of a silk-and-cotton blend watered cloth. The thirteenth- and fourteenth-century inventories of the kings of Aragon and the Castilian nobility list *ataby* among the luxury textiles. Italian Mediterranean traders (Genoa, Pisa, Florence) acquired *ataby* through al-Andalus or directly from Eastern markets; the textile-collection inventories of Cosimo de'Medici in fourteenth-century Florence mention *tabbino*. The French royal *Garde-Robe* registers (early fifteenth century) use *tabis*: for the ceremonial garments of the royal family (especially coronation and wedding), since the watered effect of light on the silk amplified the splendour of the court. The word entered England through Anglo-Norman French at the end of the 1300s as *atabis / tabis*; until the mid-fifteenth century it remained a luxury of court and aristocracy; by the sixteenth century it had descended into the bourgeois textile market.         Abbasid Baghdad (after 762) was positioned as one of the silk-weaving centres of the Islamic world. The city's administrative planning was neighbourhood-based (*ḥayy*); each major family or trade-cluster gathered into a quarter. The *ḥayy al-ʿAttābī* is recorded in detail by Yāqūt al-Ḥamawī's *Muʿjam al-buldān* (c. 1224): the quarter that grew from the estates of *ʿAttāb ibn Asīd* (d. 13/634-5), a descendant of the first Umayyad caliph Muʿāwiya, in the south-west of Baghdad, dense with silk-weaving workshops. The Arabic name of this cloth type is *al-ʿAttābī* — *of the quarter*. Yāqūt's record implies that the word was alive throughout the ninth-to-twelfth centuries; al-Maqrīzī's *Khiṭaṭ* (c. 1422 Cairo) lists *al-ʿAttābī* among the luxury textiles Egypt imported from Baghdad.
