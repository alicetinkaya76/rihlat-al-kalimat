      en: |
        The thirteenth-century star-catalogue project of Toledo, the *Alfonsine Tables* (under the patronage of King Alfonso X "the Wise" of Castile, 1252-70), is the summit of the Latinisation of Arab astronomical tables (especially al-Battānī's *Zīj al-Ṣābiʾ* and Ibn al-Zarqālī's *Tablas Toledanas*). In this project, Arab star-names were systematically adapted to Latin forms; dozens of star-names like Aldebaran, Altair, Betelgeuse, Rigel, Deneb passed from their Arabic origins into the Latin European astronomical vocabulary.

        The production-process of the word *Vega* is a typical instance of compact-Latinisation: the three words of the Arabic full-phrase *al-nasr al-wāqiʿ* were too long for Latin. The translator dropped the *al-nasr* (eagle) part because the Lyra-Cygnus-Aquila trio was all in the *al-nasr* (eagle) family (a Latin distinction was needed); the remaining *wāqiʿ* (falling) word was written, by sound-fitting, as *Vega*. Likewise the word *al-nasr al-ṭāʾir* (the flying eagle) was Latinised as *Altair*. Sound-change instance: Arabic *w-* → Latin *v-* (in alphabet-mismatch), *q* + *iʿ* (the throat-sound + i + the ayn-sound) replaced by *g* for the Latin ear. This is a standard-instance of twelfth-and-thirteenth-century Arab-Latin philological adaptive-practice.

      en: |
        The Arab astronomical tradition developed, alongside the Greco-Ptolemaic mythological constellations, a star-naming system from pre-Islamic Arab desert-imagery. The three bright stars in the Summer Triangle (Vega, Altair, Deneb) were imaged in the Arab tradition as *al-nusūr al-thalātha* ("the three eagles"): (i) *al-nasr al-wāqiʿ* (the falling/swooping eagle) — the α-star of Lyra (Vega) — the pose of an eagle closing its wings and diving on its prey; (ii) *al-nasr al-ṭāʾir* (the flying eagle) — the α-star of Aquila (Altair) — an eagle flying with spread wings; (iii) *al-dajāja* (the hen-duck) — the α-star of Cygnus (Deneb) — the form of a flying bird.

        The written standard of this three-star eagle symbol was set in the tenth century in al-Ṣūfī's *Kitāb al-Kawākib al-Thābita* (The Book of Fixed Stars, c. 964). The work is a hybrid uniting the star-list of the Greek Ptolemy's *Almagest* with the Arab-folk-imagery; each constellation is shown with two distinct picture-descriptions. In al-Battānī's *Zīj al-Ṣābiʾ* (c. 900) the position-tables of Vega were recorded with minute-accuracy of right-ascension and ecliptic-declination; this Arab-Islamic astronomical data-infrastructure passed into Europe by the Toledo transfer of the twelfth-thirteenth century, and the modern word *Vega* is a direct philological inheritance of this practical-observational tradition.

      en: |
        In pre-Islamic Arabia the astronomy-practice of the desert-Bedouin nomadic clans (the Jāhiliyya period, c. 500 BC - AD 600) was preserved in oral tradition; the night-sky map was the practical infrastructure of nomadic-life — direction-finding, season-tracking, water-searching, caravan-travel. The Arab desert-star tradition named every bright star in the sky with oral-practical names; not mythological, but words tied to desert-life experience — zoonyms (animal-names) and toponyms (place-type-names).

        The three-eagle symbol (*al-nasr al-wāqiʿ*, *al-nasr al-ṭāʾir*, *al-dajāja*) was the practical direction-finding name of this pre-Islamic Arab desert-star tradition for the Lyra-Cygnus-Aquila region. The practical function of the Summer Triangle: in the Arabian peninsula in summer months, the three stars are prominent in the high-east position of the night sky; they help to determine the east-direction in night caravan-travel. Because the eagle-image is a symbol of strength-courage-swiftness in Arab culture, it became the natural practical-naming for these three brightest stars of the sky. The Islamic-era Arab astronomical practice combined this pre-Islamic desert-knowledge with Greek-Hellenistic astronomical theory; the Baghdad Bayt al-Ḥikma translators, when carrying Greek Ptolemaic mythology into Arabic, recorded the pre-Islamic Arab-folk naming in parallel.

      en: |
        In ancient Mesopotamian astronomy-mythology (Sumerian-Akkadian, 3000-1000 BC), the Lyra region was imaged as the *Pabilsag* star-cluster; in Akkadian tablet-records it is also mentioned as a "bird-form star". The ancient Greek astronomical tradition (Eudoxus, 400 BC), taking up this Mesopotamian image, gave the constellation the name *Lyra* (the Lyre); in Greek mythology, the lyre that Orpheus played was said to have been lifted into the sky by Zeus after his death. Vega *Lyrae* — "the α-star of Lyra" — is the standard naming of the Greco-Latin astronomical tradition.

        The name *al-nasr al-wāqiʿ* independently developed by pre-Islamic Arabia for the same star is an instance of the parallel-imagery of ancient cultures: a single bright point, two distinct images — the "bony tree-lyre" of the Greek and the "eagle swooping" of the Arab. The linguistic-archaeological meaning carried by the modern word *Vega*: a single star, over three thousand years, has successively taken on the images of three distinct cultural-practical traditions (Mesopotamian, Greco-Latin, Arab), and survives in the modern European-Anglo scientific-practical vocabulary preserving the Arab-image. In modern astronomy books *Vega* (the α-star of Lyra) is a single reference — but beneath it lies three thousand years of imagistic-archaeology.
