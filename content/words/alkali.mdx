      en: |
        In modern chemistry *alkali* is both a technical concept and a class-name: (i) *alkali metals* — group 1 of the periodic table (lithium, sodium, potassium, rubidium, caesium, francium); reactive metals with a single outer electron that react violently with water; (ii) *alkaline-earth metals* — group 2 of the periodic table (beryllium, magnesium, calcium, strontium, barium, radium); (iii) in the general vocabulary of chemistry, *alkali* is the acid-opposing base solution, with pH greater than 7. *Alkalinity*, *alkaloid* (the plant-derived nitrogen-based organic compounds: morphine, nicotine, caffeine), *alkalize* — among the basic terms of the language of modern science.

Global potassium hydroxide and potassium carbonate (*K₂CO₃*) production is the basic input of the fertilizer industry (the precondition of all modern agricultural productivity). The survival of the word as the name of a chemical group in the modern world is the direct transfer to modern science of the technical-practical accumulation of the eighth-century Jābir workshop. No Greek-Latin alternative word was accepted; the international standardness of the word *alkali* is undisputed.

Sözcüğün Lavoisier-Berthollet-Davy-Mendeleev hattında dört nesil bilim insanı tarafından çekirdek-terim olarak benimsenmesi, Arap simya geleneğinin modern kimyaya tam-aktarımının dilbilimsel kanıtı. Lavoisier'in kendisi Cābir bin Hayyân'a referans vermez, ama kullandığı terminolojinin %15-20'si Arap-İslam simyacılarından gelir (Marcellin Berthelot 1893 tezi *La Chimie au moyen âge* bu filolojik bağlantıyı kanonik olarak kanıtlar).
      en: |
        Antoine Lavoisier's *Traité élémentaire de chimie* (1789) — the founding book of modern chemistry — in beginning the systematic classification of chemical elements, reorganised the terminology that remained from the craft-words of the Arab alchemist. He placed the word *alkali* at the centre of acid-base theory: acid (*acidum*) and alkali (*alkali*) are the two poles of Lavoisier's theory of chemical interaction. After him Claude Berthollet (1748-1822) systematically defined the class of *alkali metals* and their common electrochemical properties. In 1807, Sir Humphry Davy (1778-1829) first separated pure sodium and potassium in elemental form by electrolysis; he experimentally proved the category of "alkali metals".

        In 1869, when Dmitri Mendeleev constructed his periodic table, he placed the alkali metals as a single group (group 1); the fundamental mathematical-predictive success of Mendeleev's modern periodic system came from the group-coherence of the properties of the alkali metals. The adoption of the word, over four generations of scientists in the Lavoisier-Berthollet-Davy-Mendeleev line, as a core-term, is the linguistic proof of the full transfer of the Arab alchemical tradition into modern chemistry. Lavoisier himself does not cite Jābir ibn Ḥayyān, but 15-20% of the terminology he uses comes from the Arab-Islamic alchemists (Marcellin Berthelot's 1893 work *La Chimie au moyen âge* canonically proves this philological connection).

      en: |
        In the twelfth-century Toledo translation school, Gerard of Cremona and the other translators (Robert of Chester, Dominicus Gundissalinus) carried Arabic alchemical texts into Latin. Between 1150 and 1200, parts of the corpus of Jābir ibn Ḥayyān (the hundreds of treatises attributed to him), al-Rāzī's *Kitāb al-Asrār* (The Book of Secrets, c. 925), the chemical sections of Ibn Sīnā's *Kitāb al-Shifāʾ*, were Latinised. In these translations the word *al-qilī* was consistently Latinised: *alkali*, *alcali*, *alchali*. The Arabic article *al-* was taken with the Latin form together (the word as a single unitary "alkali" rather than "el-kali"); modern *alkali* is a direct descent of this Toledo form.

        Andalusi and Maghrebi soap-production traditionally used *al-qilī* as the basic input: seaweeds (the *salicornia* of the Syrian coast, the *barilla* of the western Mediterranean), desert-plants (the *Salsola kali* of North Africa — the modern Latin plant-name preserves this Arabic-origin word) were burned, the ash was mixed with water, the salt-component of the solution was filtered. These soap-making techniques spread in the thirteenth century from the Spanish coast (Castile, Seville) to Provence (Marseille), Italy (Genoa, Venice) and Britain; the word *alkali* passed into European industry as a technical term of the craft-lingua-franca. By the sixteenth century, European glass-making (the Murano glass of Venice) and soap-production were fully using the term *alkali*. This is the first European use, independent of modern science, that came through the craft-practice.

      en: |
        Jābir ibn Ḥayyān (c. 721-815, Kufa-Baghdad) is the founding figure of Arab-Islamic alchemy; the Jabirian Corpus attributed to him is the first systematic mass of technical chemical literature, in hundreds of treatises. Jābir's methodological invention is *mawāzīn* (the balance-principle) — reading chemical interaction as the equalising of the qualities hot/cold and dry/moist. *Al-qilī* (the ash-salt) is, in Jabirian chemistry, the base-salt carrying the "hot-dry" character, the opposite-balancer of the acid-practice (known in Jābir's age as *al-zāj*, vitriol).

        Al-Rāzī (c. 854-925), in *Kitāb al-Asrār* and *Kitāb Sirr al-Asrār*, systematised the Jabirian tradition; he tied to written standards the laboratory-organisation of chemical practice (crucibles, furnaces, alembics, sieves) and the classification of matter (minerals, salts, the compound family of alkalis and acids). Al-Rāzī distinguished the two main sources of *al-qilī* (the ash-salt): plant-origin (seaweed, the ashes of desert-plants — modern potassium carbonate *K₂CO₃*) and mineral-origin (natural lake-salts — modern sodium carbonate *Na₂CO₃*).

        Ibn Sīnā (Avicenna, 980-1037), in the *Maʿādin wa-Āthār ʿUlwiyya* (Minerals and Atmospheric Phenomena) of his *Kitāb al-Shifāʾ*, divided chemical compounds into four basic classes: salts, vitriol-acids, alkali-salts, fusible-metals. The word *al-qilī* is the technical standard-term of this Arab-Islamic alchemical tradition. The birth of the word independently of the Greek-Latin tradition is important: Greek Hellenistic alchemy (Zosimos, fourth century CE) is the precursor of this Arab practical-vocabulary, but the specific *al-qilī* term is the product of the Arab practice; in Greek there was *natron* (a potash-soda mixture), but the conscious classification-infrastructure is Arab.

      en: |
        The use of ash-salt is one of the oldest chemical-technologies of human civilisation. In ancient Mesopotamia (3000 BC), the word *uḫūlu* (Sumerian ash-salt) appears in tablet-records in recipes for glass-making and soap-making; the Akkadian *uḫūlu* (ash-salt) and *uḫūlu qarnānu* (horned/sharp ash-salt = potassium carbonate) were distinguished. Ancient Egypt (2000 BC) extracted natural *natron* (a sodium carbonate-bicarbonate mixture) from the Wadi al-Natrun lakes — the basic input of mummification, glass-making, soap-production. The Greek Hellenistic world (300 BC - AD 300) inherited this Egyptian practical knowledge through Alexandria; *nitron* (the Greek name of natron) is widespread in Greco-Roman texts.

        The Arab-Islamic inheritance of this ancient tradition took place with the conquest of the seventh and eighth centuries. The Mesopotamian-Egyptian practical-knowledge passed into the Arab workshops of Baghdad-Kufa; the word *al-qilī* is a new Arab-origin term (the Arab verbal root *q-l-y*, "to burn"), but the infrastructure of practical-knowledge has its ancient roots. The original contribution of the Arab alchemist (Jābir, al-Rāzī, Ibn Sīnā): the placing of practical-knowledge in a systematic theoretical framework, the establishment of a classification-infrastructure, the development of written standards.

        The three-layer history of the word: (i) the ancient Mesopotamian-Egyptian craft-practice (3000 BC - AD 600); (ii) the Arab-Islamic period of systematisation (AD 700-1200); (iii) the modern European transfer of science and practice (AD 1200 to the present). The modern word *alkali* carries the naming-system of the Arab-phase of this five-thousand-year-long craft-practical tradition. A classical instance of the "India → Arab → Europe" tripartite-transfer pattern of the history of science — but here there is a *Mesopotamian-Egyptian* beginning instead of India.
