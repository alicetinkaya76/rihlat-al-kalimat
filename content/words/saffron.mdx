  tr: "*birebir anlamı:* sarı, sarı-altın boya. Arapça *zaʿfarān* (زَعفَران) hem çiçeğin (Crocus sativus'un stigmasının), hem de o stigmadan elde edilen boyanın adıydı; bitkinin ve rengin tek bir kelimede çakışması, kelimenin Avrupa'ya geçişinde *baharat* + *boya* anlamlarını birlikte taşıdı. Endülüs Arapçası *az-zaʿfarān* artikelle birlikte İspanyolcaya *azafrán* olarak girdi; oradan İtalyanca *zafferano*, Fransızca *safran*, İngilizce *saffron* — her dile geçişte *al-* önekinin İspanyol-Latince hattında düşmesi tipik."
  en: "*literally:* yellow, the yellow-gold dye. Arabic *zaʿfarān* (زَعفَران) named both the flower (the stigma of *Crocus sativus*) and the dye drawn from it; the plant and the colour collapsed into a single word, and that collapse carried both senses — *spice* and *pigment* — across the word's European passage. Andalusian Arabic *az-zaʿfarān*, definite article and all, entered Spanish as *azafrán*; thence Italian *zafferano*, French *safran*, English *saffron* — the *al-* prefix lost on the Spanish-Latin line, kept in Iberian Spanish."
  ar: "*حرفيًّا:* الأَصفَر، الصِّبغُ الأَصفرُ الذَّهَبيّ. كانت العربيّةُ *زَعفَران* تُسمّي في آنٍ واحد الزَّهرةَ (مَيسَمَ نباتِ *Crocus sativus*) والصِّبغَ المُستَخرَجَ منها؛ وانطِواءُ النَّباتِ واللَّونِ في كَلِمةٍ واحدةٍ نَقَلَ المَعنَيَيْن معًا — *البَهارَ* و*الصِّبغَ* — في عُبورِ الكلمةِ إلى أوروبّا. ودَخَلَت العربيّةُ الأَندَلُسيّةُ *الزَّعفَران*، بأَلِها التَّعريفيّ، الإسبانيّةَ صِيغةَ *azafrán*؛ ومنها *zafferano* الإيطاليّةَ، و*safran* الفرنسيّةَ، و*saffron* الإنكليزيّةَ — مع سُقوطِ بادِئةِ *al-* على الخَطِّ الإسبانيِّ-اللاتينيّ."

      en: |
        With the fall of Granada in 1492 and the final expulsion of the *Moriscos* (forcibly Christianised Muslims) in 1609-1614, Andalusia's Islamic institutions were erased — but agricultural knowledge stayed in the soil.       en: |
        Old French *safran* entered Middle English via Anglo-Norman in the late twelfth century; the earliest written use is c. 1200, in the *Ancrene Wisse* manuscript, as a medicinal dye. Italian *zafferano* settled in the thirteenth century; Florentine merchants used two parallel sources — Spanish La Mancha and the Iranian-Syrian route — and Pegolotti's *Pratica della Mercatura* (c. 1340) lists saffron grades and prices city by city (Granadan and Indian saffron quoted separately). German *Safran* fixed by the mid-fourteenth century, Dutch *saffraan* in the same window; each transit moves the word from the merchant ledger to the kitchen lexicon. The word carries three registers — *agriculture* + *trade* + *cuisine* — inside a single utterance.

        Classical Arabic *zaʿfarān* derives from a prehistoric Semitic layer: Akkadian *azupīrānu* (before the seventh century BCE), Aramaic *zaʿparānā*, Hebrew *karkōm* (different root, but the same plant). The earliest cultivated traces of *Crocus sativus* are Minoan Crete (c. 1700 BCE, the Akrotiri frescoes) and Persian medicine of the *Zang* age. Medieval Arab medicine handled the word as a medicinal-aromatic spice: Ibn Sīnā's *Qānūn* (Book V) and Ibn al-Bayṭār's *Kitāb al-Jāmiʿ li-Mufradāt al-Adwiya wa-l-Aghdhiya* (c. 1240, Damascus) listed the effects of saffron on mood and heart. The Indian Ocean–Mediterranean merchant network carried both the word and the spice along the Baghdad–Damascus–Egypt–Maghreb–Andalusia line from the tenth century onward; al-Andalus was already a new field for the word, not its native ground.
