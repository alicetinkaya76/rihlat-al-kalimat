      en: |
        One of the foundational concepts of modern meteorology and climate science, *monsoon* names not a single Indian-Ocean phenomenon but, globally, the class of similar seasonal wind-rainfall systems (the South Asian monsoon, the East Asian monsoon, the West African monsoon, the North Australian monsoon). Some two billion people — India, Bangladesh, Pakistan, southern China, Southeast Asia — bind their annual agricultural rhythm directly to the arrival-time of this system. In modern English *monsoon* carries two senses: (i) the annual wind system (climatological), (ii) the rainy season itself (everyday speech). Turkish *muson* does not carry that doubleness; it is more a technical-geographical term. In Arabic the modern scientific term is still *al-mawsim* (in its classical sense), alongside *mūnsūn* (back-borrowed through European phonetics). *That is to say, the same Arabic word travels into Europe along three separate language lines at three separate times, and on its fourth journey returns.*

      en: |
        The three key works of Arabic Indian-Ocean navigational literature — Aḥmad ibn Mājid's *Kitāb al-Fawāʾid fī uṣūl ʿilm al-baḥr* (Bahir, c. 1490), Sulaymān al-Mahrī's *al-ʿUmda al-Mahriyya* (early sixteenth century), and Ibn Ḥājir's *al-Minhāj al-Fāḫir* (mid-fifteenth century) — use the word *al-mawsim* in a single sense: "the sailing season, the time-window suitable for spreading sail." An important technical detail: the rhythmic predictability of the Indian-Ocean monsoon (May-September = SW monsoon, November-March = NE monsoon), unlike single-direction-wind oceans such as the Mediterranean or the Atlantic, makes possible *a calendar-bound departure*. A merchant of Aden knows exactly in which month of Shaʿbān he will sail to Calicut, in which month of Ramaḍān he will return; where the European Atlantic sailor waits years for the wind, the Arab merchant of the Indian Ocean moves by an annual calendar. *Mawsim* also means "the season of the Pilgrimage"; the same word is read both as the religious calendar and the navigational calendar. *Three meanings — geographical, religious, economic — of one word: the same annual time-frame.*

        The Arabic word *mawsim* is from the root *w-s-m*, "trace, brand, to mark"; *wasama* (to brand, to set a mark), *mawsim* "the marked time, the appointed time, the season." In classical Arabic, *mawsim* carries two principal senses: (i) the Pilgrimage season (the most common classical use), (ii) the harvest or fair season.       en: |
        In pre-Islamic Arabia the word *mawsim* is used in a single sense: the annual fair-season. The Arabian peninsula's inter-tribal trade had a structural calendar-system: *ʿUkāẓ* (near Mecca, the largest fair), *Majanna*, *Dhū al-Majāz* — each at a fixed month of the year, each bound to the "sacred months" (*al-ashhur al-ḥurum*) in which inter-tribal warfare was forbidden. *Mawsim* is exactly these fair-times; the word takes its sense "annually recurring, appointed time" from this use. After Islam the word is re-meant in a religious frame: the Pilgrimage season (the month of Dhū al-Ḥijja) becomes the most common sense of *mawsim*. Seven hundred years later the Indian-Ocean navigator uses the same word for another historically-coinciding "annually-recurring appointed time." *In three historical phases of one word, three different kinds of appointed time: pagan fair → Pilgrimage → sailing season.* The same semantic mould filled three times historically. Etymology here shows not a word's meaning but who the bearer of a concept is: a calendar-word, whoever its bearer (fair-goer, pilgrim, sailor), serves as the principal marker of the annual cycle.
