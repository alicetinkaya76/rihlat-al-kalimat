        Yanlış-okumanın izi modern Avrupa dillerinde donmuş hâlde yaşar: doğru olsaydı sözcük *sament* / *samt* olurdu, ama tarih *zenith* formunu seçti. Zenith-azimuth ikilisi modern astronomi terminolojisinde aynı kelimenin iki ayrı yanlış-okuma sonucudur. Bilim tarihinin tipik bir paradoksu: yanlış-okumalar yerleşir, doğrulamaya gerek kalmaz.
      en: |
        Gerard of Cremona (1114-1187) translated, in Toledo, the Arabic astronomical texts of al-Battānī, the Arabic version of Ptolemy's *Almagest*, Ibn al-Haytham and others, into Latin. Three basic coordinate-terms in astronomy are of Arabic origin — *samt al-raʾs* (zenith, the direction over the head), *naẓīr al-samt* (nadir, the very opposite of the direction over the head), *al-samt* (azimuth, the horizontal direction in the sky) — and were Latinised by Gerard. But during the Latinisation an orthographic mishap: the Arabic letter *m* of the word *samt* (the three-dotless middle form ـمـ) was misread by the Latin copyist as a two-dotless *n* + an *i* → *senit* / *zenit*.

        The trace of the misreading lives frozen in modern European languages: if it were correct the word would be *sament* / *samt*, but history chose the form *zenith*. From the same translation desk: nadir (Arabic *naẓīr*, "opposite") was translated correctly; azimuth (Arabic *al-samt* — the very same word — "direction") was also Latinised through a different path. The zenith-azimuth pair, in modern astronomical terminology, is the result of two different misreadings of the same word. A typical paradox of the history of science: misreadings settle, no need for correction.

Yani Arap *samt* sözcüğü hem matematik-astronomik (gök-küresi koordinat-sistemi) hem dini-pratik (kıble-belirleme) bir terim.       en: |
        In Arab astronomy, *samt* (سمت, "direction, line, axis") is a fundamental coordinate-concept: an imaginary line drawn from an observer towards some other direction. *Samt al-qibla* (the direction of the qibla), *samt al-shams* (the direction of the sun), *samt al-raʾs* (the direction over the head) — all are different coordinate-applications of one root word. Al-Khwārizmī's *Zīj al-Sindhind* (c. 830), al-Battānī's *Zīj al-Ṣābiʾ* (c. 900), Ibn al-Haytham's *Kitāb al-Manāẓir* (c. 1020) — in all of them the term *samt al-raʾs* is used systematically as the observer's vertical-direction reference.

        The practical mathematics of qibla-determination: the *samt al-qibla* of an observer (the angle of direction to the Kaʿba in Mecca) was a canonical applied problem of Arab astronomy. For its solution, *samt al-raʾs* (the vertical reference axis) was needed as a starting point. So the Arab word *samt* is both a mathematical-astronomical term (the celestial-sphere coordinate system) and a religious-practical one (qibla-finding). In the Latinisation the mathematical sense (zenith, azimuth) came to the fore; the religious-practical sense (qibla) did not pass into Europe.

        Greek Hellenistic astronomy (Hipparchus, second century BC; Ptolemy, second century AD) named the celestial point directly above the observer-station with phrases such as *katà koryphḗn* (κατὰ κορυφήν, "toward the summit") or *zḗniṭ tou katà koryphḗn*. Ptolemy's *Almagest* (c. AD 150) uses this concept systematically in the celestial coordinate system. In the ninth century, al-Ḥajjāj ibn Yūsuf's translation of the *Almagest* into Arabic (the Baghdadi period) identified the Greek coordinate-terminology with its Arabic counterparts: *katà koryphḗn* → *samt al-raʾs*.

        The Arab astronomers (al-Battānī in the ninth, Abū al-Wafāʾ in the tenth, Ibn al-Haytham in the eleventh) fundamentally preserved Greek theory but extended it with mathematical-practical instruments (observational spheres, the astrolabe, the gnomon). The Arab coordinate-vocabulary is the practical-instrumental extension of the Greek theoretical inheritance. When in the twelfth century, in Toledo, Gerard of Cremona translated the *Almagest* from Arabic into Latin, the Greek-Arab vertical-direction terminology (zenith) passed into Europe together with the mathematical-practical concepts proper to Arabic (azimuth). The famous "Greek-Arab-Latin" tripartite-transition pattern of the history of science.

      en: |
        The fundamental concepts of the celestial-sphere coordinate system were developed in ancient Babylonian astronomy (from the seventh century BC in the Mul.Apin tablets): horizontal direction (azimuth), vertical direction (zenith), declination (latitude-angle), right ascension (longitude-angle). The Babylonian astronomers established an observer-centred coordinate system; "above-the-head" (Sumerian *ki-sag*) and "under-the-foot" (Sumerian *ki-gìr*) were the term-pair of the observational practice. This conceptual system was carried to the Greek Hellenistic astronomy (Hipparchus, Ptolemy); from there into the Arab-Islamic world (al-Khwārizmī, al-Battānī); from there into European Latin (Gerard of Cremona 1145, Sacrobosco 1230).

        So behind the modern word *zenith*: (i) the Babylonian concept of "the head-above" (700 BC); (ii) the Greek word *katà koryphḗn* (150 BC); (iii) the Arab word *samt al-raʾs* (830 AD); (iv) the Latin *zenit* (misread, 1145); (v) the modern European *zenith* (standard from the 1300s). A single technical concept, over a seventeen-hundred-year word-history, passes through five distinct language-layers. This process is the typical philological feature of the language of science: under conceptual continuity, the successive change of word-forms.
