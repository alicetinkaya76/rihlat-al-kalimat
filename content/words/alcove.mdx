      en: |
        In Andalusi palace architecture, the *qubba* (القُبَّة, "dome") is a square or octagonal main room with a domed roof; the central-ceremonial space of the palace, the throne-room where the caliph sat. The Madīnat al-Zahrāʾ palace of Córdoba (936-1010, built as the caliphal capital by ʿAbd al-Raḥmān III) was designed as a complex of *qubba*s; each *qubba* was allotted a specific ceremonial function (audience, reception, hospitality, council). The architectural form is the Arab-Andalusi adaptation of the Perso-Sasanian *chahār-ṭāq* (four-arched dome) tradition. The Alhambra of Granada (fourteenth century) offers later and more refined examples of this *qubba* architecture.

        The semantic richness of the word in the Arab architectural vocabulary: *qubba* is both a structural name (the dome-roof) and a functional one (the palace-throne-room). In Andalusi architectural documents (for example in Ibn al-Khaṭīb's description of the Granada palace, *al-Iḥāṭa fī akhbār Gharnāṭa*, fourteenth century), *qubba* recurs often. This architectural-lexical richness was largely lost in the passage to Spanish *alcoba*: Spanish *alcoba* settled only as "an inner room, a bedroom"; the sense of architectural grandeur was effaced.

It emerged as the structural type of Sasanian Zoroastrian fire-temples (third-to-sixth century AD); by the sixth century it was also used as the ceremonial room-type in Sasanian palace architecture. The Arab conquest (637) carried the Sasanian architectural tradition into the palace architecture of the Islamic world; *qubba* became the principal naming-unit of the Arab architectural vocabulary — while the Pahlavi word *gunbad* (dome) survived as a separate Turko-Persian architectural term, *kümbet* / *gunbad*.

        So the technical-architectural line behind the Andalusi *qubba*: Sasanian chahār-ṭāq → Abbasid Baghdadi palace architecture → Andalusi Córdoban caliphal architecture → Spanish Mudéjar architecture → European interior domestic architecture. The word *alcove* is the last link of a seventeen-hundred-year architectural-lexical chain, from a Persian fire-temple to a modern European bedroom-niche.

Yani modern *alcove* sözcüğünün altında en az iki ayrı tarihsel damar var: (i) sözcük-kök tarihi — antik Sami "kubbe" sözcüğünün İslam ortaçağına geçişi; (ii) mimari-teknoloji tarihi — Mezopotamya-Pers-Roma kubbe-örme geleneklerinin Endülüs saraylarında sentezi.       en: |
        Dome-building technology is attested in Neolithic Mesopotamia from around 6500 BC; the corbelled-dome technique became, under Sumer and Akkad, the standard of monumental building. The Roman *cupola* / *cella* technique (the Pantheon, AD 126, the greatest ancient dome in the world) is the Mediterranean adaptation of that Near-Eastern inheritance. The *chahār-ṭāq* of Sasanian Persia is the synthesis of the Perso-Roman-Mesopotamian dome traditions.

        Arabic architecture's word *qubba* unites, with the roots it inherits semantically from the ancient Semitic (Akkadian *qubbatu*, "dome, tent-top"), the Sasanian technical inheritance. So beneath the modern word *alcove* there are at least two distinct historical veins: (i) the word-root history — the passage of an ancient Semitic word for "dome" into the Islamic Middle Ages; (ii) the architectural-technological history — the synthesis of the Mesopotamian-Perso-Roman dome-building traditions in the Andalusi palaces. Reading a word from its original etymological root requires also seeing the architectural practice in which it lived.
