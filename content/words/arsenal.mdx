      en: |
        From the sixteenth century, Italian *arsenale* gave French *arsenal*, Spanish *arsenal*, German *Arsenal*, English *arsenal*. In the same passage the meaning narrows: the older sense (shipyard, dockyard) drops away, and an armoury — the land establishment of military manufacture — takes its place. This is the lexical trace of Habsburg and Bourbon state-building: monarchies consolidated weapons-production in unified institutions, and named them with the oldest word the Mediterranean had for them. Madrid's *Arsenal*, Vienna's *Wiener Arsenal* (1856), Berlin's *Zeughaus* as its rival. The *Atarazanas* of Seville, Barcelona and Valencia — the older form of Arabic *al-dār aṣ-ṣināʿa* — survive as port-quarter names; but the modern weapons-depots of those cities now carry *arsenal*. In three hundred years one word crossed from ship to weapon.

İthal edilen yalnız bir kelime değildi: Fatımî Mısır'ın <em>dār aṣ-ṣināʿa</em>'sını gören İtalyan denizcileri ve elçileri — kıyıda devasa, surlu, devlete ait, organize emek ile gemi yapan bir tesis — kendi limanlarına döndüğünde aynı yapıyı kurmaya başladılar.       en: |
        The Venetian *Arsenale* is dated to 1104 — the first industrial-scale state shipyard in the Mediterranean. Venetian records refer to it with spellings close to the Arabic original — *arzanà, darsena, darxana* — and the form *arsenale* settled in the thirteenth century. In Genoa *darsena* survived as the name of the main harbour basin; in Pisa, Palermo, and Mallorca the same root lived under different vowels. What was imported was not only a word: Italian sailors and envoys who saw the Fatimid Egyptian *dār aṣ-ṣināʿa* — a coastal, walled, state-owned plant building ships with organised labour — returned to their own ports and began to build the same structure. They took an *engineering model*: storage, timber-working shops, rope-making yards, ship-basins, all under one administrative roof. Fourteenth-century inventories of the Venetian Arsenale are structurally and surprisingly close to eleventh-century inventories of Cairo's. The word survived because the institution needed something to call it.

      en: |
        Abbasid Baghdad attests in the ninth century a *dār aṣ-ṣināʿa* at Basra in its military accounts; but the mature institutional form appears in Fatimid Cairo in the eleventh. Al-Muqaddasī (d. c. 991) and Ibn Ḥawqal record the *dār aṣ-ṣināʿa* of the Nile delta and the Mediterranean coast — timber-working shops, rope-making yards, ironwork, sail-shops, dry docks, all on one walled site under tight state administration. Through the Mamluk period (1250-1517) the *dār aṣ-ṣināʿa* of Alexandria remained one of the largest shipyards in the Mediterranean. The same institution appeared in Aghlabid Tunisia (Mahdia, 921), in Andalusian Seville, in several Mediterranean ports. The phrase *dār* (house) + *ṣināʿa* (craft, manufacture) was transparent in its parts; together *dār aṣ-ṣināʿa* always named one specific kind of institution, working as a proper name rather than a descriptive phrase. For a century and a half the Mediterranean's standard institutional vocabulary carried this name.

      en: |
        In early Islamic Arabic, *dār* (house, abode, place — *dār al-Islām*, *dār al-ḥarb*, *dār al-kutub*) and *ṣināʿa* (craft, art, production — from the root *ṣ-n-ʿ*) were two independent lexical items. Their joining — the general pattern *dār al-X* "house of X", as in Baghdad's Bayt al-Ḥikma — was an institutional-naming habit of Abbasid administration in the seventh and eighth centuries: a specific state function defined by *dār* + the name of the function. By the time the phrase reached the shipyard context, *dār aṣ-ṣināʿa* was not yet a proper noun but a category-name — *a state house of craft*. But when the first military shipyard at Basra is documented (c. 700-750), the phrase was already prepared to designate one type of institution. The next three centuries would watch a category-name become a proper noun. The word's appearance in al-Andalus, Tunisia, Sicily, Venice in the same form is the journey not of the word but of the institution — the word only accompanied it.
