Türkçe *nadir* bu mecazî yan-anlamı zaten dil-içinde bağımsız taşımadığı için modern Türkçede teknik astronomik terim olarak kalır; \"en dip nokta\" mecazına Türkçenin kendi sözcüğü vardır (dip, ucu, en aşağı). Arapça *nāẓir* (نَاظِر) modern bilimsel sözlükte hâlâ astronomik teknik anlamı taşır; mecazî dip-anlamı modern Arapça'da yaygın değil. Sözcüğün tek bir Arap-Latin geçişinden sonra Avrupa dillerinin gündelik kullanımında ekstra bir mecazî anlam edinmesi: filolojik olarak ilginç bir gözlem. *Bir uzayda görelilik sözcüğü, mecazi olarak tarihsel-bireysel dipnoktanın adı olur* — Arap astronomisinin epistemik açısı, Avrupa kullanımında psikolojik ve biyografik açıya dönüşür. Aynı sözcük, iki dil topluluğunda iki ayrı anlam evrenini taşır.
      en: |
        In modern English *nadir* works on two levels: (i) as an astronomy-and-navigation term, the antipode of the observer's overhead point (zenith), the point under the celestial sphere; (ii) in everyday language in the metaphorical sense of "the lowest point, the bottom-out, the worst moment" — "the nadir of his career." Turkish *nadir* carries no such metaphorical extension intrinsically, and remains in modern Turkish a technical astronomical term; for the bottom-out metaphor Turkish has its own native words (*dip*, *en aşağı*). Arabic *nāẓir* (نَاظِر) in the modern scientific lexicon still bears the astronomical-technical sense; the metaphorical bottom-out is not current in modern Arabic. The word, after a single Arab-Latin transit, acquires an additional metaphorical sense in the everyday usage of European languages: a philologically interesting fact. *A word of spatial relativity becomes metaphorically the name of a historical-individual low point* — the epistemic angle of Arab astronomy becomes a psychological and biographical angle in European usage. The same word carries, in two language-communities, two separate universes of meaning.

      en: |
        Geoffrey Chaucer's *Treatise on the Astrolabe* (c. 1391) — written only for his ten-year-old son Lewis, the first technical scientific text in English — records the word *nadir* as an astronomical technical term: "the nadyr is the point in the firmament that is directly under thi feet." All the technical terminology Chaucer uses to teach his son how to use the astrolabe — *azimuth*, *zenith*, *nadyr*, *almucantar*, *capricorn*, *aries* — passes from Arabic into Latin, from Latin into English; the English of Chaucer's child in the 1390s already speaks with a half-Arabic vocabulary of astronomy. The five-hundred-year history of this: the Baghdad-and-Toledo translations of the ninth and tenth centuries plant the Arabic astronomical-technical lexicon in Latin, the twelfth-century Toledo school of translators carries it in canonical form into European Latin astronomy, and from there it enters the fourteenth-century scientific texts of the national languages. Chaucer's *Treatise* is the first link in that chain to open in English.

      tr: |
        12. yy ortalarında Toledo çeviri okulunda Gerard of Cremona (1114-1187) Arap astronomik metinlerinin sistematik Latinceye çevrilmesini yürütür: 80'den fazla eser. El-Fergânî'nin *Cevâmiʿ ʿİlm en-Nücûm*'u (c. 850), Bettânî'nin *Zîc*'i, İbn Heysem'in optik eserleri Latinceye geçer. Tam burada *naẓīr al-samt* tek bir terim olarak Latinceye çevrilirken iki ayrı strateji uygulanır: *azimuth* (Arapça *as-samt*'ın Latince fonetik translit translit) ve *nadyr* (Arapça *naẓīr al-samt*'ın kısaltılmış translit; tam çevrilmez, sadece ilk ünsüz değişimi *ẓ* → *d*). Yani sözcük yarı-tercüme, yarı-fonetik-ödünç: Arapça anlam (\"karşı eş\") yerine Arapça sesin Latin damağına uydurulması. *Bir teknik terimin çevrilmek yerine ödünç alınması*; Latin Avrupa astronomları için *nadyr* artık \"Arapça'da bir şey demek olan, Latince'de teknik anlamı olmayan, ama astrolab kullanırken bilmeniz gereken bir nokta\"dır. Sözcüğün anlamsızlığı (Latin için) onu daha teknik kılar. Bir filolojik paradoks: yabancılığı, terimsel yetkinliğini garanti eder.
      en: |
        In the mid-twelfth century at the Toledo school of translation, Gerard of Cremona (1114-1187) conducted the systematic translation of Arabic astronomical texts into Latin: more than eighty works. Al-Farghānī's *Jawāmiʿ ʿIlm al-Nujūm* (c. 850), al-Battānī's *Zīj*, the optical works of Ibn al-Haytham cross into Latin. Just here *naẓīr al-samt* is taken into Latin as a single term by two distinct strategies: *azimuth* (Latin phonetic transliteration of Arabic *as-samt*) and *nadyr* (an abbreviated transliteration of Arabic *naẓīr al-samt*; not fully translated, only the first consonantal shift *ẓ* → *d*). The word is, that is, half-translation, half-phonetic-loan: in place of the Arabic meaning ("the counterpart") the Arab sound is fitted to the Latin palate. *A technical term taken on loan rather than translated*; for the Latin European astronomer *nadyr* is now "a thing that means something in Arabic, has no technical sense in Latin, but is a point you need to know when you use the astrolabe." The very meaninglessness of the word (for the Latin) makes it more technical. A philological paradox: its foreignness guarantees its terminological precision.

    body:
      tr: |
        9. yy Bağdat'ında astronomik koordinat sistemi olarak kullanılan "altmış-derecelik" sistem (yerel ufkun tam baş-üstü noktası = sıfır, ufuk üzerindeki tüm noktalar 0°-90°, eksiler tabloya alınmaz) iki ana referans noktasına dayanır: *as-samt* (السَّمت — zenith, baş üstü) ve *naẓīr al-samt* (نَظير السَّمت — nadir, ayak altı). Bu iki nokta birbirinin antipot tanımı; ikisi de "yıldız değil, gözlemcinin pozisyonuna göreceli bir koordinat çerçevesinin sıfır noktası." El-Fergânî'nin (c. 800-870, Bağdat) astronomi el kitabı *Cevâmiʿ ʿİlm en-Nücûm* (c. 850) bu sistemi sistematik olarak öğretir; aynı dönemde el-Hârezmî'nin (öl. 850) *Zîc el-Sind-Hind*'i Hint astronomik tablolarını Bağdat koordinat sistemine geçirir.         In ninth-century Baghdad, the astronomical coordinate system used (the "sexagesimal" system: the local horizon's overhead point = zero, all points above the horizon 0°-90°, negatives not tabulated) rests on two principal reference-points: *as-samt* (السَّمت — zenith, the overhead) and *naẓīr al-samt* (نَظير السَّمت — nadir, the underfoot).       en: |
        The Arabic root of *naẓīr* is *n-ẓ-r* "to look, to observe, to compare"; *naẓara* "he looked, observed", *naẓīr* "the opposite of the seer, the counterpart of the seen, that-which-is-compared." In pre-Islamic Arabia — without astronomy as a systematic science, but with the desert night-sky as the principal instrument of Bedouin orientation — there were already compound names like *naẓīr al-falāh* ("the counterpart of the dawn-star"). The Bedouin sky works through a referential system founded on "comparing star with star"; the positions of stars are defined in star-star relations, not absolute. This epistemic template — *to define a point by using another point* — when it passes into ninth-century Baghdad astronomy, makes the term *naẓīr al-samt* "the counterpart of the zenith" ready-made. The technical use of the word, that is, is a direct continuation of its use in the Bedouin observational epistemology; the technical abstraction is added on top of the everyday use. *The root of a technical term has its place as the everyday concept of an agrarian-observing civilisation.* The astrolabe was not in the pocket of the pre-Islamic Arab; but the word for the astrolabe was.
