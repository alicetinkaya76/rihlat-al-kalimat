      en: |
        In May 1747, the British naval surgeon James Lind, aboard HMS
        Salisbury, ran one of modern medicine's first controlled clinical
        trials: he assigned twelve scorbutic sailors to six pairs, each
        receiving a different treatment — cider, sulphuric acid, vinegar,
        seawater, a spiced pill, and *two oranges and one lemon*. Within
        six days the result was clean: the two sailors on citrus
        returned to duty; the other ten were still ill. By the time
        *A Treatise of the Scurvy* appeared in 1753, Lind did not know
        what scurvy actually was — the concept of vitamin C lay almost
        two centuries away — but he had observed correctly that the
        fruit prevented it.

        The Royal Navy instituted a daily lemon-juice ration for sailors
        in 1795, almost half a century late. Scurvy — a disease that
        could reduce a ship's company by a third on an Atlantic crossing
        — disappeared from naval service records within a few years.
        *Lemoneer*, *limey* — the slang epithet by which the British
        sailor would later be known — is a by-product of that decision:
        the British navy's identification with the lemon turned a
        geopolitical advantage into the name of a fruit.

        lemon orchards of Sicily (not the Caribbean — the lemon is not
        sugarcane, it wants a temperate climate) shift from being a
        Genoa-and-Messina export monopoly into a colonial economy.
        Britain, securing its supply, invested in Palestine and Jaffa

        become an empire's health policy.
