      en: |
        The 1767 publication by Nevil Maskelyne (1732-1811) of *The Nautical Almanac and Astronomical Ephemeris* was born as one of the solutions to modern navigation's longitude problem. To know a ship's east-west position (its longitude) one needed to know in real time the positions of the stars and the moon according to Greenwich time; the *Almanac* is exactly the annual, precomputed schedule of this calculation. Maskelyne inherits the word from the *Opus Majus* — Roger Bacon had already used the same word in the sense of a table in 1267.

        The *Almanac* of the eighteenth century already carried a known link to its Arab-Andalusian source. The modern history of science (Ramsay's *The Nautical Almanac*, 1979) shows that Maskelyne's marginal note *Tabula Almanachae* refers to the thirteenth-century Toledan Tables and to their Andalusian sources. In this century the word becomes a *technical category*; while the ordinary public uses *almanac* as an annual agricultural forecast (the *Old Farmer's Almanac*, begun in the USA in 1792), the scientific community continues to use it for the star-table. This double use persists to the present day.

      en: |
        Roger Bacon (c. 1219-1292), the Oxford Franciscan theologian and natural philosopher, wrote his *Opus Majus* (Great Work) in 1267 at the request of Pope Clement IV. In Part III he defends what the modern history of science has called "Bacon's astronomical method": that precomputed tables of stellar, lunar and solar positions are mandatory for ecclesiastical calendar reckoning (the accurate finding of the date of Easter in particular). He calls these tables *Almanac* — direct evidence of his contact with the Arabic astronomical literature.

        Although Bacon could not read Arabic, he was taught from Arabic works through the Latin translations; in his *Opus Majus* he refers directly to the works of al-Farghānī (Alfraganus), Ibn al-Haytham (Alhazen) and al-Biṭrūjī (Alpetragius). The word *Almanac* comes from this Latin translation channel: the twelfth-century Toledo workshop preserves the term *al-manākh* not as *tabula astronomica* but as *almanac* (the Arabic article *al-* is kept as part of the word in its Latin passage — as in *azimuth*, *algorithm*, *alembic*). Bacon makes this Latin form permanent in the sense of an astronomical table; the word circulates with this sense in the European astronomical literature for the next six hundred years.

        In Toledo around 1085, al-Zarqālī (1029-1100, in Latin *Azarquiel*) compiled an annual table entitled *Almanaque del año 1085* as the most mature product of the Andalusian astronomy school. This is not merely a calendar — it is the annually-renewed version of the broader astronomical table that modern historians of science call the *Toledan Tables*. al-Zarqālī's observational instrument, the *ṣafīḥa al-zīj* (the table-plate), represents the technical peak of Andalusian astronomy; at the same time it is the central work the twelfth-century Toledo translation workshop will export to the rest of Europe.

        Here the word *manākh* has settled as a technical term in Arab astronomy, but the context had matured over the previous century: in Cairo Ibn Yūnus's *Zīj al-Ḥākimī* (c. 1010), in Cordoba Maslama al-Majrīṭī's adaptation of the Khwārizmīan tables to the latitude of al-Andalus (c. 1010), and in Toledo with al-Zarqālī's synthesis, these three centres founded a canon of astronomical tables. *al-manākh* is the shared name of this canon — as a sister concept to *zīj* (the annual star-table) it carries the layers of meaning of climate, a place of staying, a table standing full and ready to wait.

Bu ayrım Endülüs astronomları tarafından korunur ve 13. yüzyılda Latin'e *almanac* olarak geçen kelimenin anlam dokusunun temelini oluşturur. Modern *almanac*'ın "yıllık tablo" anlamı buradaki *manākh*'tan gelir; "iklim" anlamı *manākh*'ın paralel anlam katmanından (durulan yer, ikamet) dallanmış bir başka iz.
      en: |
        Ibn Yūnus (c. 950-1009), the court astronomer of Fāṭimid Egypt, compiled the *Zīj al-Ḥākimī al-Kabīr* (The Great Ḥākimī Table) under the patronage of Caliph al-Ḥākim (985-1021). This work in 81 chapters contains trigonometric tables to a decimal degree for the positions of the stars, the moon and the sun, and mathematical corrections for observational error; modern historians of science (Roughton 1971) accept it as the most accurate astronomical table since the *Almagest*.

        The term *al-manākh* is not born here; it had long been in use in pre-Islamic Arab astrological literature as a sister concept of *manzil al-qamar* (the lunar mansion, *nakṣatra*). But Ibn Yūnus uses *al-manākh* as a technical chit distinct from *zīj*: a *zīj* is a dynamic calculation table (giving the annual positions of the sun and the moon by continually updated formulas); *manākh* is more static, a schedule showing the succession of the lunar mansions and of the star groups. This distinction is preserved by the Andalusian astronomers and forms the basis of the semantic texture of the word that will pass into Latin as *almanac* in the thirteenth century. The "annual table" sense of modern *almanac* comes from the *manākh* here; the "climate" sense is another trace, branched from the parallel layer of meaning in *manākh* (a place of staying, residence).
