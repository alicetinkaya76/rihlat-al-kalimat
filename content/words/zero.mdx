Peano's axiom system of 1889 took zero as the *initial point* of the natural numbers: remove zero and counting has no place to begin.

        This refoundation became a tangible thing a half-century later in Turing's abstract machine (1936): a blank cell on a paper tape, a 0 bit in computer memory — every number resting on a layer of emptiness underneath. <em>Zero</em> was no longer merely a digit; it was a *foundation*.

The lexical trace of the strife was the twin sense of <em>cifra</em>: at once *void* (the zero-digit) and *concealed writing* (a script hidden in numerals — *cipher*). Before the prohibition, zero was a symbol; under it, *zero became a suspicion*.

      en: |
        The book that carried the Hindu-Arabic decimal system to Latin Europe was al-Khwārizmī's *Kitāb al-Ḥisāb al-Hindī*, whose Arabic original is lost. Around 1145, Adelard of Bath's Latin translation entered Christian Europe as *Liber Algorismi de numero indorum*. The Arabic word the book used — <span class="term" lang="ar">صِفْر</span>, "void" — fell into a medieval Latin pen as *cifra* (likely pronounced with a soft *c*, *tʃifra*). Across three centuries the word forked.

        One branch landed in the Italian abacist tradition: *cifra* drifted into Venetian dialect as *zefiro / zefro / zevero*; in the fifteenth century it shortened into *zero* (around the time of Luca Pacioli's *Summa* of 1494). In the sixteenth century English borrowed <em>zero</em> from this branch — most likely through the French *zéro*. The other branch ran through French: *cifra* entered Old French as *chiffre*, which still means "digit" in modern French and acquired the sense "code" along the way. By the fourteenth century English had borrowed this branch too: <em>cipher</em>, originally just "the digit zero," then "writing concealed in numerals."       en: |
        In 628 CE at Bhillamāla, Brahmagupta's <em>Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta</em> gave zero its first complete arithmetic — addition, subtraction, the rules for zero's own behaviour, all written down here for the first time. The Sanskrit *śūnya* ("empty") here stopped being merely a word for absence and became a *quantity*. Brahmagupta took one further step: zero too should hold a place value, the tenth member of a ten-digit system. In the Indian astronomical tradition of Ujjain — through to Bhāskara II in the 1150s — zero was at home.

        Two centuries later in Baghdad, when al-Khwārizmī wrote his *Kitāb al-Ḥisāb al-Hindī*, he chose an Arabic equivalent for the Sanskrit *śūnya*: <span class="term" lang="ar">صِفْر</span> — "empty, bare, nothing." The meaning was a perfect fit; the word is a *calque*, not a phonetic borrowing. The choice held: twelve centuries later, Turkish *sıfır*, Latin *cifra*, Italian *zero*, French *chiffre* — all are descendants of this Baghdad–India junction. Further back, two millennia back, the cuneiform tablets of Babylon had already known place-value writing — but they left only an *empty space* where zero should have stood. A ghost without a sign. India gave the ghost a body; Baghdad gave it an Arabic name.
