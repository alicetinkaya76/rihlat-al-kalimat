      en: |
        Marco Polo (c. 1254-1324), in his *Il Milione* (composed 1298, dictated in the Genoese prison to Rusticello da Pisa), in narrating his eastern journeys, describes Mosul (in the book *Mosul* or *Musul*) as a textile-production centre. In the Latin European copies of the work, *mussolina* (the Mosul cloth) becomes standard in Italian; through the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries it appears in the trade-ledgers of Venice, Genoa and Marseille as a Levantine cloth category. In his *Pratica della mercatura* (c. 1340), Francesco Pegolotti notes that the Mosul cloth is a fine cotton with gold and silver embroidery and that its price is very high in proportion to its weight.

        In the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, with the decline of Mosul production after the Ottoman conquest, the term *mussolina* / *mousseline* loses its geographical meaning and becomes a generic name for "fine transparent cotton cloth". After 1750 the fine Bengal cottons coming from India to Europe were sold under the same epithet *muslin* — in place of the original Mosul-sourced cloth, but of the same weaving quality. The transition of the name from its geographical sense to a generic-class name is a typical pattern of the modern European textile vocabulary: *damask*, *satin*, *Cordoba*, *Madras* — all have the same transition.

      en: |
        Mosul (Arabic *al-Mawṣil*), on the western bank of the Tigris, is the centre of Upper Mesopotamia. The eighth-to-thirteenth-century Abbasid period is Mosul's golden age: the *al-Jazīra* region between Baghdad and Mosul — ideal for cotton and linen cultivation — works textiles; the adjectival construction *mawṣilī* names a fine cotton-linen blend. In al-Maqdisī's *Aḥsan al-taqāsīm fī maʿrifat al-aqālīm* (c. 985) the distribution of Mosul textiles in the markets of Baghdad, Aleppo and Damascus is recorded; the word *mawṣilī* names both the source of the raw material and the weaving technique.

        The Saljuq and Atabeg period (twelfth-and-thirteenth centuries) is the golden age of textile production: the Zangid atabegs (1127-1262) rule Mosul as both a military and an economic centre; *mawṣilī* becomes a standardised category of the luxury market. In Ibn al-Athīr's *al-Kāmil fī al-tārīkh* (c. 1210) the share of textile revenue in the tax-value of Mosul is conspicuous. It is during this Arab-Atabeg golden age — contemporaneously with the Crusades — that Mediterranean traders carried *mawṣilī* into Europe.

      en: |
        Mesopotamia's textile history reaches back to the third millennium BC: Sumerian economic tablets (the Ur III period, 2100-2000 BC) describe systematic wool and linen weaving in temple and palace workshops. In the Assyrian and Babylonian periods *Naharain* (the land between two rivers, i.e. Upper Mesopotamia) was known for its fine linen and wool products; *anaku* (the fine/translucent) linen was a luxury textile-class name in the Assyrian royal correspondences.

        The city of Mosul (in modern archaeological excavations, directly across from the Assyrian capital Nineveh, as a Sasanian settlement layer) operated as a node of the Sasanian textile network. The Arab conquest (637) took over this Sasanian textile industry; the textile-workers were largely the Christian Assyrian population, and the weaving techniques carried through unchanged from the Sasanian period. That is, the fame of Mosul textile in the Arab period is the last link of a millennium-long industrial continuity; behind the word *mawṣilī* lies the continuous production tradition of Mesopotamian textile from Sumer onward.
