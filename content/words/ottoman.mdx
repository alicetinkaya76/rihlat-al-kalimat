      en: |
        In 1729, the Ottoman Sultan Ahmed III's ambassador Yirmisekiz Çelebi Mehmed Efendi's visit to Paris (contact with Voltaire and Madame de Pompadour) — the birth-point of the *Turquerie* (Turkish-style) fashion-current in the French aristocratic salons of the early eighteenth century. *Turquerie* — beginning in the boudoirs of Marie-Antoinette at Versailles and spreading to the aristocratic residences of Paris, London and Vienna — integrated Turkish cushions and Ottoman palace-furniture elements into European-style upholstery, with the wall-panelling. The Turkish *sedir* (the wall-running cushioned sitting platform, the standard element of the Turkish house) was reduced, in French workshops, to the chest-bodied, hinged, cushioned small footstool and sold as *un ottoman* / *une ottomane*.

        From the mid-eighteenth century, the term *ottoman* entered the standard category-lists of French interior-architecture workshops like Boulle, Cressent and Rœntgen; in the nineteenth century it passed into Victorian Britain, taking its place in "Turkish style" / "Eastern style" furniture catalogues. There is an interesting historical irony: in the Turko-Ottoman original, this small footstool did not bear that name — the word *otoman* later entered the modern Turkish furniture market as a re-loan from French. The "from the Ottomans to Paris, then back to Turkey with a different meaning" pattern of the word is the typical case in the **boomerang etymology** category of modern comparative-lexicology.

        Osman Bey (Osman Ghazi, c. 1255-1326), Söğüt'te küçük bir Türk-beyliği kurucu-atası. Anadolu Selçuklu devletinin (1075-1308) çöküşü sürecinde, Bizans imparatorluğunun Anadolu'nun batı sınırına yakın küçük bir beylik — kendi adının altında nesilden nesile büyüyerek altı yüzyıllık imparatorluğun adının kaynağı oldu. Sözcük modelleri: Osmanlı Türkçesi *ʿOsmān oğulları* → *Osmānoğulları* → *Osmān-ī* → *Osmanlı* (Osman'a ait olan, Osmanlı hânedanına bağlı). Bu sözcük 14. yy itibariyle Türk-İslam dünyasının yönetim-pratiğinde standart kullanım haline geldi.

        Latince-İtalyanca-Yunanca komşu-devletlerin Osmanlı için adlandırması: Latince *Othomani* (15. yy ortası), İtalyanca *Ottomano* (15. yy sonu), Fransızca *Ottoman* (16. yy başı), İspanyolca *Otomano*. Coğrafi-kuşak: 15.-17. yy boyunca Osmanlı sınırının Avrupa'ya doğru ilerlemesi (Konstantinopolis 1453, Belgrad 1521, Mohaç 1526, Viyana kuşatması 1529, 1683) sözcüğün Avrupa dillerinde günlük-bilinçle yerleşmesinin tarihsel zeminidir.

        İlginç bir leksikolojik detay: 15.-17. yy Avrupa belgelerinde *Ottoman* sıklıkla "Turk" ile yan-yana ve eşanlamlı olarak kullanılır; modern akademik konvansiyon (Ottomanist tarihçiliği, 20. yy ikinci yarısı) bu iki terimi ayırdı — *Ottoman* hânedan/imparatorluk adı, *Turkish* dil/etnik kimlik adı. Bu ayrım modern Türk Cumhuriyeti'nin (1923) ulus-devlet inşasının terminolojik bir yansıması.
      en: |
        Osman Bey (Osman Ghazi, c. 1255-1326), the founder-ancestor of a small Turkish-beylik at Söğüt. In the process of the collapse of the Anatolian Saljuq state (1075-1308), a small beylik close to the western frontier of the Byzantine empire in Anatolia — growing from generation to generation under his name, it became the source of the name of a six-century empire. The word-models: Ottoman Turkish *ʿOsmān oğulları* → *Osmānoğulları* → *Osmān-ī* → *Osmanlı* (belonging to Osman, attached to the Ottoman dynasty). By the fourteenth century, the word had become standard use in the governance-practice of the Turko-Islamic world.

        The naming of the Ottomans by neighbouring Latin-Italian-Greek states: Latin *Othomani* (mid-fifteenth century), Italian *Ottomano* (late fifteenth century), French *Ottoman* (early sixteenth century), Spanish *Otomano*. The sound-change is a typical Romance sound-adjustment: the Turkish initial *o-* was preserved, the Turkish *s* sound was taken as Romance *t* (the Romance ear hears the *s* of the foreign tongue as inadequate and often replaces it with *t* or *th*). The geographical-band: through the fifteenth-seventeenth centuries, the advance of the Ottoman frontier into Europe (Constantinople 1453, Belgrade 1521, Mohács 1526, the sieges of Vienna 1529 and 1683) is the historical ground of the word's settling, in everyday-consciousness, in the European languages.

        An interesting lexicographic detail: in the fifteenth-seventeenth-century European documents, *Ottoman* is often used side-by-side with and synonymous to "Turk"; modern academic convention (Ottomanist historiography, the second half of the twentieth century) distinguished the two terms — *Ottoman* as the dynastic/imperial name, *Turkish* as the linguistic/ethnic-identity name. That distinction is a terminological reflection of the modern Turkish Republic's (1923) nation-state construction.
