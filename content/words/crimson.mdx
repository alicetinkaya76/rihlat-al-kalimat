      en: |
        The kermes insect (Kermes vermilio) is a small female-dwelling beetle of the Mediterranean-basin oak; in its body it carries carminic acid (in modern terms). In the ancient Mesopotamian, Egyptian and Greek worlds it was used as a dye source; named in Latin *coccus* (Greek *kókkos*, "grain, seed") — describing the soft body of the insect as a "seed" is an ancient mistake. In the Arab world, from the Persian *kirm* (worm, insect) was derived *qirmiz*; *qirmizī* "of kermes" → "kermes-dyed" → "bright red".

        Through the ninth-to-thirteenth century, Damascus, Aleppo, Acre and Sidon — the Levantine cities — were the largest kermes-dyeing centres of the Mediterranean world. In Ibn al-ʿAwwām's *Kitāb al-Filāha* (twelfth century) and Ibn al-Bayṭār's *al-Jāmiʿ li-mufradāt al-adwiya wa-al-aghdhiya* (thirteenth century), the harvest technique, the dye-house processing and the quality-grading of the kermes insect are fully described. This dye was the most expensive colour category; it was used only in imperial-court and ecclesiastical ritual vesture. The commercial importance of Acre in the Crusader period lay in its being the centre of kermes-dyeing in the textile market.

      en: |
        The prestige red dye of the Mediterranean world came from two parallel sources: (i) the Mediterranean-basin kermes of the oak (modern *Kermes vermilio*) — eastern Mediterranean, central Anatolia, the Levant; (ii) the Polish-kermes of the Polish-Ukrainian plains (*Porphyrophora polonica*) — the Slavic world. The two insects give almost the same carminic-acid dye. The ancient Mesopotamian (Sumero-Akkadian economic tablets, c. 2000 BC) lists kermes-dyed wool as Babylonian court vesture; with the "imperial purple" dyeing of Tyre (the Phoenician *murex*-snail dyeing), kermes-red was one of the two main luxury-colour systems of the ancient eastern Mediterranean.

        In the Roman imperial period, *coccinata* (kermes-dyed) textile is the standard of senatorial and imperial-court vesture. The Byzantine period institutionalised the dye-monopoly under state control. After the Arab conquest (seventh century) kermes-dyeing was terminologically standardised in the court-textile vocabulary of the Islamic world as *qirmizī*; behind modern Europe's *crimson* stands this three-thousand-year Mediterranean dye-history.
