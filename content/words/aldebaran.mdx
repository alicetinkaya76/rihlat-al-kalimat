      en: |

This Bayer naming adds a technical coordinate alongside the Arabic star names, but does not displace them — in the modern *Astronomical Almanac* both appear together with equal weight. Early-modern European astronomy *neither rejects nor displaces* the Arab canon; it places it inside a numerical system.

      en: |
        ʿAbd al-Raḥmān al-Ṣūfī (903-986), the court astronomer of the Buwayhid ruler ʿAḍud al-Dawla, completed his *Kitāb Ṣuwar al-Kawākib al-Thābita* (The Book of the Pictures of the Fixed Stars) at Shiraz around 964. This is not a translation of Ptolemy's Greek *Almagest* star catalogue — it is a *redrawing*. al-Ṣūfī checks the Greek *Hipparchian* coordinates for each constellation, adds the parallel names from the Bedouin Arab astronomical tradition, and draws each star picture in two senses (as seen from earth and reversed on the celestial sphere).

        For Taurus al-Ṣūfī gives two layers of naming: from the Greek canon *Boōtēs* (the Heart of the Bull, *Cor Tauri*); and from the Bedouin Arab tradition *al-dabarān* (the follower of the Pleiades). Why was the second name chosen? Because in the old calendar of Mesopotamia and Arabia the rising of the Pleiades was the beginning of the rainy season, and the rising of *al-dabarān* just behind them was an observational sign used to confirm the position of the cluster. al-Ṣūfī's work passed by the eleventh century to Cairo, to Baghdad, to al-Andalus, and from there through Sicily to Latin Europe; with each crossing *al-dabarān* (deformed into Aldebaran) was kept as a permanent technical name.

      en: |
        al-Ṣūfī did not invent the name *al-dabarān* in 964 — he *recorded* it. The name itself was present in the Mesopotamian-Arabian astronomical-calendrical tradition at least two thousand years earlier. The dawn rising of the Pleiades (*al-thurayyā*) on the eastern horizon in May was the official sign of the beginning of the rainy season / planting time from the Sumerian calendar (2000 BCE) to the Akkadian, through the Babylonian MUL.APIN tablets (1100 BCE), and on to the ancient Bedouin Arab calendar. The rising of *al-dabarān* just behind it was the confirmatory secondary-sign of this sign — if an observer could not say the Pleiades had risen, he would look for al-dabarān; the two together were the confirmation of the seasonal change.

        On the Babylonian MUL.APIN tablets the star is listed as *Ku₆.A.MEŠ* ("fish-follower"); it is translated by Akkadian *šitaddaru* ("the one following"). In ancient Bedouin Arab star-lore (Ibn Qutayba's ninth-century *Kitāb al-Anwāʾ* compiles this oral tradition) the star already carries the name *al-dabarān* — from the root *dbr* ("to follow, to come after"). The only thing al-Ṣūfī did was to place this name *inside a technical star catalogue*, to assign formal coordinates to it, and to propose it as the equal canon of the Greek *Almagest*. The true genealogy of the modern Aldebaran is a thousand years between Greenwich-Toledo-Shiraz, and another thousand between Shiraz and Babylon.
