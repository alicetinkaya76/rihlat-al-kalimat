  en: "*literally:* a single drink, a swallow. Arabic *sharbat* (شَربات) — from the same *sh-r-b* root as *sharāb* (a drink) — built on *sharbah* (a single sip, a draught); classically *a sweet, cold fruit drink taken at one sitting*. In the Ottoman court of the fifteenth to seventeenth centuries it became the central beverage of court protocol (alcohol-free, observing the Hanafi prohibition kept inside the imperial setting). From there it entered Italian as *sorbetto* through sixteenth-century Venetian diplomacy, French *sorbet* at seventeenth-century Versailles, English *sherbet*. **Note:** an instance of the one-word-two-corridors discipline, the same Arabic root having reached Latin earlier and in a narrower medical sense as *syrupus* (see <em>syrup</em>)."

      en: |
        Procopio Cutò (Francesco Procopio dei Coltelli, 1651-1727), Sicilian-born and brought to Versailles, opened the first café-pâtisserie in Paris — Café Procope, 1686 — and established *sorbet* as a service. Cutò's innovation lay in cooling technology: brine-and-ice mixtures to freeze the dishes, the fruit-flavoured drink then served half-frozen. From that date *sorbet* parted ways with *sharbat*: no longer a *drink*, but a *half-ice*. The shift added an important element to the European diplomatic table — the *entremet* (between-courses) serving in which *sorbet* cleansed the palate. Eighteenth-century European ambassadors noted in their travel diaries that "if the Turks saw how differently we made this, they would be astonished." The same word, in two geographies, two distinct things: Turkish *şerbet* liquid and cold, European *sorbet* half-solid and frozen.

      en: |
        *Sharbat / sharbah* derives in classical Arabic from the same root as *sharāb* (sh-r-b) but with a different morphological pattern — *sharbah* meaning "a single drink, a single swallow" (the *faʿla* pattern). The word is older than either the medical or the courtly setting; it is alive in pre-Islamic poetry, and in the Qurʾān: *Sūrat al-Insān* (76:21) gives the inhabitants of Paradise *sharāban ṭahūrā* (a purified drink). The classical exegetical tradition kept the distinction between *sharāb* (a drink in general) and *sharbat / sharbah* (specifically a fruit-flavoured, sweetened, or cold draught). In the ninth-century Baghdad pharmacy workshop *sharāb* was medicalised (and later became Latin *syrupus* through Salerno); *sharbah / sharbat* held its social-table sense and settled at the Ottoman court. *Two siblings, two corridors, two European memories.*
