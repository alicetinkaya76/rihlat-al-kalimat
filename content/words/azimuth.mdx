A century on, the survey of the Paris meridian by Cassini and Méchain seated *azimuth angle* at the foundations of geodetic triangulation. The navigators of the same century pulled the word into the compass's vocabulary: *true* azimuth (relative to geographic north) and *magnetic* azimuth (relative to compass north) became the two halves of the equation by which a ship's true position could be computed. A word that had left the astronomer's lexicon now served three trades at once — astronomer, navigator, engineer.

        Cremona'lı Gerard (öl. 1187) ve Tuleytula'nın iki dilli çevirmen atölyeleri, Arapça astronomi metinlerini Latinceye taşırken her tekniksel terimle aynı çıkmazla karşılaştılar: harf-i tarifle birlikte mi, ondan ayrı mı yazılmalıydı? Çoğu kelimede *al-*'ı isim gövdesine yapıştırarak tek bir Latince kelime yarattılar — yedinci yüzyıldaki Norman'ın denizde yaptığını çevirmen burada satırda yaptı. *al-sumūt* (السموت), önce *azimut* sonra *azimuth* oldu. Arapçada bir noktanın ufuktaki açısının çoğul hâliyle gelmesi — birden çok yöne uzanan bir kavramı belirtmek için — bu doğuşta düşürüldü; Latin kelime tekildir, yalnız bir açıyı işaret eder. Çevirinin tortusudur: nesnenin kendisi değişmez, ama gramatik şekli iki dilin arasında erir.
      en: |
        Gerard of Cremona (d. 1187) and the bilingual translation workshops of Toledo, working through Arabic astronomy texts in Latin, faced one and the same problem with every technical term: was the article to be written attached to the noun, or set apart from it? With most words they chose to fuse the *al-* onto the noun stem and produce a single Latin word — what the Norman ear had done at sea, the translator's pen did in the line. *al-sumūt* (السموت) became first *azimut* and then *azimuth*. The plural form in which an Arabic horizon-angle was given — a plural that named a class of possible bearings — was dropped at birth; the Latin word is singular and names exactly one angle. It is the sediment of translation: the object itself stays the same, but its grammatical shape melts between two tongues.
      ar: |
        وقفَ جيرارد الكريمونيُّ (تُوُفِّيَ 1187) وحَلَقاتُ التَّرجمةِ ثُنائيّةُ اللغة في طُلَيطِلَة، وهم يَنقلون نُصوصَ الفَلَك العربيّةَ إلى اللاتينيّة، أمامَ المَأزقِ نفسِه مع كلِّ مُصطلَحٍ تِقَنيّ: أَتُكتَب أداةُ التعريف ملصقةً بالاسم أم منفصلةً عنه؟ في معظم الألفاظ اختاروا أن يُلصِقوا الـ*أل-* بجَذعِ الاسم فيَخرُجَ بكلمةٍ لاتينيّةٍ واحدة — ما فعَلتْه الأُذُنُ النُّورمانديّةُ في البحر، فعَلتْه يَدُ المترجِم في السَّطر. صارت *السموت* أوّلًا *azimut* ثمّ *azimuth*. أمّا صيغةُ الجَمع التي كانت تُعطى بها زاويةُ الأُفُقِ العربيّةُ — جَمعٌ يُسمِّي فئةً من الاتّجاهاتِ المُمكِنة — فقد سَقطتْ عند الولادةِ؛ الكلمةُ اللاتينيّةُ مُفرَدةٌ تُسمّي زاويةً واحدةً بعَيْنها. تلك تَورُسُ التَّرجمة: لا يَتبدَّلُ الموضوعُ نفسُه، لكن شكلَهُ النحويَّ يَذوبُ بين اللُّغتَين.

      en: |
        At the Bayt al-Ḥikma in Baghdad, al-Battānī's *Kitāb az-Zīj* (d. 929), and a century and a half later, in Andalusian Toledo, al-Zarqālī's *az-Zīj aṭ-Ṭulayṭulī* (d. 1100) — one fusing the Sasanian *zīj* tradition with Ptolemy's *Almagest*, the other re-grounding the old computation in the latitude of Toledo — fixed *samt* as a term of art. Of the two numbers an instrument (quadrant, later astrolabe) gave back when sighted on a star, one was the *samt* and the other the *irtifāʿ* (altitude). The word was not an abstraction held alone: it pointed to a line on a brass plate held in the hand, an eye, a calculation. The plural *as-sumūt* appears often, because astronomy works not on a direction but on a table of them.

      tr: |
        Arapçada *samt* kelimesi astronominin terimi olmadan çok önce, gündelik dilde "yol, patika, yön" anlamlarını taşıyordu. Pre-İslamî şiir, bir yolcunun "vâdînin samtına" yöneldiğini söyler; klasik tefsir, *samt* sözcüğünü "doğru, dürüst yol" mecazıyla da anar. Astronomi terimine dönüşmesi, yedinci-sekizinci yüzyıllarda Arap-İran bilim çevirisinin Yunan-Hint terimlerini Arapça mukabilleriyle eşleştirmesiyle gerçekleşti: ufuk üzerinde bir yön gerek olunca, hâlihazırda elde olan en uygun kelime *samt* idi. Önce çobandı, sonra astronom oldu.
      en: |
        Long before *samt* became a term of astronomy, ordinary Arabic carried it as "way, path, bearing." Pre-Islamic poetry says a traveller bent his course "to the *samt* of the valley"; classical commentary uses *samt* metaphorically for a *straight* (i.e. upright) way of life. Its passage into astronomy belonged to the seventh- and eighth-century translation activity of the Arabic-Persian scholarly world, which paired Greek and Indian astronomical terms with their nearest Arabic equivalents: when a bearing along the horizon was wanted, the readiest word in hand was *samt*. First a shepherd's word, then an astronomer's.
      ar: |
        قَبلَ أن تَصيرَ *سَمتٌ* مُصطلَحًا فَلَكيًّا بزمنٍ طويل، كانتِ العربيّةُ تَحمِلُها في الكلامِ اليوميِّ بمعنى الطريق والاتّجاهِ والمسلَك. يقول الشِّعرُ ما قَبلَ الإسلاميُّ إنّ المُسافِرَ أمَّ *سَمتَ الوادي*؛ ويَستعمِلُها التفسيرُ الكلاسيكيُّ مَجازًا للطريقِ المستقيم، أي صَلاحِ السُّلوك. أمّا انتقالُها إلى الفَلَك فقد تَمَّ في حركةِ التَّرجمةِ العربيّةِ-الفارسيّةِ في القَرنَين السابع والثامن، حين قابلوا الألفاظَ اليونانيّةَ والهنديّةَ بأقربِها في العربيّة: حين احتاجوا اتّجاهًا على الأُفُق، كانتْ *سَمتٌ* أَوْلى ما تَحتَ اليَد. كلمةُ الراعي قَبلًا، كلمةُ الفَلَكيِّ بَعدًا.
