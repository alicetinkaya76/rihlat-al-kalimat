      en: |
        When the twelfth-century Toledo translations (Gerard of Cremona) and the lingua franca of the Cremonese traders carried the word *al-lāzaward* into Latin Europe, the product itself (the Badakhshan lapis-lazuli pigment) was carried along with it. With the *al-* article dropping (a tendency, in the Romance ear, for the Arabic article to separate from the word), the word settled in the form *azur* / *azurum* instead of *lāzaward* — distinct from parallel cases like alkali / alembic / alchemy that retain the *al-*.

        In the thirteenth-sixteenth-century Renaissance workshops, the ultramarine pigment (Latin *ultramarinus*, "beyond-the-sea", because it came from Badakhshan via the Mediterranean trader) was more expensive than gold — by the gramme. In Cennino Cennini's *Il libro dell'arte* (c. 1390), the price-list cost-note for ultramarine: "*More expensive than the sultan's gold, because it comes from the other end of the world.*" Masters such as Giotto, Fra Angelico, Titian and Raphael used ultramarine only to paint the garments of Christ, the Virgin and important saints — an economic-symbolic limit. Modern X-ray fluorescence analysis has proved that the blue headscarf of Vermeer's *Milkmaid* (c. 1660) is ultramarine; half the cost of that painting was spent on pigment.

        Heraldry: in European medieval armorial-science *azure* became the official name of the blue colour-category (from the twelfth century onwards); together with *gules* (red), *sable* (black), *vert* (green), *purpure* (purple), and *or* (gold), *argent* (silver), the six standard heraldry-colours/metals. These categories are still used by the same names in modern heraldry; in the colour-standards of modern diplomacy (the UN, NATO, civic-armorial design), modern *azure* descends directly from Renaissance European heraldry.

      en: |
        In the colour-palette of the Arab-Islamic visual-art tradition (eighth-fifteenth centuries), *al-lāzaward* had a special place. The mosaics of the mihrab-arches of the Cordoba Mosque (785-988), the pigment-contrast of the muqarnas ceilings of the Court of the Lions at the Alhambra (1370-90), the gold-and-blue miniature composition of the manuscript-workshops of Baghdad-Tabriz-Shiraz — in all of them the lapis-lazuli pigment was the basic visual-signature of Islamic aesthetics. The mineral-salt is ground and mixed with water and applied as a water-based colour; its permanence and brilliance stand out compared with other blue-pigments (Egyptian blue, cobalt, azurite-copper).

        The knowledge-practice of pigment-production is in the written standards of the Arab alchemists: al-Rāzī's *Kitāb al-Asrār* (c. 925) explains the purification process of lapis-lazuli in detail (grinding, water-based filtration, the magnetic separation of the iron-content, lime-based cleaning). Ibn al-Bayṭār's *Kitāb al-Jāmiʿ* (thirteenth century) also documents sources of lapis-lazuli other than Badakhshan (Chile, the Sinai peninsula). In al-Andalus, in al-Zubaydī's *Lubāb al-ʿArab fī ʿIlm al-Adab*, the *lāzaward*-blue image of the Arab heraldic-narratives is a standard symbolic category.

        Arab-Islamic pigment-production was not in the monopoly of a single trader: from the Badakhshan mine to Persia, and from there through Baghdad-Damascus-Cairo to al-Andalus and the eastern Mediterranean, numerous Arab-Perso-Jewish-Christian traders worked in parallel within a distributed trade-network. The knowledge-archive of the lapis-lazuli trade is read in modern comparative-history studies (Olga Davidson 2003, Norman Yoffee 2010) as one of the table-instances of Islamic-Mediterranean economic history: a single mineral, a production-and-trade network spread over four continents, an economic stability of eight centuries.

      en: |
        In the ancient world, colour-naming developed not so much in objective abstract-categories like "blue" as through the naming of concrete-materials. The Sumerian *zaginu* (the blue-stone) refers to the mineral lapis-lazuli, while at the same time naming the colour "sky-blue"; the Akkadian *uqnû* (blue-precious-stone) carries the same double-function. This ancient linguistic-practice is read in modern science as the precursor of the anthropological colour-meaning studies (Brent Berlin & Paul Kay's *Basic Color Terms*, 1969).

        The Persian *lāzaward* is the continuator of this ancient world-practice: the name both of the concrete mineral (the lapis-lazuli mine at Badakhshan) and of the abstract colour (the sky-blue tone). This double-function passed into the Arabic *al-lāzaward* via Baghdad, from there through Islamic-Andalusia into Latin Europe; in Europe in the modern period the colour-function and the mineral-function eventually separated (*azure* the colour-name, *lapis lazuli* the mineral-name), but the underlying conceptual unity is preserved — in modern official heraldry, *azure* still carries the common naming-logic with the mineral (lazurite).

The survival of a single Persian mine-name, as the final lexical-signature of the ancient Sumerian mineral-colour conceptual unity, in the modern technology-brand, is one of the case-studies of linguistic-archaeology's "concept-word continuity".
