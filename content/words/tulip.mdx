      en: |
        In the early seventeenth century the Dutch Republic grew rich quickly as the centre of global colonial-trade; the merchant class began to channel its new wealth into luxury consumption, and the *tulip* bulb became one of the principal products of that channel. The controlled technical character of bulb-cultivation (the limited stock coming from the Turkey-Istanbul-Habsburg triangle and being multiplied in Holland; the rare "broken" tulips of viral infection patterning) made the product a standard luxury-commodity. Between 1634 and 1637, futures-contracts on tulip-bulbs began trading at the merchant exchanges of Amsterdam, Haarlem and Hoorn; with the "forward sale" system, while the bulb was still in the soil, a virtual-trade was born in which the contract changed hands many times between buyer and seller.

        At the peak in February 1637: a single *Semper Augustus* bulb (Holland's rarest flower, with red-and-white flame-patterning) sold for 10,000 florins — the price of a luxury stone house around Amsterdam. In March 1637, a sudden collapse: in Haarlem a merchant found no buyer in one or two sales; the news spread across Holland, prices fell by 95%, the futures-contracts became worthless. In modern economic historiography, the Tulpenmanie is placed as *the first speculative bubble of the modern world* (Anne Goldgar's 2007 revisionist studies show that the real scale of the bubble was smaller, but its role in the history of discourse is undisputed). The survival of the word as the metaphor *tulip mania* / *tulip bubble* in modern speech is the semantic residue of that event of 1637.

      en: |
        Ogier Ghiselin de Busbecq (1522-1592), as the ambassador of the Habsburg Emperor Ferdinand I to Sultan Süleyman (the Lawgiver), served in Istanbul from 1554 to 1562. While visiting Süleyman's campaign-tent at Edirne, in front of a red flower shown by the sultan's aide-de-camp-gardener, the ambassador asked the word. The Turkish-speaking interpreter explained that *the flower looked like a tülbent* (a turban-tie, a head-covering cloth) — in Turkish the actual name of the flower was *lâle* (taken from Persian literature); the interpreter used the word *tülbent* for the *form-description* of the flower. Busbecq noted the word down as the name of the flower.

        On his return to Vienna in 1562, Busbecq brought tulip bulbs with him; the first tulip garden of Europe was established in the old Habsburg palace. In 1573, the botanist of that garden, Carolus Clusius (1526-1609) — one of the founding names of European botanical science — took the bulbs to the Leiden University Botanical Garden. The Dutch tulip culture was born from Clusius's Leiden garden. The first written European record of the word is in Conrad Gessner's *Historia plantarum* of 1581: "*tulipan persicum* — the Persian turban-flower". European botanical literature never used the word *lâle*; it always carried the trace of Busbecq's 1554 misunderstanding.

        The typical irony of knowledge-transmission: from the Persian world (the original rose-garden flower, *lāle*) → to the Turkish-Ottoman world (the palace flower, still *lâle*) → to Europe (the misnamed *tulip*). A single Persian word, *dulband* (turban-tie), passed into two distinct worlds in two distinct forms: on the one side still a clothing-accessory (European *turban*), on the other mistakenly a flower-name (European *tulip*).       en: |
        Ottoman palace-gardening lived its technical golden age in the cultivation of *lâle* (*Tulipa schrenkii*, the wild Anatolian tulip, and *T. clusiana*, the Persian tulip, and their hybrids) between the fifteenth and seventeenth centuries. The Has-bahçe (Privy Garden) of Topkapı palace, the Cool-Fountain Garden at the Edirne palace, the gardens of the Bursa palaces, were modelled as organised tulip gardens. In Süleyman's Topkapı garden, in the mid-sixteenth century, more than 1500 tulip-varieties were registered (in the illuminated garden-registers of the palace miniaturists). This variety-diversity is a technical gardener's-production knowledge: the annual reproduction of tulip bulbs (each bulb makes about two or three new bulbs each year), grafting, the organisation of space, the management of flowering periods.

        An important detail of the word: in Turkish the name of the flower was *lâle* (taken from Persian, from the thirteenth-fourteenth-century Seljuk Anatolian Persian literature); *tülbent* was never used as a flower-name. *Tülbent* was the name of the Ottoman military turban-binding cloth — a soft cotton-linen blend, with a colour-pattern indicating military rank. The threefold distribution of the Persian *dulband* into Turkish settled over the fourteenth-to-sixteenth century: modern Turkish *tülbent* (a gauze-like thin cotton cloth), *türban* (for the modern hijab), *sarık* (the traditional military-religious head-cover).

        The next step: the Lâle Devri (the Tulip Era, 1718-1730) — the political and bodily summit of the tulip palace-culture in the reign of Ahmed III and the Grand Vizier Damat İbrahim Pasha. Tulip-cultivation contests, lacquer-decorated palace ornamentation, tulip-imagery in architectural motifs, literary descriptions ("opening like a tulip", "the tulip stained with blood"). In that age the Turkish word *lâle* condensed in botanical, literary and politically-symbolic senses. A century after Europe's Tulpenmanie, the Ottoman court had its own parallel *lâle-mania* — the same flower, two distinct cultural-economic cycles.

      en: |
        Persian *dulband* (دلبند) — *dol* (wound, wrapped) + *band* (tie, cord) — is the name of a Persian palace clothing-accessory of the Sasanian period (224-651 CE). It was the turban-binding cloth of the Persian aristocracy; with one end of the cloth at the top of the turban, the other end falling as a long wing over the shoulder. In Sasanian representational art (reliefs, ivory-panels, silver bowls) it is seen consistently on the heads of court-figures. After the seventh-century Islamic conquest, the Perso-Islamic world inherited this clothing-form; the word *dulband* / *dūlband* lived alongside the Arabic *ʿimāma* (turban).

        The splitting of the word into two distinct world-words occurred between the twelfth and sixteenth centuries. (i) First path: the Mediterranean Arab-Italian-Latin lingua franca carried *dulband* into Europe as *turbante* / *turbant* / *turban* (the crusader-contacts of the twelfth century, the Mediterranean merchant corridor of the thirteenth-fifteenth). This path preserved the *clothing-accessory* sense. (ii) Second path: in the fourteenth-to-sixteenth-century Ottoman-Turkish world, the word *tülbent* (the Turkish-phonological adaptation of Persian *dulband*) was a technical part of the Ottoman military uniform. In the 1554 Busbecq-Istanbul encounter, this Turkish *tülbent* word passed into Europe — but via a *form-description misunderstanding* as a flower-name. *Tulip* and *turban* are two distant cousins of the same Persian word.

        A rare instance of a single word surviving through two distinct semantic forkings. Modern English *turban* and *tulip* are etymological relatives (the recognition has existed since the 1670s: Edward Phillips notes it in his *New World of English Words*), but daily speech does not see the relationship. A single Persian clothing-word, over an 800-year process of diffusion, survives as two distinct word-family-members of the modern world. In philology this phenomenon is named *etymological doublet* or *cognate-twin*; examples are rare.
