  tr: "*birebir anlamı:* keçiboynuzu tohumu, küçük tartı birimi. Arapça *qīrāṭ* (قِيراط) Helenistik Yunanca *kerátion* (κεράτιον, küçük boynuz — keçiboynuzu meyvesinin tohumu) sözcüğünden alıntı; Bizans tartı sisteminin altın/cevher ölçü birimi 7. yy'da Müslüman fethiyle Arap maliye sözlüğüne girdi. Klasik Arapçada *qīrāṭ* dinarın 1/20'si (paranın saflık ölçüsü) ve cevher tartısının dakikası — pratik olarak 0.195-0.212 gram arası, yere göre değişir.   en: "*literally:* a carob seed, a small unit of weight. Arabic *qīrāṭ* (قِيراط) is a Hellenistic loan from Greek *kerátion* (κεράτιον, little horn — the seed of the carob fruit); the Byzantine weighing system's unit for gold and gem entered the Arab fiscal lexicon with the seventh-century Muslim conquests. In classical Arabic *qīrāṭ* was 1/20 of a dinar (a measure of metal purity) and the smallest division of jewel weight — practically between 0.195 and 0.212 grams, varying by region. The Sicilian-Andalusian line carried the word into the Mediterranean merchant tongue; Italian *carato* (twelfth-to-thirteenth century), Old French *carat* (mid-fourteenth century), English *carat* (early fifteenth century). In the modern world the International Carat Convention of 1907 standardised the unit to **exactly 200 mg**. The spelling *karat* survives for gold purity (24-karat gold = pure gold); *carat* for gem weight — one word, two spellings, one root."

      en: |
        The nineteenth-century diamond market grew dramatically: the 1867 discoveries at Kimberley (South Africa) multiplied Euro-American jewel trade ten times over.       en: |
        The coinage reform of ʿAbd al-Malik ibn Marwān in 696/AH 77 (Damascus, Umayyad) introduced an Islamic gold and silver system: the *dīnār* (gold, c. 4.25 grams) and the *dirham* (silver, c. 2.97 grams). The dinar was divided into **24 qīrāṭ** — a quarter of the Byzantine *kerátion* assignment was taken. The number (24) entered medieval Islamic mathematics as a foundational system: the day's twenty-four hours, the academic calendar's twenty-four divisions. *Qīrāṭ* in time acquired two distinct uses: (a) weight, for jewels and precious metal, and (b) a fractional share of full ownership of a tangible thing (full ownership of a house = 24 qīrāṭ; "the house was sold by the qīrāṭ" meant "sold in partial shares"). The second sense (the ownership fraction) lasted in Ottoman legal vocabulary as *kırat / kırrat* until the nineteenth century. In Abbasid Baghdad, the arithmetical works composed alongside al-Khwārizmī's *al-Jabr* — his *Kitāb al-Jamʿ wa-l-Tafrīq bi-Ḥisāb al-Hind* among them — use *qīrāṭ* as the standard unit name in worked examples of financial calculation. The word was, from its first appearance, a measure both **physical and fiscal** — and that double identity is what prepared its later European entry along two distinct registers.

      en: |
        The Greek *kerátion* (κεράτιον) is "a little horn" — the small horn-shaped pod of the carob (*Ceratonia siliqua*). The **striking uniformity** of the seeds inside that pod — modern measurement gives an average of 0.20 ± 0.01 grams per seed — made the carob seed the natural standard unit of the ancient Mediterranean and Near Eastern weighing systems. In Rome, the same unit was *siliqua* (Latin for carob), 1/24 of a *solidus* (the Byzantine gold coin). The Byzantine imperial treasury was already using *kerátion* for both coin purity and jewel weight in the seventh century. After the Muslim conquest the system passed to Damascus (the capital of the Umayyad caliphate), and from there became, by ʿAbd al-Malik's reform, the foundation of Arab fiscal practice. Only half of the word *qīrāṭ* is known — the Arab half; the other half (the Greek-Byzantine layer) is where the word begins. In classical philology this is a "loan of a loan" — the passage of Greek into the Muslim world, then of the Muslim world into Europe, gathered into a single unit of weight.
