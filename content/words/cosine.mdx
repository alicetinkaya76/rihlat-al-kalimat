      en: |
        Cosine is the orthogonal complement of sine: on a unit circle, for an angle θ, *sin θ* gives the y-coordinate and *cos θ* the x-coordinate.         In modern linear algebra the word *cos* never appears in its full form: rotation matrices, Fourier series, Hermitian operators — all use sine and cosine together, but the notation is abbreviated to `cos θ` or `c`.       en: |
        Edmund Gunter (1581-1626), the Cambridge English mathematician-astronomer, in his 1620 *Canon Triangulorum, sive Tabulae Sinuum et Tangentium Artificialium ad Radium 10000.0000*, systematised modern trigonometric notation. The book is a corpus of trigonometric tables: logarithmic tables for the sine, cosine, tangent, secant, cotangent and cosecant. Gunter abbreviated the phrase *complementi sinus* (the sine of the complementary angle) to *co.sinus* and wrote it as one word: *cosinus*. The abbreviation took; from the 1630s onward every European trigonometric text shows *cosinus* (French *cosinus*, English *cosine*).

        That this word is a transparent derivative from the Latin terminology has a notable consequence: *cosine* is independent of the translation mistake Gerard of Cremona made with *sinus*. If Gerard had rendered *jayb* in its proper sense as "the bowstring", the sine might now be called *chorda dimidiata* (the half-chord); and cosine, by the same rule, *complementi chorda dimidiata*. Words are historical accidents; but the conceptual structure (the sine and its complement) is the direct inheritance of the mathematical architecture of Arab astronomy. *jayb al-tamām* (the sine of the complementary angle) is systematically tabulated in al-Battānī's *Zīj al-Ṣābiʾ*; Gunter's abbreviation *co.sinus* is the Latin notational extension of that Arab table tradition.

      en: |
        In Arabic astronomical texts, the term *jayb al-tamām* (تمام الجيب — "the bowstring of the complete angle"; the sine of the difference of an angle from 90°) is used systematically from the ninth century onward. Al-Battānī in his *Zīj al-Ṣābiʾ* (c. 900) tabulates with radius 1 instead of 60; the values of *jayb al-tamām* are given in columns parallel to those of *jayb*. Latin translators rendered the structure directly: *jayb* → *sinus*, *tamām* → *complementi*. *complementi sinus*. Four centuries later, in a single notational gesture by Edmund Gunter — the binding of the two words into one — modern *cosine* was born. The Arab stage's scientific contribution: the conceptual structure (the sine and its complement as independent tables). The Latin stage's terminological contribution: the compression of two words into one.

        Regiomontanus's *De triangulis omnimodis* (written 1464, printed 1533) is the first systematic textbook of European trigonometry. In it, sine is already *sinus*; but the complementary sine is written as *sinus complementi* or *sinus residui* (the sine of the remainder) — long-form translation from the Arabic terminology. Through the late fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries the long form persists in Italian and German trigonometric texts: Fincke (1583) *complementi sinus*, Pitiscus (1595) *sinus complementi*, Maginus (1592) *co. sinus*. The notation has not settled.

        Pitiscus's *Trigonometriae sive de dimensione triangulorum* (1595) is in fact establishing the term *trigonometry* itself — Greek *trigōnon* (triangle) + *metron* (measure). Pitiscus abbreviates *sinus complementi* in his tables: *si. co.* Gunter (1620) inverts this abbreviation: *co.sinus* — *co* before *sinus*. This small notational gesture is one of the silent turning points in the history of mathematics: European mathematics tried for a century and a half for the word *cos*; Gunter's form wins ergonomically (it reads side by side with *sine*), and through the seventeenth century becomes universal.
      ar: |

Aryabhata 24 değerlik bir *jyā* tablosu verir; aynı 24 değerin ters okunuşu *koṭi-jyā* tablosu olarak görür. Yani Hint trigonometrisinde sinüs ve kosinüs aynı yapısal kümenin parçasıdır — onları ayrı fonksiyonlar değil, tek bir tablonun iki sütunu olarak okuyorlar.

        Bu Hint pratiği Arap astronomisine Sindhind çeviri programıyla (770-810) geçer; *koṭi-jyā* Arapçaya *jayb al-tamām* olarak çevrilir. Yani modern "kosinüs" sözcüğünün tüm kavramsal yapısı Brahmagupta'nın 7. yüzyıl Bhillamāla'sında zaten kurulmuştu — Avrupa'ya gelene kadar geçen bin yıl boyunca yalnızca adı değişti.
      en: |
        In Brahmagupta's *Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta* (628) and Āryabhaṭa's *Āryabhaṭīya* (499) there is a basic triplet of trigonometric quantities: *jyā* (the bowstring, the modern sine), *koṭi-jyā* (the cathetus bowstring, the modern cosine) and *utkrama-jyā* (the reverse bowstring, the modern versine = 1 − cos θ). *Koṭi* in Sanskrit is "the tip of the edge, an angle, the vertex of a triangle"; on the unit circle, *jyā* gives the y-coordinate and *koṭi-jyā* the x-coordinate; the two are the orthogonal complements of each other.

        Indian mathematicians used the triplet for computational purposes; planetary position, lunar-month distance, ecliptic-plane transformations — all become simpler when *jyā* and *koṭi-jyā* are placed in the same table. Āryabhaṭa gives a *jyā* table of twenty-four values; the reverse-reading of the same twenty-four values is seen as the *koṭi-jyā* table. That is, in Indian trigonometry the sine and cosine are part of a single structural set — read not as separate functions but as the two columns of a single table.

        This Indian practice passes into Arabic astronomy through the Sindhind translation programme of c. 770-810; *koṭi-jyā* is rendered into Arabic as *jayb al-tamām*. The whole conceptual structure of the modern word *cosine* was then already in place in Brahmagupta's seventh-century Bhillamāla — through the thousand years on the road to Europe, only its name changed.
