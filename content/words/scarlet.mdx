        Greek *sigillatos* (σιγιλλᾶτος), Latin *sigillatus* — "sealed, stamped; with an embossed/relief pattern" — was used in Roman ceramic terminology for *terra sigillata* (sealed earth, stamped pottery); it crossed into Byzantine textile terminology over the fourth-to-sixth centuries and came to describe a silk-wool cloth with relief patterning. Under Justinian (527-565) the placing of the Byzantine textile industry under imperial monopoly made this luxury cloth the standard of court vesture.

        The Sasanian Persian textile industry copied the Byzantine technique in the sixth century; the product entered circulation under the Persian-Pahlavi name *siglīṭ* / *siglāt*. This Sasano-Byzantine pattern-wool offered a continuous technical infrastructure to the court-textile tradition of the Islamic world after the Arab conquest. That is, beneath the modern word *scarlet*: the Greco-Byzantine pattern-seal → Persian textile technology → Arab court vocabulary → Andalusi production workshop → Latin European luxury market → modern English colour-qualifying adjective.         Roma terra sigillata (Arezzo, La Graufesenque, MS 1.-2. yy) kalıba basılarak desenlenen kırmızı seramik — *sigillata* sıfatı "mühürlü, kalıba-basılı" demek. Bu seramik tekniği 4.-6. yy boyunca Bizans Akdeniz dünyasında tekstil üretimine paralel bir teknik altyapı oluşturdu: matbaa-öncesi dünyada *desenleri tekrarlamak* için kalıp-baskı yaygın bir teknolojiydi. Bizans tekstilcileri benzer kalıp-baskı tekniğini ipek ve yün dokumaya uyguladıklarında, mevcut *sigillatos* sıfatını ürünleri için kullandılar.

        Yani "scarlet" sözcüğünün arkasında bir teknoloji-tarihi katmanı vardır: seramikten tekstile geçen bir Roma kalıp-baskı tekniği. Sözcüğün kırmızı-renk anlamına geçişi (Venedik 13.-15. yy, kermes boyalı yün) bu eski teknolojik bağlam ile ilgisizdir; sözcük renk-anlamına kavuşmadan önce 1500 yıl boyunca "desenli/mühürlü lüks ürün" anlamında yaşadı.
      en: |
        Roman terra sigillata (Arezzo, La Graufesenque, first-and-second centuries AD) was a red pottery patterned by being pressed in a mould — the adjective *sigillata* means "sealed, mould-pressed". This ceramic technique offered, during the fourth-to-sixth centuries, in the Byzantine Mediterranean world, a parallel technical infrastructure to textile production: in the pre-printing world, mould-stamping was a widespread technology for *repeating a pattern*. When the Byzantine textile workers applied a similar mould-stamping technique to silk and wool weaving, they used the existing adjective *sigillatos* for their products.

        So, behind the word "scarlet" is a layer of technology-history: a Roman mould-stamping technique passing from ceramic to textile. The transition of the word into a red-colour sense (Venice, thirteenth-to-fifteenth century, kermes-dyed wool) has no relation to this older technological context; the word lived for fifteen hundred years in the sense of "patterned/sealed luxury product" before settling into a colour-meaning.
