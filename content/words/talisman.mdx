      en: |
        In modern English *talisman* carries two layers of meaning: (i) in museum and anthropological context it is still technically "an occult object prepared at a specified astrological conjunction, ritual-loaded" — Sir Walter Scott's 1825 novel *The Talisman* (which fictionalises the meeting of Saladin and Richard the Lion-heart) sets the word in literary English; (ii) in everyday speech it is the metaphorical "lucky charm, protective object, anything that is a mainstay of one's morale." Turkish *tılsım* carries a stronger mystical-folkloric resonance; in modern Turkish it is still bound directly to the contexts of magic, sorcery, and the esoteric, with less metaphorical loosening. In Arabic *al-țilasm* lives still in its classical sense, plural *țilasamāt*. An important culture-historical note: the European Enlightenment (seventeenth-eighteenth century), in excluding astrology from rational science, also exiled the terminology of the Arabic alchemical corpus into the occult category; but the exiled terminology is resurrected, in the hands of the nineteenth-century Romantics and the twentieth-century psychology of C. G. Jung, as the "archetypal symbol." *A word's historical life: high-science → superstition → archetypal-image → luck-fetish.* The same word passes through four epistemological transits.

      en: |
        The French *talisman* appears in 1638 in the correspondence of Pierre Petit, the apothecary-astrologer of Cardinal Mazarin; the *Encyclopédie* (Diderot and d'Alembert, 1751-1772) gives an entry on the word as the "technical term of Oriental sorcery." It is recorded in English in 1638 in John Brereton's traveller's notes *Late Discoveries*; in 1717 in Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's letters from her embassy in Turkey there is observation of Ottoman *talisman*-practice, and finally in 1825 Sir Walter Scott's novel *The Talisman* sends the word into English with a permanent literary investiture. Scott's novel takes as its subject a talisman (a stone) given to Richard the Lion-heart by Saladin in the Third Crusade; the image of the "talisman" that the novel sets up becomes a central prop of nineteenth-century Orientalism: at once feared and fascinated, at once superstitious and sacred, an object of the East. The historical-etymological irony of the situation: a word originally Greek crosses from Arab alchemy into Latin Europe and there is settled as the symbolic object "of the East, of the non-European." *A word Greek-and-Arab by origin becomes the image of Europe's dream of the East.*

      en: |
        In the Andalusi Arab culture of the tenth-eleventh century is compiled *Ghāyat al-Ḥakīm* ("the Goal of the Sage"), the practical handbook of the alchemist-magician: how the influences of the planets are to be locked into talismans, which material (stone, metal, parchment) is to be used for which planet, what symbols are to be drawn at what astrological conjunction. Lexically *al-țilasm* is the technical name of all these practices — "an object made to draw a certain magnetic influence under a specified astrological conjuncture." King Alfonso X of Castile (the Wise, 1221-1284) has the book translated into Latin; under the name *Picatrix* it becomes, from the mid-thirteenth century to the seventeenth century, the principal book of magic of the European intellectuals (Marsilio Ficino, Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa, Giordano Bruno). Ficino's 1489 *De Vita Coelitus Comparanda* can be considered an epigone of the *Picatrix*. What is striking: that the high intellectual culture of the European Renaissance feeds its magical-Eastern magic-literature still directly from Arab sources; without the *Picatrix*, the Natural Philosophy of the Renaissance is not understood. The movement of the word is the principal trace of this magical-knowledge corridor.

      en: |
        Late Greek *telesma* (τέλεσμα, root *teleō* "to complete, to consecrate, to make sacred"; *telete* the initiatory ritual of the Eleusinian Mysteries) in Byzantine Christian use means "a thing consecrated, money offered to God, a sacred object"; the material object of an ecclesiastical consecration. In the eighth-tenth century, in the Greek-to-Arabic translation movement at Baghdad, Christian Arab translators (Ḥunayn ibn Isḥāq and the generation of his successors) borrow this word to name the technical object of Persian-Arab alchemy; *al-țilasm* initially carries the sense of "a ritual-loaded sacred object." In the corpus of Jābir ibn Ḥayyān (eighth-ninth century, whose historicity is debated but the corpus is extant in ninth-tenth-century shape) and in al-Kindī's *Risāla fī Sirāj al-Mulūk*, *al-țilasm* is technical-termified as the materialised form of planetary influence. Jābir's four-element theory is the metaphysical foundation of the alchemical procedure; *al-țilasm* is the practical-productive end of that theory. An important philological point: the Greek *telesma*, while continuing to live in Byzantine Greek, acquires the meaning of *talisman* only in the Arab-alchemical context — *a word acquires, at its destination, a new meaning that was not in its source*. Meanings are built not in the source language but in the target language.

      en: |
        From the Ancient Greek root *telos* (τέλος, "end, goal, completion") two principal derivatives: *teleō* "to complete, to bring to its goal, to consecrate (ritually)", and *telete* "the initiatory rite, the mystical consecration" — particularly the initiation rites of the Eleusinian Mysteries (fifth-century BC classical records). *Telesma* (τέλεσμα) is the material object of that act: the consecrated money itself, the offering made to a god, the sanctified object. In the Hellenistic period (third-first century BC), the term expands in Pythagorean and Neoplatonic philosophy to the sense of astrological magia: the idea that an object "consecrated" under a particular astrological conjuncture carries a magnetic influence. This is the theoretical template, later, of Arab alchemy. In Byzantine Christianity the word (still *telesma*) takes on an ecclesiastical use: an offering to the church, a consecrated object. The semantic history of the word in three layers: (i) classical Greek "the consecrated thing," (ii) Hellenistic "the astrologically-consecrated object," (iii) Byzantine Christian "the sacred object offered to the church." All three meanings come together in Arabic *al-țilasm*; Arab alchemy rejoins the classical Greek concept to the Hellenistic magia concept.
