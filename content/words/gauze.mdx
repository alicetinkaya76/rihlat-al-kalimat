      en: |
        In the modern hospital vocabulary, *gauze* (English), *gaze* (Turkish), *الشّاش* (Arabic) means a thin, sparsely-woven cotton cloth — wound dressing, surgical sponge, bandage. Its structural property is its sparseness: the pores let moisture through, the cloth stays dry. The modern medical sense ties to nineteenth-century sterile surgical practice; after Lister's development of antiseptic dressing in 1867, the term "gauze dressing" became standard for the sterilisable cotton bandage.

        The tie between the modern medical sense and the medieval luxury-textile sense is the property of fine texture: the medieval "gauze" was a silken, luxurious, display-valued cloth — transparent in touch; the modern "gauze" is a cheap cotton, functional, hygienic — pore-permeable in touch. The same word, over six centuries, crosses from the luxury market to the medical market; the same weaving structure (sparse weave) in two different contexts of use.

      en: |
        The first Latin appearances: *gazzatum* in Genoese records (c. 1218), *pannus de Gaza* in Marseille (c. 1248). Old French *gaze* (mid-thirteenth century), modern English *gauze* (fifteenth), Italian *garza* (fifteenth). Two parallel etymological roads exist, and they are contested in philology:

        (i) **The toponym source**: *Gazza*, the Palestinian coastal city, was known in the Byzantine and early Islamic periods (4th-9th c.) as a centre of fine linen and silk weaving; the Crusades (after 1099) introduced Gazene cloth into Europe; *de Gaza* / *gazzatum* "the Gazene cloth" → *gauze*. The modern OED prefers this account.

        (ii) **The Arabic word source**: Arabic *qazz* (قَزّ — raw silk) was in circulation in the Mediterranean trader's lingua franca as "silk-kind cloth"; *qazz* → Italian *gazza* (q→g sound-shift typical of Mediterranean lingua franca) → Old French *gaze*. The modern *Trésor de la langue française* prefers this account.

        The philological dispute is undecidable: the two etymological lines mutually reinforce one another (the Gazene cloth was made from raw silk; the toponym *Gazza* was semantically equivalent in the Arab world to the word *qazz*). The most reliable reading is that the two parallel sources work *together* beneath the modern word.

      en: |
        Gaza, from antiquity to the eighth century, was a textile-production and port city on the western flank of the Levantine coast. It was one of the significant points of the Byzantine textile industry (described in Procopius's *De aedificiis*); it worked fine linen (linum) and raw silk (from China along the *Bilād al-Shām* route). The early Islamic conquest (635) carried the textile production through; in the ninth-and-tenth-century Abbasid period, Gaza textiles appear in the Baghdadi court inventories with the adjectival construction *al-ghazzī* (Gazene).

        The Crusader campaign (the First Crusade of 1099, Gaza under Frankish control by 1100) introduced Gazene weaving directly into Europe: the trade-emissaries who came with the Frankish knights carried Gazene cloth into the ports of Genoa, Marseille and Pisa. The term *pannus de Gaza* settled in thirteenth-century Mediterranean trade documents as a standard luxury-textile classification — in the same toponym-construction logic as *damascus cloth* (the Damascene cloth). The word is of Crusader context: *gauze* entered Latin Europe not by the internal trade channels of the Islamic world but from the context of war-contact.

      en: |
        Raw silk (Chinese *sī* → Sanskrit *kauśeya* → Persian/Pahlavi *kaz* → Arabic *qazz* → Greek *sēr*) moved as a single raw material from east to west through the Chinese silk road, the exit-route of the Mediterranean trade in the golden age. The original production is in China; the Persian textile industry processes the Chinese silk raw material, naming it by the Sasanian word *qazz*; the Byzantine textile industry (the looms of Constantinople) processes the same silk thread as its raw material. In the sixth century, under Justinian, the Byzantine empire took over silk-worm cultivation, and a parallel silk industry was established in the western Mediterranean (its centre on the Syrian-Palestinian coast, of which Gaza was a part).

        The seventh-century Arab conquest did not fragment this textile network; the production continued, only the rulers changed. Through the Crusader period (twelfth century) the double naming of the silk raw material — *qazz* in the Arab interior of the Levant, *Gaza-cloth* on the Mediterranean coast — operated in parallel. The word *gauze* that passed into Latin Europe is to be read as a term uniting the two terminals of that double naming.
