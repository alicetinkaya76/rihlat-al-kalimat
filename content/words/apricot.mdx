      en: |
        The elder Pliny, in the fifteenth book of his *Naturalis Historia* (77 AD), lays out the Roman gardener's fruit-calendar and names an early-ripening variety of the peach (*persica*) as *praecoqua* — "the early fruits," the substantivised neuter plural of the adjective *praecox*. The botanical species named is, in fact, *Prunus armeniaca* — called "Armenian plum" in Latin because Rome received it via Iran from Armenia, but species-wise separate from peach, an independent apricot. Pliny's *praecoqua* drifts in Byzantine Greek to *praikokion*, then to *prekokion*; two centuries later it enters Arabic, with the botanical literature brought into Mesopotamia by Syrian Christian traders, as *al-barqūq*. At what point in its drift does a Latin adjective become a proper noun — in the mouth of another language. *Praecoqua* says "early fruits" in genuine Latin; in Arabic it has become "that particular fruit." The next seven centuries are the name of that particular fruit.
