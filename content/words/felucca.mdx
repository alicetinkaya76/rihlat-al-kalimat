      en: |
        Italian *feluca* / *filuca* (first attested in Genoese records c. 1465), Spanish *faluca* (late fifteenth century), Old French *felouque* (mid-sixteenth century), English *felucca* (from 1620 onward). The rapid spread of the word into the Mediterranean European maritime languages rests on the direct contact of Egyptian-Syrian coastal trade with the Mediterranean western market: in the fourteenth-to-sixteenth century, traders from Genoa, Venice and Marseille had direct observation of Arab navigation in the ports of Egyptian Alexandria, Beirut, Damascus-Acre.

        This period of word-diffusion coincides with the golden age of the Mediterranean lingua franca: the common means of communication for Arab, Italian, Spanish and Greek traders, as the trader-language, the navigator-language, the transport-language. The technical-practical meaning carried by the word *felucca* (lateen-sailed, narrow-hulled, coastal-use small boat) remained consistent in all these languages. Mediterranean European navigation integrated the technical properties of the felucca (the long-narrow hull, the horizontal-wind manoeuvring-capacity) into its own ship-engineering; in particular the Spanish-Italian coastal-fishing boats are a felucca-derivation.

Sözcüğün Arapça kullanımında *falūka* (فلوكة) sahil-kullanımlı, küçük-orta boyutlu, lateen yelkenli teknenin standart adı; Bağdat-coğrafya yazarları (al-Yaʿqūbī 9. yy, al-Maqdisī 10. yy), Endülüs-coğrafya yazarları (al-Idrīsī 12. yy), Mağrip-Mısır seyyahları (al-Wazzān 16. yy) sözcüğü tutarlı olarak kullanır. Yine Memlûk Mısır (1250-1517) dönemi *falūka* sözlüğünün Latin Avrupa'ya yayılmasının üretici dönemi: Memlûk-Italian deniz ticareti, *galleys*-anlaşmaları, denizci-tüccar eğitim değişimleri sözcüğün hızla Akdeniz Lingua Franca'sına geçişini sağladı.
      en: |
        Arab Mediterranean navigation (ninth-fifteenth centuries) was the Mediterranean-adaptation of the dhow-based navigation of the Indian Ocean. In the Arabic use of the word, *falūka* (فلوكة) is the standard name of a coastal-use, small-or-mid-sized lateen-sailed boat; the Baghdadi geographers (al-Yaʿqūbī in the ninth century, al-Maqdisī in the tenth), the Andalusi geographers (al-Idrīsī in the twelfth), the Maghrebi-Egyptian travellers (al-Wazzān in the sixteenth) use the word consistently. The practical use-context: coastal fishing, coastal passenger-carriage, small-cargo trade, in-port manoeuvring. It does not make great transoceanic dhow voyages, but is the infrastructure of daily coastal sea-operations.

        The Nile delta of Egypt and the port-network of Alexandria-Damietta-Rosetta was the most intensive use-centre of *falūka* boats: at the interface of the Nile and the Mediterranean, the *falūka* was the ideal ship-type for the navigation needs of a double-environment (a fresh-water river + a salty sea). It is in the Mamluk Egyptian period (1250-1517) that the *falūka* vocabulary spread productively to Latin Europe: the Mamluk-Italian sea trade, the *galleys*-agreements, the navigator-and-trader educational exchanges enabled the rapid passage of the word into the Mediterranean lingua franca.

      en: |
        Greek *epholkion* (ἐφόλκιον, from the verb *epholkō* "to tow, to draw") meant "a towed boat, a small boat attached to a main-ship"; it was a standard term in the maritime vocabulary of the Byzantine period (fourth-seventh centuries). The Byzantine sea-forces (the *Karabisianoi*, later the *thematic* sea units) called the small auxiliary boats *epholkia*. In Byzantine Egypt (from the Augustan period to the seventh-century Arab conquest), *epholkion* was a part of the everyday navigator's tongue.

        The Arab conquest (the Egyptian conquest of 639-642) took over the Byzantine maritime infrastructure; in the process of Byzantine-Arab transformation, the word *epholkion* was adapted into Arabic as *falūka* (Greek *e-* dropped by the Arabic prefix-apheresis; Greek *-ion* → the Arabic *-a* place-noun suffix; Greek *ph* → Arabic *f*; Greek *k* → Arabic *k*; Greek *o* → Arabic *u/o*). This Greek-Arab transformation is the typical philological pattern of the Mediterranean Byzantine-Arab transition; alongside *admiral* (Arabic *amīr al-baḥr* < Byzantine) and *cable* (the Latin *capulum* + Arabic *ḥabl* interaction), a typical instance of the multi-language-layered structure of the Mediterranean lingua franca.

        These three layers of the word's history (Byzantine Greek → Arab → Lingua Franca Italian → modern European) explain the layered archaeology of the modern word *felucca*. Felucca is not only an Arabic word, nor only a Greek one — it is a lingua-franca artifact from the multi-lingual maritime vocabulary of the ancient Mediterranean world.
