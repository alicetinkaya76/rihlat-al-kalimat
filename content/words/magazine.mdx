  en: "*literally:* a storehouse, a warehouse. Arabic *makhzan* (مَخزَن) — from the root *kh-z-n* (to store) on the *mafʿal* pattern — *a place where a thing is stored*; plural *makhāzin*. Andalusian and Maghrebi Arabic used the word for commercial and military depots. It crossed into Mediterranean merchant speech in the thirteenth century: Italian *magazzino* (Pisan documents of 1227), Old French *magasin* (early fifteenth century). The word entered English from French as *magazine* (1580s), first in the military register — a *powder magazine*, a ship's magazine. In 1731, Edward Cave's *Gentleman's Magazine* in London added an entirely new sense to the word: *a periodical in which various articles are stored* — *a kind of intellectual storehouse*. The metaphor was so successful that in modern English the publishing sense became dominant, the military sense subordinate. In Turkish, two parallel lines: *mağaza* (warehouse > retail premises, the trade route) and *magazin* (magazine, the second wave through European usage)."

      en: |
        Edward Cave (1691-1754), at his St. John's Gate press in London, brought out *The Gentleman's Magazine, or Monthly Intelligencer* in January 1731 — the title used the word for the first time to name a periodical. Cave's editorial logic: book reviews, financial news, poetry, gossip, military reports, weather — *all stored in a single magazine*. That is, *an intellectual storehouse*. The metaphor was deliberate, implying that readers were buying the whole bundle from a single source; what each subscription bought was the right to make a selection from a *storehouse*. The innovation was rapidly imitated: the *Scots Magazine* in 1747, the *Town and Country Magazine* in 1771, the *European Magazine* in 1791. By the mid-nineteenth century *magazine* in English and in most European languages (*magazine* French, *Magazin* German, *magazín* Spanish, *magazine* Dutch) primarily meant "periodical"; the military and storehouse senses had fallen behind. **A commercial word taken from Andalusian Arabic 400 years earlier received, by the hand of a printer in London, a wholly new birth.**

      en: |
        Andalusian written Arabic uses *makhzan* in two registers: (a) the closed storehouses owned by merchant families in the commercial quarters of cities like Cordoba, Seville and Granada — warehouses for spice, silk, grain, olive oil, rose water; (b) the state warehouses of arms, grain and money of the Andalusian Emirate/Caliphate treasury. In late Maghrebi Arabic *al-makhzan* came to mean **the state government itself** — *Sultān al-Makhzan* (Sultan of the Treasury) meant "the central government"; what nineteenth-century Europeans called *the Maghzen* in Morocco was the entirety of the Moroccan governing bureaucracy. The traders moving between al-Andalus and Italy were using the same word well before 1227 — documentation begins in 1227, but the practice was alive two centuries earlier.

      en: |
        The classical Arabic root *kh-z-n* means "to store, to keep, to hide"; *khazana* (the verb), *khazn* (the verbal noun), *khazīna* (treasury — a place where valuables are kept), *al-makhzan* (a storehouse). In the Qurʾān *al-khazāʾin* (treasuries) recurs as "the hidden treasures of God": *innamā khazāʾinu rabbiya* (al-Anʿām 6:50), *innī ḥafīẓun ʿalīm* (Yūsuf 12:55, "I am a guardian over the treasuries"). In pre-Islamic poetry *al-makhzan* is most often the place where private wealth was kept; with the rise of court-centred states (Abbasid, Andalusian Umayyad, Fatimid) the word took on an institutional register. Around the mid-ninth century, *khazāna* became a metaphor in linguistic terminology for *the treasury of words* (a dictionary) — *Khizānat al-luġa* "the treasury of language". A striking parallel: *the place where words are stored* — and the same word was reborn in Europe eight centuries later in the sense of a *publication that stores words*. **The word returns indirectly to its own beginning: an Arabic dictionary-as-treasury metaphor revives, eight centuries on, in a printing house in London.**
