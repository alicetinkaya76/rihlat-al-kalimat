      en: |
        In Arab-Islamic palace and domestic architecture, *al-ṣuffa* (الصُّفّة) carries two basic senses: (i) a wall-side sitting-platform, raised from below, with carpet and cushions — a palace ceremonial space or a domestic reception area; (ii) a portico'd/landing corridor surrounding the courtyard or space of a building — the colonnaded corridor around the courtyard of the Damascus Great Mosque is called the *ṣuffa*. The word comes from the verbal root *ṣaff* (line, file); the sense "the place of lining-up".

        In early Islamic history, the *Aṣḥāb al-Ṣuffa* (the People of the Ṣuffa) — the poor *muhājir* Muslims who lived on the *ṣuffa* of the Prophet's mosque-courtyard in Medina (seventh century) — gave the word a religio-historical context. In the later centuries, in Arab domestic architecture, *ṣuffa* was a standard interior-architectural element; in the courtyard *riad* of the Maghribi house and in the Egyptian mansion-house (e.g. Beit Suheymi, seventeenth century, Cairo), typical examples are visible. Turkish Ottoman architecture took over this Arab *ṣuffa* element as *sofa* and made it the defining element of classical-period (sixteenth-seventeenth century) domestic architecture.

      en: |
