      en: |
        Tangent has two distinct ancient sources. The first is the gnomon practice: from the Babylonian tablets (1500 BC) to the Indian *Sūrya Siddhānta* of the second century AD, the length of the shadow of a vertical stick is a direct measure of the elevation angle of the sun. The Sanskrit *chāyā* (shadow) is the Indian name for the tangent value. The second is Apollonius's *Conica* (c. 200 BC): a straight line touching a circle at a point (*haptesthai*, to touch) is a geometric object. The Greeks did not use this touching-line trigonometrically; they left it as a pure circle-geometry term. The Arab astronomers united the Babylonian-Indian gnomon practice into a mathematical table, and the Greek touching-geometry into a conceptualisation — both under the word *ẓill* (shadow). When Fincke in 1583 carried both into Latin, he chose the Greek-conceptual half (touching) and abandoned the Arab-technological half (the shadow).
