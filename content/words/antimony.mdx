      en: |
        In the Arab medical-pharmaceutical tradition, *al-ithmid* is the technical term reserved for the specific mineral (stibnite, antimony sulphide), while *al-kuḥl* is a broader term used in the sense of practical-cosmetic-medical eye-treatment. The conceptual distinction between the two was systematically established in al-Rāzī's *Kitāb al-Manṣūrī* (c. 903): *al-ithmid* is the raw-material-mineral, *al-kuḥl* is the treatment-powder obtained by grinding and filtering that mineral. The same distinction continues in the eye-medicine sections (Book III/2, ophthalmology) of Ibn Sīnā's *Kitāb al-Qānūn* and in the mineral-sections of the *Kitāb al-Shifāʾ*.

        Ibn Sīnā's medicine recommended *al-ithmid* in three principal uses: (i) treatment of common eye-inflammations (conjunctivitis-trachoma) — modern science confirms the antibacterial effect of antimony-sulphide; (ii) eye-protection from the glare of the desert sun (the damage of UV-rays of Sahara light, a precursor of modern UV-protection technology); (iii) aesthetic eye-ornamentation — a social-norm for women and men. This three-way use continued as standard pharmacist-practice in Mamluk Egypt and al-Andalus until the fourteenth-fifteenth century; the survival of the word as *al-kuḥl* in the modern Egyptian-Arab world is the living evidence of this uninterrupted practical-inheritance.

        At the alchemical level: the Jābir-Rāzī tradition placed *al-ithmid* as the basic-component of mineral-chemistry. In the Jabirian corpus, *al-ithmid* is placed in the group of "sulphide-based black minerals" in the mineral-classification table; as practical laboratory-application, the reduction process by heating the sulphide-ore to obtain refined antimony-pigment is explained.         Archaeologists have extracted cosmetic-vessels containing antimony-sulphide residue, as eye-ornament material, from the Mesopotamian Sumerian-Akkadian period (3500-2000 BC); similar antimony-based kohl-residue has been documented from the Indus-valley archaeological site of Mohenjo-daro (2600-1900 BC). Because modern science-mineralogy directly inherits from this Arab-synthesis phase, the word survives in the modern element-table.
