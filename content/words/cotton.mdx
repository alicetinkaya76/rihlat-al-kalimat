      en: |
        From the 1750s to the 1860s, cotton became the axis of the modern world economy. Manchester's steam mills — Crompton's *spinning mule* (1779), Cartwright's power loom (1785) — let Lancashire spin more yarn in an hour than it had before in a day; Liverpool came to import 800 million pounds of raw cotton a year. The counterweight: a slave-plantation system in the American South. In 1790 there were about 700,000 enslaved people in the USA; in 1860 more than four million, the majority working the cotton belts of Mississippi-Alabama-Georgia. Eli Whitney's *cotton gin* (1793) sped the separation of fibre from seed by a factor of fifty, making the institution of slavery profitable just as its moral defence was eroding.

        The side-effect was incalculable: a centuries-old Indian weaving tradition collapsed. At the end of the eighteenth century Bengal *muslin* had been Europe's aristocratic cloth — a Dacca weaver could thin an object's weight to 200 threads per inch. With the 1813 Charter Act, British textiles entered India and local duties were lifted; by 1850 India had ceased to be a cloth exporter and had become a raw-cotton exporter, importing back the cloth Lancashire wove from its own fibre. The port of Bombay became one that carried the same thread twice — once raw, once finished.

        This stretched the word's burden. *Cotton* was no longer only the name of a plant but of an *economic category*: a commodity-exchange ticker, a tax line, a customs item, a shipping manifest. The American Civil War (1861-65) showed the weight of that category — when Confederate "King Cotton" diplomacy cut the supply to draw Britain in, the *cotton famine* struck at the gates of the Lancashire mills.

        Pamuk Endülüs'e Arap fethinden sonra (8.-9. yy) yerleşti, ama büyük yayılışı 10. ve 13. yüzyıllar arasında oldu. Andrew Watson'ın *Agricultural Innovation in the Early Islamic World* (1983) bu döneme "Arap-İslam Yeşil Devrimi" adını taktı — pamukla beraber pirinç, şeker kamışı, limon, portakal, patlıcan, ıspanak, harnup, bir avuç boya bitkisi ve sulu yetişen meyve ağaçları Endülüs'e bir bütün olarak yerleşti.         Cotton settled in al-Andalus after the Arab conquest (8th-9th c.), but its large diffusion came between the 10th and 13th centuries. Andrew Watson, in *Agricultural Innovation in the Early Islamic World* (1983), gave this period the name "Arab-Islamic Green Revolution" — alongside cotton, rice, sugar cane, lemon, orange, aubergine, spinach, carob, a clutch of dye-plants, and water-thirsty fruit trees entered al-Andalus as a complex. All of them were summer crops awaiting irrigation; their shared need triggered an original technical infrastructure: the <span class="term">acequia</span> network.

        The Segura basin around Murcia, the Guadalquivir bank at Seville, the Genil-Darro fork at Granada — three cities accumulated Roman canal remains, then layered atop them technologies brought from Damascus and Baghdad: the *qanāt* (subterranean conduit), the *nā‘ūra* (waterwheel), the *sāqiya* (chain-of-pots wheel). *Acequia* — Arabic *al-sāqiya*, "the irrigator" — became a network word: the trunk canal (*acequia mayor*), the branch (*acequia menor*), the in-field channel (*hijuela*), the warden of each branch (*acequiero*), the annual tribunal that audited the water budget (*Tribunal de las Aguas* — still meeting in Valencia on Thursdays). A cotton field was meaningless alone; a single parcel inside a *huerta* (irrigated garden) network, with a one-hour water right, was meaningful.

        This infrastructure made cotton one of the three principal exports of al-Andalus — the others being silk and olive oil. According to twelfth-century al-Idrīsī, Seville's weekly market filled with bales of <span class="term" lang="ar">القُطْن</span> (*al-quṭn*); Genoese and Pisan merchants carried them north into the *fustian* weaving of north Italy. *Fustian* — itself named after Cairo's *Fusṭāṭ* quarter — was a linen-cotton blend, the medieval European middle-class shirt. *Cotton* and *fustian* passed through two doors, but the same network.

        The fall of al-Andalus (Seville 1248, Granada 1492) did not extinguish cotton culture at once. The Murcian *huerta* still produced cotton into the sixteenth century; only when the new Christian administration of Granada expelled the *Morisco* gardeners at the end of the sixteenth century did much of the irrigation know-how vanish, and al-Andalus cease to be a cotton exporter. But the word's European mould had set: *algodón*, *cotone*, *coton* — and the vocabulary that would steer the Atlantic plantations of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries was inherited from this Andalusian lexicon.

        In the early Abbasid centuries (8th-10th c.) Baghdad, Damascus, and Cairo's Fustat are global textile centres. <span class="term" lang="ar">القُطْن</span> (*al-quṭn*) is an established crop across all three — grown in Egypt's Nile delta, in Syria's Orontes basin, in the Mesopotamian alluvium between the Tigris and Euphrates. Production is not a state project but a tax-registered branch of agriculture; the landowner-sharecropper (*muzāra‘a*) arrangement governs it, the fibre picked in December, ginned through winter, sold at spring market.

        The urban weaving workshops (*ṭirāz*) produce the cloth of the caliphate; for the caliphs themselves they weave silk-cotton blended robes and sashes. In Fustat an entire quarter — whose name will pass into Italian as *fustian* — is identified with cotton weaving; the cloth woven in a ninth-century atelier in this quarter, a century later, is the merchandise Coptic, Jewish, and Muslim traders bring to the Genoese market. Merchant letters from the Geniza archive (10th-13th c.) report daily cotton shipments from Fustat to Aix and on to Marseille.

        In the same period Baghdad's paper industry is also fed by cotton. The paper technology that entered from China in the mid-eighth century had initially worked, by Chinese standards, with mulberry fibre; but Baghdad and Damascus workshops swiftly switched to local cotton. The page of a ninth-century Arabic book is, in all likelihood, the second life of an Egyptian cotton — the first life was a cloth; in use the cloth grew old, became a rag, the rag became paper-pulp. The manuscripts copied at the *Bayt al-Ḥikma* opened in 832 in Baghdad were multiplied through this cycle.

        The word itself — <span class="term" lang="ar">القُطْن</span> — was by then settled in the shared vocabulary of these three capitals; how it had entered Arabic is, however, disputed. The standard view points to an Akkadian or Aramaic substrate; but the early Islamic lexicographer al-Jawharī (tenth century) derived it from within Arabic itself, from *qaṭana* "to reside, to settle" — an image of the fibre "settling" into the ground. Modern linguistics rejects this etymology, but it tells us how the word was *felt* in tenth-century Baghdad: not as an import but as a *native* word.

        The earliest archaeological evidence for cotton is from an Indus Valley site. At Mehrgarh (modern Balochistan, layer dated 6000-5000 BCE), *Gossypium arboreum* fibres were preserved around a copper bead — the earliest known use of cotton anywhere. In the following millennium — during the Indus Valley civilisation of c. 3000-1500 BCE — cotton-weaving remains were recovered at Mohenjo-daro and Harappa. The name *karpāsa* (Sanskrit; "cotton") was inherited by the Vedic texts from this era.

        Indian cotton reached the ancient world by two doors: first by sea, through the merchants of Aden and Hadramaut, from the first century BCE onward, to Rome; and by land, across Persia and Mesopotamia, to the eastern Mediterranean. Herodotus (fifth century BCE) wrote that he had heard of "wool-bearing trees" in India — the term is confused with sheep's wool, but the plant described is the cotton tree. Theophrastus (fourth century BCE) describes the same plant more accurately. In Rome cotton remained a luxury — named *carbasus*, again from Sanskrit *karpāsa*. But it would be the early centuries of the Arab world, not Rome, that turned Mediterranean cotton into a mainstream cloth.

        The Indian origin precipitates into two words. Arabic <span class="term" lang="ar">القُطْن</span> is not from the Indus — its name is independent, probably from a Semitic substrate. But Persian *pambak* — the ancestor of Turkish *pamuk* — is fed not by Sanskrit *karpāsa* but by the wider semantic field of Indian geography around the flower-fibre-bedding cluster. Cotton, in the history of its names, therefore belongs to two sound-families: the *quṭn* family along the Arabia-al-Andalus line, and the *pambak* family along the India-Iran-Anatolia line. Two words from one fibre — a layered trace that geography has left in language.
