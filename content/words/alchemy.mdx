      en: |
        In modern history of science, *alchemy* was long framed as "pre-chemical mystical-speculative pseudo-science": the dream of material transmutation (lead into gold), the search for the elixir-of-life, the symbolic-esoteric language, the religio-philosophical admixture. The seventeenth-century figure closest to scientific-chemistry, Robert Boyle, continued, until his death (1691), to read philosophical-alchemy; in Newton's personal archive (at Cambridge) there are more than a thousand pages of alchemical notes.

        The popular-cultural image lives in parallel: J. K. Rowling's *Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone* (1997, the American edition *Sorcerer's Stone*), with the reference to Nicholas Flamel — a French notary who really lived (1330-1418), who practised alchemy — introduces alchemy to the vocabulary of millions of child-readers.       en: |
        One of the most critical products of the Toledo School of Translators in the mid-twelfth century: Robert of Chester's 1144 Latin translation of the *Liber de compositione alchemiae* (The Book of the Composition of Alchemy), attributed to Morienus Romanus. This text was the first-explicit alchemy reference of Latin Europe; the word *alchemia* in its title was directly Latinised from the Arabic *al-kīmiyāʾ*. In the following centuries the intense-transmission of Arab alchemy-writing (the Jābir ibn Ḥayyān corpus, al-Rāzī's *Sirr al-asrār*, Ibn Wāfid's *Kitāb al-adwiya al-mufradah*, Ibn al-Bayṭār's *al-Jāmiʿ*): Albertus Magnus (1200-1280) writing *De alchimia*, Roger Bacon (1214-1294) on alchemy-methods in *Opus tertium*, Petrus Bonus's 1330 *Pretiosa margarita novella* a full-system alchemy textbook.

        Through the thirteenth-to-seventeenth century the Latin European alchemy laboratory-practice was built on the practical-inheritance of Arab sources: distillation techniques (al-anbīq → alembic), the cataloguing of reactive-elements (the classification of 200+ chemical substances in al-Rāzī's *Sirr al-asrār*), the practical laboratory-outputs (sodas, acids, salts — words of Arab origin like *alkali*, *calcium*, *borax*). Robert Boyle (1627-1691, *The Sceptical Chymist* of 1661) was drawing the opening page of the modern chemical-paradigm while still working in the alchemy-context; Isaac Newton (1643-1727), while writing on optical-mechanical physics, was at the same time reading symbolic alchemy day and night. Modern chemistry is not a break from alchemy but an emergence from within. The "*al-*" prefix of the word is the invisible-signature of this long-lasting Arabic root.

      en: |
        The Bayt al-Ḥikma translation-school of Abbasid Baghdad (mid-ninth century) systematically transferred the Greek Hellenistic scientific heritage into Arabic. In this process, alchemy-type texts — in particular the pseudo-works of Bolos-Democritus written at Alexandria in the Hellenistic period, the *Cheirokmēta* of Zosimos of Panopolis (third-fourth century), the commentaries of Synesius (fourth century) and Olympiodorus (sixth century) — were translated into Arabic. At the same time the Persian Sasanian alchemy-practice (the Jundi-Shapur academy of the sixth-seventh century) and the Indian *rasāyana* literature (the traditional mercury-based long-life-elixir) became part of the Arab intellectual synthesis. The bringing-together of these three principal sources at Baghdad was the shaping of *al-kīmiyāʾ* as a systematic-Arab science.

        Two canonical figures: (i) Jābir ibn Ḥayyān (c. 721-815, Ṭūs/Khorasan-Kufa) — the combination of neoplatonic numerology-speculation and practical laboratory-experimentation; the hundreds of works that make up the Jābir-corpus, like *Kitāb al-mawāzīn* (the Book of Balances), *Kitāb al-aḥjār* (the Book of Stones), *Kitāb al-mulk* (the Book of Sovereignty/Minerals) — the authenticity of the authorship is debated in modern philology; the Shīʿa esoteric circles of the ninth-tenth century probably added under the name Jābir. (ii) Al-Rāzī (854-925, Rey-Baghdad) — a more pragmatic clinical-pharmacist, his work *Sirr al-asrār* (the Secret of Secrets) a systematic catalogue of 200+ chemical elements/reactives; the structural-prototype of the modern chemical-archive. In the same period the distillation-instruments (al-anbīq, alembic), the chemistry-laboratory-glass standard (al-balqāʾ, balqūn), and the cataloguing-systems were standardised in the Arab-Baghdadi workshops, in a form that would reach Europe many centuries later.

        The Greek *khēmeia* / *khymeía* (χημεία / χυμεία) was a synthetic scientific-practice taking shape in the Alexandrian workshops in the Late Hellenistic period (second century BC - third century AD): practical-metallurgy (gold-silver alloys, the manufacture of imitation-jewellery), medico-pharmacy (drug-making, distillation), the dyer's craft (pigments, mordants), perfume-making.
