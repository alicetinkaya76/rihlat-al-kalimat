      en: |
        In modern industry *camphor* comes from two sources: natural camphor (steam-distillation of the *Cinnamomum camphora* tree in Taiwan, China and Japan) and synthetic camphor (a chemical synthesis from turpentine developed in Germany in 1903). Annual world production is some thirty thousand tons; the principal uses are as a plasticiser (the foundational component of celluloid film from 1869 to 1950, before modern photographic film), as a stabiliser of explosives (the stabiliser that turns nitrocellulose into stable "gun-cotton"), in dermatological preparations (the itch-relief, cold-compress effect), and in aromatherapy. The word *camphor* is fixed in modern scientific English as the name of this Asian tree-exudate; Turkish *kafur* lives equivalently in traditional pharmacy. An important cultural-medical continuity: Indian Ayurveda, Chinese traditional medicine, Greek-Arab Galenic medicine, and modern dermatology all use the same substance (the camphor crystal) under different epistemic frames; in Middle-Eastern funerary practice the use of camphor in washing the dead (in a hadith of ʿĀʾisha) runs continuously for fourteen centuries. *A thousand-year-old knowledge-object becomes a substrate for modern industry as well.*

      en: |
        After the First Crusade (1095-1099) the Frankish kingdoms — Jerusalem, Antioch, Tripoli — are the place where the European knight-and-merchant comes into direct contact with Levantine Arab pharmacy; the shared Muslim-Christian markets of Antioch and Acre are the nodes at which "Eastern substances" — camphor, pepper, cinnamon, sandalwood, myrrh — reach the Latin merchant. Stephen of Antioch's 1127 translation into Latin of al-Majūsī's *Kāmil aṣ-Ṣināʿa aṭ-Ṭibbiyya* (the *Liber regalis*) puts hundreds of Arab pharmaceutical names into the European pharmacy lexicon, *al-kāfūr* among them. In the thirteenth century the *Antidotarium Nicolai* of the Salerno medical school (around 1140, then the standard pharmacy formulary into the fourteenth century) lists *camphora* in twenty-three recipes; the commonest uses are as a wound-compress and in cough-and-cold syrups. The word *camphor* reaches English in the fourteenth century (Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales* records it as *camfre/camphire*); the leading Arabic article *al-* has dropped, and a Latin morphology (*-a*) has been added. A market-word of the Levant becomes one of the principal words of the European apothecary's lexicon.

      en: |
        The Cairo Genizah documents (the eleventh-and-twelfth-century Jewish merchant archive) display the workings of Arab pharmacy as the link between the Mediterranean and the Indian Ocean: camphor is classified by varieties — *al-kāfūr al-fanṣūrī* (from Fansur, the north-west coast of Sumatra; the purest and most expensive), *al-kāfūr al-rabāḥī* (from Rabāh, of lower quality), *al-kāfūr al-qaymūrī* (Chinese camphor, the bark-shaving), *al-kāfūr al-ʿīsawī* (a synthetic Arab product, by distilling fresh flower-water). This five-fold classification is already set up in tenth-century Baghdad pharmacy (Yaʿqūb al-Kindī's *Aqrābādhīn*, c. 870, the principal source for the 'canonical' European pharmacy formulary and the camphor taxonomy crossing into Latin in the twelfth century). An important point of awareness: Arab pharmacy does not only import Sumatran camphor; it also exports to Europe. The Genizah records the shipping of camphor from Cairo to Aix-en-Provence; the European apothecary's dependence on Baghdad-and-Damascus pharmacy exists not only at the word-level of knowledge but also at the level of the physical flow of goods. *The knowledge-substance and the physical substance flow along the same channel.*

      en: |
        Between the seventh and tenth centuries the word *karpūra*, arriving in Baghdad by Indian-Ocean sea-trade, passes through the process of accommodation to Arabic morphology: Sanskrit *karpūra* ("white, bright, camphor") first undergoes an inner-consonant shift, since the *p*-phoneme is absent from the consonantal tonality of Arabic (*p* > *f*); next, the Arabic root-template (the habit of forming a regular three-letter root) reads the word into the root *k-f-r* by an "inner softening" of semantic drift. An important sound-change: the Arabic root *k-f-r* carries senses of "to cover, to hide, to conceal" (and from the same root *kāfir* "the coverer, the concealer"); the essential nature of camphor as "what disappears, what is volatile, what sublimes" sits comfortably with this Arabic root-meaning — that is, in entering Arabic morphology the word acquires, in the process, a kind of "natural etymology" via Arabic root-semantics. This is the typical example of a loanword's adjustment to a host language: the foreign word behaves as if it were a native root. Al-Kindī's *Aqrābādhīn* draws up the five-fold knowledge-structure of camphor: its source, its classification, its preparation, its dosage, its therapeutic indication. Al-Rāzī's *al-Ḥāwī* lists camphor recipes one by one. These two texts are the principal sources for the passage into Latin.

      en: |
        The Sanskrit *karpūra* (कर्पूर) comes from the adjective *karpū-* ("white, bright, luminous"); the resin of the *Cinnamomum camphora* tree, when crystallised, looks clear, white, sparkling. In Indian geography, fifth-to-seventh-century Sanskrit texts (Bhagavata Purana, Garuda Purana) name the island of Sumatra *Karpūradvīpa* ("the Camphor Island"); Indian navigators recognise the port of Fansur (modern Barus) on northern Sumatra as the place where the world's finest camphor is produced. Malay *kapur* ("lime, white, the bright substance") is from the same root but comes from the Indo-Austronesian contact-zone shared with Sanskrit. An important anthropological note: the Batak indigenous people of Sumatra negotiated with the tree-spirit by ritual-dance before taking the camphor from the tree; the harvest of camphor remained, until the colonial period, bound to an animist rite. This animist setting is lost on entry into the Indian-Ocean trade; only the word and the substance remain — *the spiritual sense of camphor lasts until its passage to the Baghdad apothecary; there it fossilises as a technical term.* The ethnography of a substance is erased before the objectivity of pharmacy.
