İkinci Dünya Savaşı'nda Bletchley Park'ta Alan Turing, Gordon Welchman ve diğerleri Enigma'yı ve Tunny'yi kırarken sözcük "*cipher*" ve "*encipherment*" matematiksel kesinlikle tanımlandı.       en: |
        Charles Babbage broke the Vigenère cipher in 1854, but never published; Friedrich Kasiski published the same method independently in 1863. From the mid-nineteenth century onward, *cipher* belonged not to courtly intrigue but to the military and diplomatic institution. At Bletchley Park during the Second World War, Alan Turing, Gordon Welchman and others broke Enigma and Tunny, and through that work *cipher* and *encipherment* were defined with mathematical precision. In the same stretch, English scientific vocabulary fixed the distinction between *cipher* and *code* (the first character-level, the second a wholesale lexical recoding). Claude Shannon's 1949 *Communication Theory of Secrecy Systems* formalised modern cryptography inside information theory; what had been the courtly cotton of the nineteenth century became, overnight, the mathematics chair of the state.

      en: |
        Leon Battista Alberti's <em>De Componendis Cifris</em> (1467) is the first systematic Western treatment of polyalphabetic cipher; the title carries *cifra* on its face. The word now meant not just "numeral" but "writing concealed in numerals": French *chiffre* fastened the sense "cipher" firmly, and *déchiffrer*, "to decipher," entered the vocabulary as a derived verb. The "Black Chambers" of the European chanceries — in Paris from the 1590s, in Vienna from the seventeenth century — were by then employing staffs whose job was to open the diplomatic post and break its ciphers. The name of a digit, within three hundred years, had become the word behind the eye of the state.

      en: |
        Adelard of Bath's translation of al-Khwārizmī, c. 1142-1145 — *Liber Ysagogarum Alchorismi* — set the Arabic <span class="term" lang="ar">صِفْر</span> down in the Latin pen as *cifra*. The word was part of the mathematical lexicon Latinised by the Toledan translators; but unlike most of the other technical loans of that workshop, *cifra* refused a single orbit. In French it became *chiffre*, again beginning as "digit" and through the fourteenth century also acquiring "writing concealed in digits". Medieval English borrowed both branches: <em>zero</em> from Italian (after Pacioli, 1494) and <em>cipher</em> from French (through Old French *cifre, chiffre*) — first settled use c. 1400. *From the same Arabic word, on the same Latin page, in the same English tongue*: two distinct words.

        When al-Khwārizmī wrote *Kitāb al-Jamʿ wa-t-Tafrīq bi-Ḥisāb al-Hind* (Book of Addition and Subtraction in Indian Arithmetic) at the Bayt al-Ḥikma around 825, the word <span class="term" lang="ar">صِفْر</span> was drawn from the ordinary Arabic sense of "empty, bare" and made into the Arabic equivalent of the Indian <em>śūnya</em> (the place-marker of emptiness in positional arithmetic). At that stage the word had nothing to do with secrecy or concealment: it was a plain mathematical term naming the slot held open in the place-value system. In its Arabic life *ṣifr* meant only one thing — *the missing digit, the empty channel*. The word's marriage with secrecy and cipher would happen three hundred years later in Europe — not on a Baghdad desk, but on Latin paper. For "emptiness" to become "concealment", the word first had to leave Baghdad; in Baghdad it had concealed nothing.
