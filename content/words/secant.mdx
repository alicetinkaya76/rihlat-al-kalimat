      en: |
        One of the six basic functions of modern trigonometry — sine, cosine, tangent, cotangent, secant, cosecant. In modern computation a separate secant table is rarely needed; the notation *1/cos θ* is usually enough. But in the historical-geometric development of trigonometry, secant is a practical object: the distance, on a circle, between an external point and the point where the line touches the tangent. In ancient gnomon-astronomy practice, this distance is an engineering quantity tied to the cosecant of the elevation angle of the sun.

      en: |
        Thomas Fincke (1561-1656), the Danish physician-mathematician born in Flensburg, in his *Geometriae rotundi libri XIV* (printed at Basel in 1583), established the modern European trigonometric naming system. He replaced the table formerly called *umbra versa* (the reversed shadow) with *secans* (cutting), borrowed from Apollonius's *Conica*; in the same book he also standardised the terminology of tangens, sinus rectus and sinus versus. Fincke's innovation was a clean terminological decision: he abandoned the *umbra* (shadow) terminology used for gnomon-astronomy (the Arab *ẓill* and *quṭr al-ẓill* tables), and took refuge in the *circle-geometry* terminology (Apollonius's touching and cutting concepts) in its place.

        Fincke's choice was historically successful. Through the seventeenth century, Vieta, Napier, Briggs, Gunter — every European writer on trigonometry adopted his terminology. The modern *sin/cos/tan/cot/sec/csc* notation is the direct descendant of that 1583 renewal. The terminological vein of Arab gnomon-astronomy (the shadow, the gnomon-height, the shadow-diameter) does not pass into Latin European mathematics; only the tables and the techniques of calculation remain.

      en: |
        In the Arabic astronomical tables, secant is called *al-quṭr al-ẓill* (the shadow-diameter) — its geometric reading is clear: the distance from the tip of the gnomon's shadow to the gnomon's top, that is, the hypotenuse. Al-Battānī in the *Zīj al-Ṣābiʾ* presents this table in the same chapter as *ẓill*: for the same angle, *ẓill* and *quṭr al-ẓill* can be read side by side. Abū al-Wafāʾ in the tenth century derives, by the Pythagorean theorem, the ratio between the two; he makes clear the Arab form of the modern secant-tangent identity (*sec² − tan² = 1*).

        For Arab astronomers, secant — *quṭr al-ẓill* — is a more practical quantity than sine, because it gives the length of the direct line of sight from the tip of the gnomon's shadow to the observer; it is used in navigation, in determining the *qibla*, in time-of-prayer astronomical practice. This practical-engineering context is lost in the Latinisation; Fincke's clean *secans* (cutting) names a geometric quantity independent of the gnomon-context.

        Apollonius of Perga (c. 240-190 BC) was the most important figure of Hellenistic geometry after Euclid. The eight books of the *Conica* — the systematic geometry of conic sections (circle, ellipse, parabola, hyperbola). At the opening of Book I three possible relations between a circle and a straight line are defined: (i) the line does not cut the circle at all, (ii) the line touches the circle at a single point (Greek *haptesthai* = Latin *tangere*) — this is the *tangens*, (iii) the line cuts the circle at two points (Greek *temnein* = Latin *secare*) — this is the *secans*.

        Apollonius's terminology remains for fourteen centuries a purely-geometric conceptualisation; it is not used for any trigonometric function. The Arab astronomers (al-Battānī, Abū al-Wafāʾ) name the trigonometric quantities with *ẓill* and *quṭr al-ẓill* (terminology coming from the gnomon-context). In 1583 Thomas Fincke cuts across these two terminological veins: he takes the words *tangens* and *secans* from Apollonius's *Conica* and re-applies them as the names of trigonometric functions. The pure-geometric Greek names become a technical vein in the Arab gnomon-practice — the word-history of modern trigonometry.

      en: |
        The pre-mathematical history of the secant function is tied to the practice of gnomon astronomy. On the upper corners of the Mesopotamian ziggurats a vertical stone stick (the gnomon) — the length of its shadow gave the sun-time, and the direct distance from the top of the stick to the tip of the shadow (in modern terms: gnomon-height × sec(angle) ÷ cos(angle)) was used in angle-measurements from the stars. This practice read the three basic quantities of a right triangle — the adjacent side (cosine), the opposite side (tangent), and the hypotenuse (secant) — from a single astronomical instrument.

        These practical measurements of the ancient world were not a mathematical conceptualisation; they were only the practice of sun-clocks and star-positions. To turn the measurements into a mathematical concept took two steps: (i) Greek geometry (Apollonius's touching-and-cutting naming) sets up a pure-abstract terminology; (ii) Arab astronomy (al-Battānī's tabulation) attaches that terminology to numerical computation. The modern *secant* function — as a numerical value — is the joint result of those two steps: Apollonius's conceptual term, Arab astronomy's numerical table, Fincke's terminological union.
