      en: |
        Ibn al-ʿAwwām's *Kitāb al-Filāḥa* (Seville, c. 1180) places *al-faṣfaṣa* among the three key components of Andalusi irrigation-agriculture (the others being grain and sugarcane). The significance is this: the Andalusi water-mill economy (the *acequia*-system: from Arabic *as-sāqiya*, "the water-carrier") is not an annual but a continuous, multi-cropping agricultural system; a legume that can be cut three or four times a year and that fixes nitrogen back to the soil while feeding animals is the ecological keystone of that system. *Al-faṣfaṣa* serves the double function of forage and soil-improver: the continuity of feeding horse, donkey and cow makes possible Andalusi animal husbandry and, in turn, Andalusi plough-agriculture. Its arrival in al-Andalus: the seed of *aspist* (the New Persian "horse-feed"), grown in eastern Iran since at least the Sasanian period, passes after the Arab conquest from Baghdad into Ifrīqiya, and from there into al-Andalus; the Latin agronomic literature, by contrast, is ignorant of it until the eighteenth century. Andalusi agriculture, that is, holds for eight hundred years a system of ecological knowledge closed off from Europe.

Arapça morfoloji aşçısının bu tekniği — yabancı kökü Arapça kalıba dökmek — Endülüs Arapçasındaki onlarca Pers, Yunan ve Latin ödünçle çalışır. *Al-faṣfaṣa* bunun en sessiz örneklerindendir; çünkü sözcük o kadar iyi Arapçalaşmıştır ki sözcüğün Pers kökeni filolojik analiz olmadan görülmez.

        The Persian word *aspist* passes into Arabic through two processes. The first: because of the absence of the *p*-phoneme in the consonantal tonality of Arabic, the Persian *p* of *aspist* (in its inner syllable) shifts to *f* — a canonical shift seen in other Persian loans into Arabic (e.g. *pīl* > *fīl* "elephant"). The second: the disyllabic Persian shape (*as-pist*), through inner-consonantal reduplication, slides into a four-syllable symmetrical Arabic shape: *fiṣ-fiṣ-a* (in the neo-coinage of the root *f-ṣ-f-ṣ*, with a reduplicative semantic slide meaning "side-by-side, plural, abundant"). The result: the word looks, on the outside, entirely Arabic (a three-letter root, plausible Arabic morphology); on the inside it is purely Persian. *A word renewing its morphological clothing*: a foreign word puts on a shirt cut to the host language's grammatical template, so it can look native in the host language's lexicon. This Arabic-morphology-cook's technique — casting a foreign root into an Arabic mould — operates on dozens of Persian, Greek and Latin loans in Andalusi Arabic. *Al-faṣfaṣa* is among the most silent examples; the word is so well Arabicised that its Persian origin is invisible without philological analysis.

      en: |
        *Aspist* in Old Persian is a compound of two roots: *aspa* (horse) + *-pist* (fodder, herb). The Achaemenid Empire (550-330 BC) — the age in Persian history when the horse is the military-economic foundation — cultivates *aspist* actively across Anatolia, Cappadocia and Syria for war-horse supply; in Xenophon's *Anabasis* (fourth century BC), the herb harvested on the Persian side, called there "the Median grass," is clearly *aspist* (Greek *mēdikē*, "of Media," because the first Persian province the Greeks encountered was Media). Theophrastus, Aristotle's pupil, in his *Historia Plantarum* (fourth-third century BC), describes *mēdikē* as a horse-fodder brought from the Persian Empire to Athens late. Pliny, *Naturalis Historia* XVIII.144, tells of its passage to Rome "by the armies of Darius during the Greek-Persian wars." *A plant carried along with the millennium-long military-economic system carried on the back of the horse.* The word is a by-product of that system. Latin *medica herba* ("the Median grass") is the Greek-Latin name of the same plant — a parallel Persian-Greek line existing in Europe before al-Andalus. Two paths: Persia → Greek → Latin → French *luzerne*, and Persia → Arab → al-Andalus → Spanish *alfalfa*. The same seed, the same plant, two words, two etymologies.
