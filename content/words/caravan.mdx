      en: |
        The modern word *caravan* circulates with at least four parallel senses. The *container-ship convoy* — of the roughly 19,000 ships that pass through the Suez Canal each year, a group binds itself together from the exit of one port to another; though the official term in the maritime industry is *convoy*, the economic literature alludes to it through the metaphor of *modern Silk Road caravans*. The *military convoy* — in the terminology of armies, the direct heir of the caravan: an escorted group of road vehicles carrying fuel, ammunition or soldiers. The *refugee caravan* — in the terminology of the 2010s, particularly used for the on-foot migration waves heading from Central America to the United States (the Honduran *caravan* of 2018 carried this use to a symbolic level). The *caravan / RV* — in British and American English, the recreational towed or motorised dwelling-vehicle.

        Between these four uses is a common structure: *a group of units* + *coordinated movement* + *a kind of bond to a lodging-infrastructure*. The system-wholeness of the Persian-Arab root *kārwān* + *kārwān-sarāy* (caravan + caravanserai) still works beneath the modern kinship of meanings. The ports of container ships (the integral nodes of loading), the base-stations of military convoys, the stopping-points of refugee caravans, the RV parks — are all contemporary heirs of the *caravanserai* function. The etymological root does not carry only a word; it carries an idea of infrastructure.

      en: |
        Through the sixteenth-to-nineteenth centuries the word *caravan* becomes permanent in the European languages, but its meaning shifts: away from the bare reality of the merchant caravan (Antioch in the 1190s, Venetian merchant documents of 1250) it is drawn into a literary-romantic use as *an Eastern landscape*. John Mandeville's fanciful fourteenth-century *Travels*, Marco Polo's narratives, Antoine Galland's French translation of *Mille et Une Nuits* in 1704-1717 — through these texts the word *caravan* becomes the carrier of *the Eastern imagination*: the desert-rhyme of camels loaded up, the curtained caravanserais, the storytelling tradition by the fireside under the stars.

        Nineteenth-century Orientalist painting and photography carries this romantic use to its peak: Eugène Delacroix's sketches from his trip to Morocco in 1832, John Frederick Lewis's caravan paintings of Cairo in the 1840s, David Roberts's lithographs of Palestine in 1838-1839, Felix Bonfils's caravan photographs of the 1870s. The word settles in European speech through this visual-literary circulation, but its sense shifts from *institution* (the Abbasid *barīd*, the Mamluk *Maḥmal*) to *landscape* — the actual infrastructure remains invisible, only *the picture* is quoted. This drift persists to the twentieth century; only the modern history of science (Faroqhi's *Travel and Artisans* of 2014, Pedersen's *Classical Persian Literature* of 2010) brings the institutional layer back to view.

        The first written entry of the word into Latin appears in the Antioch of the 1190s, in the documents of the Crusader kingdom: *caravana* — in the sense of "an escorted merchant convoy of Eastern origin". It comes out of the reality of the region: the Crusader kingdom of Antioch (1098-1268) was in continuous contact with the Mamluk-Ayyubid world by caravan route; a Crusader knight or a Pisan merchant would join the weekly caravans between Aleppo and Antioch, and the word entered their working vocabulary.

Through Polo's journey to China the terminology of the caravan is a systematic category: *caravanseragl* (the caravanserai), *caravane* (the caravan), the head of the caravan, the structure of caravan-property. The Persian-Arabic word and the institutional structure behind it enter Europe *at the same time*.

        At the same period the Italian sea-cities of the Mediterranean prefer Arabic *caravana* to native-Latin alternatives (*convoy* navalis, *grex mercatorum*) — because in their contact with the East the institutional weight of meaning of the word was the more productive way to denote the working accounting (customs tariff, escort wages, insurance risk). The linguistic economy of commercial practice: Latin *grex* is a general word, the Arabic *caravana* is the full-package signature of an institution.

      en: |
        One of the structural innovations of the Abbasid caliphate was the *barīd* system (a direct heir of the Persian *frasakh* postal service): a chain of stations starting at Baghdad and extending to North Africa, al-Andalus and India. In the time of Caliph al-Maʾmūn (813-833), at every ~30 km there is a *ribāṭ* (watch-and-lodging) or a *khān* (caravanserai). At each station: a fresh row of horses or camels, a guard-soldier, food, water, an overnight stay. A caravan covers on average 30-40 km a day; a Baghdad-Damascus journey takes about 20-25 days, Baghdad-Cairo about 50 days.

        This infrastructure unites three distinct functions:
        (1) **State post** — the officers of the *barīd* carry the caliph's orders, tax reports and military messages; al-Khaṭīb al-Baghdādī's eleventh-century *Tārīkh Baghdād* lists the state's postal line systematically.
        (2) **The Ḥajj caravans** — the annual *Maḥmal* (Egypt-Ḥijāz), Damascus-Ḥijāz, Baghdad-Ḥijāz, Yemen-Ḥijāz caravans of 8-30 thousand pilgrims plus military escort plus merchant freight, a great ceremony, a kind of half-imperial display of magnificence. These caravans, which last 7-8 weeks, are not only a religious ritual but a diplomatic and commercial network — the annual single occasion of meeting for Mamluk-Ottoman-Ḥijāzī-Indian merchants.
        (3) **Trade caravans** — regular monthly departures from Baghdad along the Silk Road (Bukhara, Samarkand, Khotan, Chang'an), to the Iran-India coast (Basra, Hormuz, Diu), and to the Maghreb (Tunis, Fez, al-Andalus). In the Geniza documents (Goitein's *A Mediterranean Society* of 1967-88), the letters of the Jewish merchants of Cairo record this caravan systematicity in detail.

        The three functions share the same infrastructure: the same caravanserais, the same wells, the same roads, the same security systems. The word *qayrawān* names this three-layered institution as a whole. In Mamluk Cairo (1250-1517) the infrastructure reaches its peak — the reign of *Sultan Qāʾit Bāy* (1468-1496) is famous for the building of twelve great caravanserais. It is this mature institutional structure that the thirteenth-century Latin merchants are naming when they say *caravana*.

        Persçe *kārwān* kelimesi Antik İran dünyasından gelir; etimolojik kökü Eski Persçe *kāra* ("ordu, halk topluluğu") + *wān* ("yöneten, koruyan") → "yürüyenleri koruyan, ordu-bekçisi". Ahameniş İmparatorluğu (550-330 BCE) zamanında bu sözcük *Royal Road* sisteminin (Susa'dan Sardis'e yaklaşık 2700 km) işleyişi içinde bir kurum-kelime haline gelir: Heredot'un yazdığı (V. Kitap, 52-54) ünlü postacılar sistemi *angareion*'un Persçe paraleli kervan-konvoyu istasyonlarda atlarını ve süvarilerini değiştirir.

        Sasani döneminde (224-651 CE) sistem *frasakh* postası olarak yeniden yapılandırılır — 1 *frasakh* yaklaşık 6 km'dir, ve her 6-12 km'de bir *chāparkhāneh* (posta-istasyonu) bulunur. Sasani frasakh sistemi'nin teknik mükemmelliği — yol kalitesi, istasyon konumlandırması, at-değişim hızı — Pers-İslam geçişinde Abbasî *barīd*'inin doğrudan modeli olur. Halife el-Me'mun'un 813-833 sistemi Sasani sisteminin operasyonel kopyasıdır; sadece Arapça'ya çevrilir (*kārwān* → *qayrawān*).

        Kelimenin köküne taşıdığı yapısal anlam: *kervan tek başına yürümez, koruyucu kurumsal yapı içinde yürür*. Bu çekirdek anlam Pers *kārwān*'dan Arap *qayrawān*'a, oradan Latin *caravana*'ya, oradan modern *caravan*'a kadar değişmeden taşınır. Kelimenin 2500 yıllık ömrünün altında bu çekirdek ilke yatar: hareket güvenlik için kurumsallaşmalı; bireysel yolcu yerine kurum-yolcu kervanın doğal birimi olmalıdır.
      en: |
        The Persian word *kārwān* comes from the ancient Iranian world; its etymological root is Old Persian *kāra* ("army, gathering of the people") + *wān* ("warden, protector") → "the protector of those who walk, the warden of an army". The first sense was a guard-participant for a military convoy or a large merchant group. In the time of the Achaemenid Empire (550-330 BCE), this word becomes an institution-word within the working of the *Royal Road* system (about 2700 km from Susa to Sardis): the Persian parallel of the famous postal system Herodotus calls *angareion* (in his Book V, 52-54) is the caravan-convoy that changes its horses and its riders at the stations.

        In the Sasanian period (224-651 CE) the system is restructured as the *frasakh* postal service — 1 *frasakh* is approximately 6 km, and at every 6-12 km there is a *chāparkhāneh* (a posting-station). The technical perfection of the Sasanian *frasakh* system — the quality of its roads, the placement of its stations, the speed of its horse-changes — becomes the direct model of the Abbasid *barīd* in the Persian-Islamic transition. The system of 813-833 of Caliph al-Maʾmūn is an operational copy of the Sasanian system, only translated into Arabic (*kārwān* → *qayrawān*).

        The structural meaning the word carries at its root: *a caravan does not walk by itself, it walks inside a protective institutional structure*. This kernel of meaning is carried unchanged from the Persian *kārwān* to the Arabic *qayrawān*, from there to the Latin *caravana*, and from there to the modern *caravan*. Beneath the two-and-a-half-thousand-year life of the word lies this kernel principle: that movement should be institutionalised for the sake of security; the natural unit of a caravan should be not the individual traveller but the institution-traveller.

        المَعنى البِنيَويُّ الذي تَحمِلُه الكَلِمةُ في جَذرِها: *لا تَسيرُ القافِلةُ وَحدَها، تَسيرُ داخِلَ بِنيةٍ مُؤَسَّسيّةٍ حامِية*. تُنقَلُ هذه النَّواةُ المَعنويّةُ دونَ تَغييرٍ من الفارِسيّةِ *كاروان* إلى العَرَبيّةِ *قَيرَوان*، وَمن هُناك إلى اللاتينيّةِ *Caravana*، وَمن هُناك إلى *Caravan* الحَديثِ. تَحتَ حياةِ الكَلِمةِ التي امتَدَّتْ أَلفَيْنِ وَخَمسَ مِئاتٍ من السَّنواتِ يَكمُنُ هذا المَبدَأُ النَّواةُ: ينبَغي أن تَتَمَأسَسَ الحَرَكةُ من أَجلِ الأَمنِ؛ يَنبَغي أن تَكونَ الوَحدةُ الطَّبيعيّةُ لِلقافِلةِ لا المُسافِرَ الفَردَ بَلِ المُسافِرَ-المُؤَسَّسة.
