      en: |
        Global coffee production today is roughly 10 million tonnes a year; Brazil, Vietnam, Colombia, Indonesia and Ethiopia lead. Turkey produces no coffee (the climate is wrong), but is concentrated on the consumption-and-service line: in the 2020s some 20,000 single-point coffeehouses were counted in Turkey. *Coffea arabica* (Arabica, the original species of the Yemen-Ethiopia line, 60% of the world's coffee) and *Coffea canephora* (Robusta, hardier and higher in caffeine, the other 40%) — two species, one global market.

        English <span class="term">coffee</span>, Italian *caffè*, French *café*, German *Kaffee*, Spanish *café*, Portuguese *café* — all these European tongues are the late links of the chain: Arabic <span class="term" lang="ar">القَهْوة</span> (*al-qahwa*) → Turkish *kahve* → European *café*. They sprang from Turkish: when sixteenth-century Venetian merchants carried back the drink they had met in the coffeehouses of Istanbul, the word they carried was *qahveh*, not *qahwa*. Dutch *koffie* is a separate branch of the Turkish-Arabic line, via Djibouti, from the seventeenth-century Dutch East Indies trade.

        Modern coffee is divided into three "waves": the first wave is the instant and supermarket coffee of 1900-1980 (Maxwell House, Nescafé), the second the Starbucks-Costa chain of 1980-2000 (espresso-based milk drinks), the third — post-2000 — "Specialty" coffee: single-origin, single-estate, dated by roast, brewed pour-over or espresso, coffee evaluated for *terroir* the way wine is. The philosophical foundation of the third wave openly rediscovers the Yemen-Ethiopia line — *bunna* (the traditional Ethiopian name for coffee) is a Sunday menu item in a Brooklyn café. A single fifteenth-century Sufi drink, seven centuries on, is still the same bean, but now in the hands of a Pacific Northwest barista.

Lloyd's of London — modern denizcilik sigortacılığı — 1688'de Edward Lloyd'un Tower Street'teki kahvehanesinde, gemi-sahipleri ve sigortacılar arasında imzalanan poliçelerden doğdu. Sigortayı, borsayı, gazeteyi, parlamenter konuşmayı — modern bürjuva düzenin önemli bir bölümünü — bir Yemen-Etiyopya çekirdeğine borçlu olduğunu söylemek abartı değil, kayıt durumudur.
      en: |
        European coffee's first gate is Venice: by about 1645 the first *bottega del caffè* — a counter serving Turkish coffee — opened; in 1683 the Caffè Florian, still standing, opened on Piazza San Marco. The second gate is Oxford: in 1650 Jacob, a Syrian Jew from the Levant, opened beside Queen's College what is known as Europe's first public coffeehouse. The third, and most consequential, is London: in 1652 Pasqua Rosée, born in Smyrna, opened a coffeehouse on St Michael Cornhill; by 1700 more than 2,000 coffeehouses were counted in London.

        The economic impact of this institution is incalculable. Lloyd's of London — modern marine insurance — was born in 1688 in Edward Lloyd's coffeehouse on Tower Street, from the policies signed between ship-owners and insurers there. When the *Royal Exchange* expelled from its official list the "garrulous brokers" of the coffeehouses at the beginning of the eighteenth century, those men relocated to Jonathan's coffeehouse; Jonathan's coffeehouse became, in 1773, formally the London Stock Exchange. The *Tatler* (1709) and *Spectator* (1711) — the ancestors of modern journalism — kept their editorial offices in a coffeehouse, and printed the address openly for their readers.

        Social structure too was rearranged: <em>penny university</em> was an English expression for the coffeehouse — admission a penny, unlimited conversation inside. The Enlightenment's "public reason" (Habermas's "bourgeois public sphere" of his 1962 thesis) emerges, historically, from these quarters. Important detail: this conversation — political, religious, scientific, financial — runs on caffeine. While alcohol remained the evening drink of the seventeenth century, the daytime "working mind" became dependent on a Sufi drink. To say that insurance, the stock exchange, the newspaper, and parliamentary speech — important parts of the modern bourgeois order — are indebted to a Yemen-Ethiopia bean is not exaggeration; it is record.

      en: |
        Coffee arrived in Istanbul in the mid-sixteenth century. The classical record: two coffeemen named Şems of Damascus and Hekim of Aleppo opened the first coffeehouse around Tahtakale in 1554-55. The earliest patrons were ulema and state secretaries — a public space opened for reading, chess, the recitation of poetry. Within thirty years the coffeehouses of Istanbul ran to hundreds; in the same years Cairo and Aleppo saw parallel explosions.

        The classical Arab-Turkish method of preparation was standardised in these centuries: the bean is pounded in a wooden mortar, roasted at length over charcoal, cooled, very finely ground (not in a flour-mill but in a hand-cranked brass *değirmen*), simmered in a small copper *cezve* with cold water and sugar (or without), and poured quickly into the cup so that the grounds (*telve*) settle to the bottom. From Istanbul to Athens, Damascus, Sarajevo, Belgrade, and Tunis, this procedure became the single standard for the whole Ottoman basin — "Turkish coffee" consolidated into one recipe.

        But from the start trouble followed: because the coffeehouse was not only a place where coffee was served but a place where people *socialised*, state authority saw it as the centre of improper speech — political, religious. The 1511 Meccan ban issued by the Yemeni governor Khā'ir Bey under Bayezid II — withdrawn within seven years but setting the rule — became a model. In the Ottoman lands Sultan Murad III (1574-95) and especially Murad IV (1623-40) banned coffee repeatedly; Murad IV, prowling Istanbul in disguise, sent to the gallows persons he caught drinking coffee. The bans did not hold, because the infrastructure was set: not to have a coffeehouse for one's quarter had come to mean not having a quarter.

        In the seventeenth-century Ottoman coffeehouse the drink did not carry meaning alone; an accompanying profession was the *meddâh* (storyteller), another the *kahve falı* (reader of fortunes from the grounds at the bottom of the cup), another the warden of the nargile. European travellers (Galland, Postel, Sherley) describe this complex; and Venetian merchants carry the same complex and reconstruct it at the Caffè Florian (Stratum 2).

      en: |
        Coffee's earliest documented purpose is as a devotional tool. In the mid-fifteenth century, in Yemen, Sufi dervishes of the Shadhili order — especially around Aden and Mokha — had to stand or sit through long night hours of *dhikr* (the rhythmic repetition of the names of God). The classical record reports that around 1454 a Yemeni Sufi, Shaykh Jamāl al-Dīn al-Dhabhānī, brought back from Ethiopia a plant, gave its decoction to his dervishes — and the dervishes could stay awake through the *dhikr*, and word spread.

        The name they gave the drink is curious: *qahwa*, in classical Arabic, was a word for *wine*. Why did the dervishes choose it? The standard reading: like wine, coffee suppresses appetite (in classical medicine *qahwa* had also meant "that which suppresses appetite"), and the dervishes placed the new substance in an existing conceptual category. The deep reading is finer: coffee, for them, was the *ḥalāl* equivalent of wine — a stimulant, an evening sociality, a wakefulness-drink — but religiously permitted. To call it *qahwa* was to rebaptise the old word with a new object.

        Late in the fifteenth century, coffee passed from the Yemeni coast along the *ḥajj* road to Mecca, where it spread also among non-Sufi Muslims. Public coffee-gatherings opened in Mecca; coffee was drunk before and after the night prayers. The diffusion alarmed the governor Khā'ir Bey: men were gathering at the coffee stations more than at the mosque, and the coffeehouses were filling beyond the prayer hall. In 1511 Khā'ir Bey sought a fatwa ordering the suppression of coffee-drinking and the closure of coffeehouses; the Meccan qāḍī signed it; the ban was issued. Sufi shaykhs and Egyptian ulema challenged it; the Mālikī qāḍī of Cairo rejected the ban; within seven years it was lifted. The brief episode left a cornerstone in the history — that coffee had entered Mecca established it as a pan-Islamic drink, and the lifting of the ban inscribed the ruling "coffee is permissible" into religious norm.

        The coffee plant (*Coffea arabica*) grows wild as a native species in the Ethiopian highlands — most notably in the Kaffa province. Of more than 100 *Coffea* species, only three have been domesticated (*arabica*, *canephora*/Robusta, *liberica*); and the *arabica* species exists in the wild only in Ethiopia's wet upland — 1200-2000 m — and nowhere else. This is a continental origin; roughly 99% of modern coffee genetic diversity is still collected within a 50,000 km² zone of Ethiopia.

        Local use — Ethiopia's own internal coffee tradition — is older by a millennium. *Bunna* (Amharic, Tigrinya, Oromo for "coffee") was a bean that had long been chewed, roasted and eaten, or rolled with butter and given to warriors going to war. As a brewed decoction, according to the standard scholarly record, the bean is a development of the late thirteenth or fourteenth century; before then the leaves were brewed, the bean consumed in fat or in a cake. In modern rural Ethiopia the *bunna* ceremony — washing the beans at home, roasting them on the fire, grinding them in a stone mortar, preparing three rounds for the drinking (*abol-tona-baraka*) — survives as a contemporary continuation of the traditional use.

        The Khaldi legend — that an Ethiopian goatherd named Khaldi noticed his goats dancing through the night after eating coffee cherries, tried the cherries himself, and discovered wakefulness — was first put in writing by the Roman Faustus Naironi in *De Saluberrima Cahve* in 1671. The likely oral origin of the report may have been Yemeni (the name "Khaldi" is not Ethiopian), but the narrative became part of a late-European coffeehouse mythology. The historical record does not confirm the goatherd, but the observation that goats love the coffee plant survives as folk observation in rural Ethiopia — the bone of the legend is real in observation, the flesh literary.

        The passage to Yemen is reckoned around the mid-fourteenth century. Yemen had been carrying on commerce with Ethiopia across the Strait of Aden for a millennium — Abyssinian slavery on the Red Sea side, Syrian merchants on the Ethiopian side, a continuous traffic. A bean crosses on that line, begins to be grown on the mountainous south of Yemen — Jabal Sabir, Jabal Manakhah, the highlands above Mokha. Yemen's climate resembles Ethiopia's; but it is Yemen's coastal export port, Mokha, that wins — the term "Mocha coffee" has been the global market's name for the trade since the seventeenth century, and survives as a metonym for Yemeni coffee.
