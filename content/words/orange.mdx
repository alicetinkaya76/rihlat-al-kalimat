        The global orange harvest today is roughly 75 million tonnes a year; Brazil produces more than half, with China, India, the USA and Mexico following. Turkey, along the Mediterranean and Aegean coasts, produces about 1.7 million tonnes — tenth in the world. *Citrus × sinensis* — "the Chinese citrus" — is the sweet orange, the thing modern speech means by "orange". Around it stand its relatives: *Citrus × aurantium* (the bitter or Seville orange — the only legitimate base for marmalade), *Citrus × bergamia* (bergamot — the aromatic oil of Earl Grey tea), *Citrus reticulata* (the mandarin), *Citrus × paradisi* (the grapefruit).

        In English, *orange* names both the fruit and the colour. The chronology is curious: the word enters English in the late fourteenth century as a fruit-name (*orenge*, by way of Old French); its use for the colour waits until the late sixteenth. Before that English speakers said *ġeolurēad* ("yellow-red") or simply *yellow-red* for what we now call orange. Other languages behave the same way — Turkish *turuncu* is a colour borrowed from Persian *turunj* (the bitter citrus); Greek *πορτοκαλί* is again a colour borrowed from the fruit. The name of a fruit precedes the name of a colour: language learns the world first as food, only afterwards as eye.

        Turkish *portakal* is a direct loan from Greek *πορτοκάλι*, and its story begins with the sweet orange's arrival by Portuguese ships in the Mediterranean in the sixteenth century (see Stratum 2). Arabic carries two words side by side: <span class="term" lang="ar">البُرتُقال</span> (*al-burtuqāl*, the eastern Mediterranean preference, from Portugal) and <span class="term" lang="ar">نارَنج</span> (*nāranj*, classical, more often for the bitter orange, though in Maghrebi Arabic also for the sweet).         Until the start of the sixteenth century the orange citrus of the Mediterranean was one thing: *Citrus × aurantium*, the bitter Seville orange inherited from al-Andalus as *naranja*. The sweet orange — *Citrus × sinensis* — had been cultivated in southeastern China for two millennia but had not reached the Mediterranean. The Portuguese spice network opened by Vasco da Gama's landfall at Calicut in 1498 brought, as a side-product, the Chinese sweet orange; the first nursery plantings in Lisbon gardens are documented from the early 1520s.

        For the Mediterranean market this new fruit — thick-rinded like the older *naranja*, but with flesh sweeter than any citrus the basin had known — earned a new name. Genoese and Venetian merchants called it *arancio del Portogallo* or simply *portogallo*. From there the word passed into the southern Italian dialects, into Greek (*πορτοκάλι*), and from Greek into Turkish (*portakal*), Ottoman Arabic, and modern eastern Mediterranean Arabic (*burtuqāl*). The same word survives in Romanian (*portocală*), Bulgarian (*портокал*), Georgian (*ფორთოხალი*), Persian (*porteghāl*), Azerbaijani (*portağal*), and Gulf Arabic — an integrated eastern-Mediterranean linguistic neighbourhood.

        The curious thing: Portugal itself does not call the sweet orange *portugal*. The Portuguese name it *laranja* (a softened reflex of the older *naranja*) — the word with which they brought the fruit did not rename it; rather, they extended the existing name to cover the new variety. Spanish does the same with *naranja*. The eastern half of the Mediterranean named the orange from the *event*; the western half named it from the *thing*: in the east it carries the name of a country, in the west the name of a fruit. Two hemispheres, one citrus, two distinct linguistic gestures.

        This episode follows the historical closure of al-Andalus (Granada 1492). Andalusian horticultural know-how — irrigation networks, tree-grafting, annual calendars — had migrated to the Maghreb and to the Ottoman eastern Mediterranean. When the sweet orange arrived in the basin, the infrastructure to raise it was already there: by the end of the seventeenth century the first major orange groves opened in Sicily, Valencia, Cyprus, and along the northern slopes of the Bosphorus. Andalusian successors grafted the new sweet orange onto the traditional bitter rootstock.

        The Andalusian orange is not sweet but bitter: *Citrus × aurantium*, the "Seville orange". It had settled along the Maghrebi-Andalusian line by the late tenth century; in 1058 a manual of garden agriculture by Ibn Baṣṣāl of Toledo describes the care of the *nāranj* tree. In Andalusian cities of the eleventh through thirteenth centuries the tree played a double role: first, a domestic courtyard ornamental — leaved, blossomed, fragrance-giving, the tree that alone explains Seville's *Patio de los Naranjos*; second, a pharmacy crop — its peel for *neroli* essential oil, its flower for *azahar* distillation, its fruit for syrup-making and for sharpening sauces.

        The modern Seville orange descends directly. The bitter orange ripening in mid-March in Seville is the same bitterness; the city's annual harvest is around 25,000 tonnes, and most of it is exported to the United Kingdom. The reason: marmalade. The Scottish-English breakfast preserve *marmalade* — the word itself from Portuguese *marmelada*, in turn from *marmelo* "quince" — began as a quince preserve. In the seventeenth century the Keiller family's atelier in Dundee began using bitter oranges, and by the nineteenth century *marmalade* had become the world's standard name for the bitter-orange preserve. A single late-medieval Andalusian tree is the staple of a Scottish breakfast table.

        The word *naranja* / *naranjo* (the tree) passed from Andalusian Arabic *nāranj* into Spanish, and, as an extension of the Spanish-Portuguese conquest, into the Americas — Florida, California, Brazil, the Caribbean. Florida's orange industry became the world's largest in the twentieth century, but its rootstocks are still descendants of sixteenth-century Andalusian grafts. The same continuity holds in the orchards of Sicily and Valencia. Five centuries after the fall of al-Andalus, the tree an Andalusian raised in his courtyard remains the genetic foundation of the global citrus economy.

        *Nāranj* — Indian *nāraṅga* arriving in Arabic via Persian *nārang* — appears as a documented substance in the Baghdad and Damascus pharmacy of the ninth century. Early Islamic medical texts (al-Kindī, al-Rāzī, Ibn Sīnā) record bitter orange in multiple uses: a fruit syrup as a digestive (*sharbat al-nāranj*), a peel infusion as an appetite-opener, a flower juice as a heart tonic, a flower distillate as a perfumed water (*mā' al-zahr*, "flower water"). Arab pharmacy adopted the bitter orange not as a plain food but as a multi-formula plant.

        More importantly: Arab pharmacy was the first tradition to see the botanical integrity of the citrus family. In antique Rome *citrus* had been confused with *citron* (Citrus medica, the rough, thick-rinded, acidic kind). Arab medicine dispelled the confusion: in Ibn Sīnā's *Qānūn* (c. 1025) the plant book lists *utruj* (citron), *nāranj* (bitter orange), and *līmūn* (lemon) as separate substances, each described in its own usage. The earliest drafts of modern botanical taxonomy lie in these Arab records.

        Distillation technology was another dimension. Andalusian and Egyptian pharmacists distilled bitter-orange blossoms in the alembic to produce *azahar* or *neroli* water (the principal essence of European perfumery, named in the seventeenth century after Anna Maria della Tremoille); this proceeded with the *alembic* (Ar. *al-anbīq*), and runs in the same library, just in another room, as alcohol Stratum 4 (cf. the *alcohol* article). All this constituted the routes by which ninth-to-eleventh-century Arab chemistry entered pharmacy.

      en: |
        The Sanskrit *नारङ्ग* (*nāraṅga*) appears in seventh-century medical texts (Suśruta, Caraka Saṃhitā) as the name of a citrus. The word is not a primitive in Sanskrit; the standard reading derives it from a Dravidian substrate (*nāram-kāy* = "oil fruit") or from an Austroasiatic substrate. An older popular etymology — *nāga-raṅga* "elephant-delight" or "elephant-coloured", from the observation that elephants loved the orange trees of the Indian forests — has been carried into many modern Indian languages.

        The Indian citrus, historically, was two things: *Citrus medica* (the citron, *bījapūra* "full of seeds"), and *Citrus × aurantium* (the bitter orange). The sweet orange — *Citrus × sinensis* — did not grow in the Indus; it arose in southeastern China, an eastern geography of its own. The Indian commercial corridor sent the bitter orange through a Persian stage into Mesopotamia between the fourth and fifth centuries CE; Pahlavi borrowed it as *nārang*, and from there Arabic *nāranj* was born.

        Modern botanical genetics (Wu et al. 2018, *Nature*) finally illuminated the story: sweet orange (*Citrus × sinensis*) and bitter orange (*Citrus × aurantium*) are separate hybrids whose three common ancestors are *Citrus reticulata* (the mandarin), *Citrus maxima* (the pomelo), and *Citrus medica* (the citron). The bitter orange arose by natural hybridisation along the Indus-Burma border, probably around 2000 BCE; the sweet orange emerged in southern China, again by natural hybridisation, but more recently (around 300 BCE). That one word names two distinct fruits — that a Sanskrit root also covers a Chinese fruit — is the result of language generalising faster than botany.

        The last projection of the Indian origin is Turkish *turunç* — via Persian *turunj*, itself a separate Sanskrit-Persian-corridor loan. *Turunç* in Turkish names the bitter orange; *turuncu* is the colour-name derived from the fruit. The same fruit nourishes Turkish through three separate words — *turunç* (bitter), *portakal* (sweet), *turuncu* (the colour) — and all three descend from Sanskrit *nāraṅga*.
