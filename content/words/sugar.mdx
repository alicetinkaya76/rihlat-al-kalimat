        Algebra, zero, algorithm — these reached Latin from the writing
        desk of the Toledo workshop. *Sukkar* took another route: the
        wharf. Egyptian refineries of the ninth and tenth centuries
        (al-Fusṭāṭ, then Cairo) laid down the industrial base of sugar
        production: repeated boiling in copper *qulam*, syrup, cooling,
        the migration from cane juice to crystalline grain. Al-Andalus,
        having brought cane from the Indo-Persian belt by the mid-ninth
        century, naturalised it to the Mediterranean climate — one of
        the central examples of Andrew Watson's *Andalusian Agricultural
        Revolution*. Sicily under the emirate (831-1091) became a second
        production zone. Both lay directly on Christian Europe's doorstep.

        Through the eleventh to thirteenth centuries, the merchants of
        Pisa, Genoa and Venice carried refined *sukkar* northward via
        Egypt and Acre. The word entered Latin not by an academic
        translation but along the pages of trading ledgers: by 1162 the
        Genoese notarial archives record *zuccarum*; by 1191, *zucharo*.
        Italian *zucchero* emerges here; Old French *sukre* follows the
        same path; Middle English *sugre, sucre* is first attested
        around 1300. For its first century in Europe, sugar is a
        spice-and-pharmacy product: in *electuaria*, in medicines, on
        royal tables — it has not yet broken honey's monopoly on
        sweetness.

        The signature of this stratum is that the word entered three
        European languages *at once*. Toledo's *algebra* came from the
        translation of a single book. *Sugar* came from hundreds of
        trading ledgers, anonymous cargoes, customs receipts. It is the
        child not of an author but of a *market*.
