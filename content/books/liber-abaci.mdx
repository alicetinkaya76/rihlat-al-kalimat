    heritage of the period: in medieval Latin *abacus* had settled into

    "the Book of Calculation" is the more accurate rendering.

        alive today); the actual circulation of the book runs through

        Tuscan commercial textbook genre carries its DNA, across the
        Mediterranean, for three hundred years.

      en: |
        The internal structure of the 1202 edition (known by
        extrapolation from the 1228 version): fifteen chapters, roughly
        three sections. Chapters 1-7 the *method*: introduction of the
        Indian numerals, rules of positional notation, the algorithms
        of addition-subtraction-multiplication-division, fractions.
        Chapters 8-11 the *application*: profit-and-loss, alloying,
        currency exchange, ratio problems — the arithmetic problems of
        commercial life. Chapters 12-15 *harder problems*: a chapter of
        mixed problems (the rabbit-breeding sequence sits here in
        chapter 12), root extraction, algebraic methods (*al-jabr* and
        *al-muqābala* — the terminology coming from al-Khwārizmī). The
        structure is the same as a modern mathematics textbook: concept
        → application → generalisation. What the *Liber Abaci* brought
        was less *Hindu-Arabic numerals* than this *three-stage textbook
        model*: first the new technology, then the concrete application,
        then the theoretical generalisation.

      en: |
        To read the *Liber Abaci* as Fibonacci's solo work is
        misleading: the book is the Latin descendant of *Kitāb
        al-Ḥisāb al-Hindī* (the Book of Indian Arithmetic) that
        al-Khwārizmī wrote at Baghdad c. 825, and to be its Latin
        descendant it required two pre-Latin translations: that of
        John of Seville (or John of Carmona) around 1140, and Robert
        of Chester's *Dixit Algoritmi* of 1145. Without these two
        translations the *modus indorum* terminology that appears in
        Fibonacci's prologue would not have been settled in Latin, and
        he would have had no conceptual vocabulary with which to write
        in Latin what he learned at Bijāya. The line al-Khwārizmī →
        Robert of Chester → Fibonacci is *a book-chain*: the same
        concepts and the same algorithms, in three languages of three
        continents, in three generations, in the same order. Fibonacci
        is the last link; but he is *not* the one who forged the chain.
