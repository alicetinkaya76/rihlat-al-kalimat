      en: |
        Between Pacioli's 1494 Venetian *Summa* and Dell'Abbaco's
        1340 Florentine *Trattato* the content difference is very
        small — both are syntheses of the Tuscan abbaco tradition.
        The difference of diffusion, however, is dramatic. The
        *Trattato* was in Tuscan vernacular Italian and was never
        translated into any other European language (it remained
        within its local-pedagogical purpose). The *Summa*, being
        printed, reached four languages within sixty years: in
        1518 in Bavaria, Henricus Grammateus wrote *Ayn New
        Kunstlich Buech* (Augsburg), a German adaptation of
        Chapter 11. In 1543 in Antwerp, Jan Ympyn Christoffels
        published the *Nieuwe Instructie* — the first Dutch
        accounting book; a French translation followed in 1547,
        and in the same year an English translation. In 1553 in
        London, James Peele's *The maner and fourme how to kepe
        a perfect reconyng* (the first English accounting
        textbook) carried the text of Chapter 11, largely
        verbatim, into the merchant education of Tudor England.
        Same content, different channel: *print is wide and
        silent as oxygen; manuscript is not*. The traditional
        narrative says *Pacioli invented accounting*; the revised
        narrative says *Pacioli wired accounting into the
        Europe-wide channel* — and this is the real shift.
