      en: |
        In English today *the canonical text* names "the book of a field taken as standard": *canonical works of literature*, *canonical theorems*, *the standard canon*. The etymology of the word reaches back to Greek *kanōn*; but the decisive stage for the modern English-speaking sense is that Ibn Sīnā's *al-Qānūn fī al-Ṭibb*, in Latin titled *Canon*, was for six hundred years Europe's *most canonical* medical book. The modern semantic deposit of *canonical* — being the central text of a field — was built on this six-hundred-year reading habit. The book's name no longer appears in medical curricula (Vesalius, Harvey do); but every page of Vesalius's body-anatomy diagrams in the *De Humani Corporis Fabrica* (1543) carries reference to the *Canon*, marked by the intention to correct the Galen-Avicenna axis. The history of European medicine takes its position, from the start, relative to the *Canon*.

        Modern historiography of science (Nancy Siraisi's 1987 *Avicenna in Renaissance Italy*; Emilie Savage-Smith's 2007 *Medieval Islamic Medicine*) draws attention to a structural paradox in this continuity: the Renaissance physicians were promising a "*return to the original Greek sources*" (*ad fontes*), but in fact they were reading Galen through the *Canon* — that is, within Ibn Sīnā's overall structure. The synthesis had become transparent, invisible. Even where the reader believed himself to be returning to the original, his conceptual organization of medicine had already been shaped by an eleventh-century encyclopaedia. *Transparency-as-authority* — that is the central feature of "the canonical book".

      en: |
        From the fourteenth century to the middle of the seventeenth, the *Liber Canonis* was the required textbook in Europe's principal medical schools (Padua, Bologna, Montpellier, Paris, Salamanca, Oxford): a medical student would, across six or seven years of training, work through this text step by step. The first Latin print edition appeared in Milan in 1473; by 1500 there were twenty-five separate editions; through the sixteenth century, hundreds. The first botanical garden in Europe, founded at Padua in 1545, was raised to grow as living plants the materia medica listed in the fifth book of *al-Qānūn*. The Latin editions of the *Qānūn* are continuously enlarged annotated versions: small additions (Andrea Alpago's new Latin translation in the early sixteenth century, for example), great commentaries (Gentile da Foligno, Ferrari da Grado), seventeenth-century supplements integrating Padua's Santorio Santorio and his mechanical-medicine experiments. *Books written, for six hundred years, on a single book* — that is the structure of European medical education, and on every page of it stands the Arabic original written near Bukhara.

        Another significant event occurred in 1593: in Rome the Medici Oriental Press (Tipografia Medicea Orientale) printed the Arabic original of *al-Qānūn* — the first Arabic book printed in Europe. This printing was 110 years after the 1483 Latin edition, but its meaning was very different: the Latin printing was translation-for-distribution; the Arabic printing was preservation-through-publication. The intention of the Medici press in Rome was to print Arabic religious texts for Eastern Christianity (not for the Muslim East), but as a by-product *al-Qānūn* — the European medical standard — became available in Europe *in its own language*. This 1593 edition became, over the following two centuries, one of the fundamental texts on which European Arabists learnt Arabic (Erpenius and Pococke used it as a textbook). A book of medicine, indirectly, became a book for the teaching of a language.

      en: |
        Gerard of Cremona (1114-1187), the most productive member of the Toledo translation school, completed the Latin translation of *al-Qānūn fī al-Ṭibb* under the title *Liber Canonis* in the year of his death, 1187. Gerard's team (some eight or ten notarised assistants) used a word-by-word structural method that followed the Arabic; the result was sometimes not "Latin in syntax" but preserved the technical vocabulary of the Arabic original in full. This translation was the only Latin text in use for the next three centuries — until Andrea Alpago in the early sixteenth century produced a smoother Latin version (and even that version retained Gerard's foundational vocabulary). The historian of science Charles Burnett describes this translation as *the birth certificate of European medical Latin*: the body systems, the categories of disease, the drug groups, the anatomical nomenclature — the Latin word for all of them was set on Gerard's desk. *Europe read Avicenna with the eyes of Gerard of Cremona.*

        The method-detail of the translation is a paradigmatic example for modern medical linguistics. Gerard chose the *word-for-word* (verbum ad verbum) method rather than the interpretive (ad sensum); for every Arabic medical term he either found a Latin equivalent (e.g. *al-ṭibb* → *medicina*, *al-maraḍ* → *morbus*) or transferred the Arabic *as it was* into Latin (e.g. *al-qālūn* → the Latinised *colon*, *al-ribāṭ* → *retina*). In this second group there are hundreds of words that have settled permanently into the European anatomy-pharmacy vocabulary: *retina*, *cornea*, *aorta*, *basilica* (the basilic vein), *cephalica*, *saphena*, *zygote*, *Algola* (from al-kuḥl), *syrupus* (syrup), *jujube*, *cassia*, *coriander*. Around thirty percent of the spine of modern European medical Latin was therefore set on Gerard's desk in 1187. This is not a miracle but a discipline: the terminological consolidation of European medical education over multiple centuries was done by the man who, in a single night's work, was carrying *al-Qānūn* from Arabic into Latin.

        Ibn Sīnā (980-1037) did not write *al-Qānūn* in one place at one sitting: after the collapse of the Samanid dynasty (1005), Avicenna in search of patrons moved from Bukhara to Gurganj, then to Gurgan, Rayy, Hamadan; each court provided a patron, each patron a library and a protection — and then each fell. Over those decades he wrote the five books of *al-Qānūn* in pieces: book I (general principles of medicine), II (simple drugs, *materia medica*), III (the topology of disease by organ), IV (the general diseases — fever, tumour, fracture), V (compound drugs). He began around 1012 and finished around 1024. The broad, coherent structure of the work emerged not in spite of the conditions of its piecemeal composition but *because of them*: a wandering physician at every new station read what he had previously written, revisited the system, tightened the technical vocabulary. In his autobiography (Ibn Sīnā is among the rare medieval figures to have written his own life) there is no direct account of this process; but his student al-Jūzjānī, in his biographical continuation, records the place and patronage context of each of the medical books.

        The structural originality of *al-Qānūn* was not merely the organisation of Galen's corpus under a single title: Ibn Sīnā synthesised, within *a new theoretical framework*, Galen's principal medical works (the sixteen books of the *Methodus Medendi* and others), al-Rāzī's *al-Ḥāwī* (sixty books), the Hippocratic corpus and pieces of the Indo-Persian medical traditions. At the centre of the novelty is book III, which arranges diseases by the organs of the body (organotopic). Galen organised diseases by clusters of symptoms, al-Rāzī by case, the older medical tradition by humoral-temperamental balance; Ibn Sīnā was the first to introduce diseases within an *anatomical order* — from head to foot, system by system (the diseases of the head, the eye, the heart and respiration, digestion, urogenital, the body surface, the extremities). This organotopic framework is the direct ancestor of modern medicine's clinical organisation: today's medical-faculty divisions into *cardiology*, *gastroenterology*, *dermatology* are continuations of the partition of the eleven sections of Ibn Sīnā's book III.

      en: |
        Historians of science often describe Ibn Sīnā's *al-Qānūn* not as *a new book* so much as *a synthesising masterwork*: the book contains individual new findings (the classification of fevers, certain visual disorders, certain drug interactions), but the achievement of the book is its *unifying frame*. The precondition of that frame is the passage of Galen into Arabic through Ḥunayn (around 840-870) and especially the *al-Ḥāwī* of al-Rāzī (d. 925) — a physician's daily notes posthumously assembled into an encyclopedic book, but in structure piecemeal, in no way *a standard*. The *standard book* Avicenna needed — coherent, systematic, usable for instruction — was lying on his desk only as the scattered pieces of the Galen-Rāzī tradition. What he did was to *re-position* the scattered material in a coherent structure. The title of the book speaks not just of *medicine* but of the *Canon in Medicine*. *There was no standard for the field before; he made the standard.*

        An important intermediate example: ʿAlī ibn al-ʿAbbās al-Majūsī's *Kāmil al-Ṣināʿa al-Ṭibbiyya* (The Perfection of the Medical Art, c. 980 — also known as *al-Kitāb al-Malikī*, "the Royal Book"; translated into Latin as *Liber Regalis*). Al-Majūsī presented it to the Buyid emir ʿAḍud ad-Dawla; he had begun, ten years earlier in the 970s, the search for *a standard book* for medical instruction — but his structure proved insufficient. *Al-Kitāb al-Malikī* carried the intention of bringing Galen's corpus into a systematic order, but the structure still followed the symptom-based framework of Galen. Forty years later Ibn Sīnā did what al-Majūsī had aimed at, but with *a more radical restructuring*. The success of *al-Qānūn* is therefore not the work of one man — it is the arrival point of a century-long *search for a standard book*, from Ḥunayn to al-Rāzī, from al-Majūsī to Ibn Sīnā. Avicenna either won the race or the search ended on his desk.
