        the Orientalist circles of Europe; it became the basic

      en: |
        At the court of the Mughal emperor Akbar in 1587, Abū
        al-Faiz Faizi — court poet, philologist, brother of Abū
        al-Fazl — translated the *Līlāvatī* from Sanskrit into
        Persian. The translation was part of Akbar's systematic
        cultural programme: to render the classical texts of the
        empire's Hindu population into Persian, the language of
        the Islamic ruling elite. Within the same programme the
        *Mahābhārata* (titled the *Bāzīm-i Akbarī*), the
        *Rāmāyaṇa* and the *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* were also translated
        into Persian. Faizi's *Līlāvatī* runs to 432 pages — the
        original Sanskrit text + the Persian translation + a long
        preface by Faizi, which links the problem-types to the
        Islamic mathematical tradition. This translation is one of
        the most concrete documents of the Indo-Islamic
        mathematical interface: it represents the other direction
        of the Hindu-Arabic numerals chain — Bhāskara II's
        Sanskrit text being re-transmitted to the Islamic world
        four hundred and thirty-seven years later (the reverse of
        the direction al-Bīrūnī had taken in the twelfth century).
        Faizi's manuscript is preserved today in the *Salar Jung
        Museum* at Hyderabad and the *Majlis Library* at Tehran.

      en: |
        The tradition of presenting mathematical problems in poetic
        form was not invented by Bhāskara II around 1150. The
        writing of arithmetic problems in poetic form in Sanskrit
        mathematical texts begins in the first century with the
        *Āryabhaṭīya* of Āryabhaṭa; is continued in the seventh
        century in the mathematical sections of Brahmagupta's
        *Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta*; in the ninth century, Mahāvīra's
        *Gaṇitasārasaṅgraha* (c. 850) is a pure-arithmetic poetic
        text — to be reckoned the closest predecessor of Bhāskara
        II. Mahāvīra's work is eleven hundred verses, Pythagorean
        triples, combinations, cistern/pool problems. In writing
        the *Līlāvatī*, Bhāskara II positions himself as the last
        link of this three-centuries-old tradition; the structure
        of the *Līlāvatī* (introductory verse → thirteen sections
        → problem-examples) is taken from Mahāvīra, but its poetic
        fluency and pedagogical tenderness (the tone of being
        written for a daughter) are Bhāskara II's contribution.
        For a long time the modern history of mathematics
        treated the *Līlāvatī* as an isolated masterwork; from the
        mid-twentieth century (Datta and Singh 1962, Plofker 2009)
        it has been repositioned as *the Bhāskara summit of the
        Indian tradition of mathematical poetry*. This recontextualisation
        does not lessen the originality of the *Līlāvatī* — it
        shows that a masterwork is not born without a tradition,
        and Bhāskara II himself explicitly names Āryabhaṭa,
        Brahmagupta and Mahāvīra as his predecessors.
