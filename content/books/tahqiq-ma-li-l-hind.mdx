titleMeaning:
  tr: |
    Kelime anlamı: "Hint'in Aklın Kabul veya Reddettiği Sözleri
    Hakkında Tahkik." Arapça *taḥqīq* = doğrulama, kritik
    inceleme, ilmi soruşturma (modern Arapça'da hâlâ
    *editio-critical-edition* için kullanılır; al-Bīrūnī
    terimi tam bu metodolojik anlamda kullanır). Başlığın iki
    yarısı al-Bīrūnī'nin metodunu açıkça duyurur: (i) Hint
    sözlerini *kabul et ya da reddet diye değil aktar*; (ii)
    sonra *aklın kabul edip etmediğini* ayrıca tartış. Modern
    Avrupa antropolojisinin *emic-etic* ayrımı bu yapıya —
    sözleri kaynaktan duyup aktarmak ile dış-gözle değerlendirmek
    arasındaki çift-katmanlı yöntem — şaşırtıcı şekilde paraleldir.
    Geleneksel kısa biçim *Tārīkh al-Hind* ("Hint Tarihi") — modern
    Arapça referansta yaygın ama yanıltıcı çünkü kitap tarih
    değil karşılaştırmalı kültür-etnografisidir.
  en: |
    Literal sense: "An Accurate Enquiry into What India Says,
    Accepted or Rejected by Reason." Arabic *taḥqīq* =
    verification, critical examination, scholarly enquiry (still
    used in modern Arabic for an *editio critical edition*;
    al-Bīrūnī uses the term in precisely this methodological
    sense). The two halves of the title openly declare
    al-Bīrūnī's method: (i) *transmit* the sayings of India
    rather than accept or reject them; (ii) then *separately
    discuss* whether they are accepted by reason. The modern
    European anthropological *emic-etic* distinction is
    strikingly parallel to this structure. The traditional
    short form is *Tārīkh al-Hind* ("History of India") —
    widespread in modern Arabic reference but misleading, since
    the book is not a history but a comparative cultural
    ethnography.

        epistemological pattern in which the West represents
        the East in a way the East cannot represent itself.

        Sabra (1996), Bruce Lawrence (1976), Vivek Sharma
        (2014) in particular — read the *Tahqīq mā li'l-Hind*

        al-Bīrūnī writes not to *criticise* Hindu beliefs but
        to *transmit* them, and learns Sanskrit from primary
        speakers, giving his sources directly. The two
        methodological principles of modern comparative
        religious studies — *learning the language of the
        source* + *transmitting before interpreting* — are
        read as the method al-Bīrūnī laid down in 1030. The
        rule of the *Tahqīq* — *translate the Brahmin's own
        words into Arabic* — is a thousand-year-anticipation
        of the *insider-voice* principle that Bronisław
        Malinowski's method of participant observation (1922,
        *Argonauts of the Western Pacific*) makes explicit. In
        her 2014 book *The Hindi-Urdu Cultural Borderland*,
        Sharma calls the *Tahqīq* "the founding text of
        comparative Indology in any language."

      en: |
        Edward Sachau (1845-1930), Orientalist of the
        University of Berlin, published in 1887 in London with
        Trübner & Co. the editio princeps Arabic edition of
        the *Tahqīq*: it became the only modern critical
        source. A year later (1888) his two-volume English
        translation, *Alberuni's India*, came out from the
        same publisher. The circulation of the *Tahqīq* in
        the Arab world had been unbroken — from the death of
        al-Bīrūnī (c. 1048) to the modern period at least
        fourteen Arabic copies are known, the principal
        collections being Istanbul (Köprülü 1062), the Berlin
        Staatsbibliothek (Ahlwardt 5666), Cairo Dār al-Kutub,
        and Patna Khuda Bakhsh Library. But within the
        European scholarly community the *Tahqīq* was unknown
        before Sachau; when William Jones (1746-1794)
        introduced Sanskritology to Europe he was unaware of
        the work al-Bīrūnī had done eight hundred years
        earlier. Sachau's translation enters the canon of
        Europe's anthropological reading of India; alongside
        Friedrich Max Müller's *Sacred Books of the East*
        series (1879-1910) it is one of the two principal
        modern starting points of Indology. Sachau defines
        his method in one sentence (preface, p. xliv): *"In
        this matter al-Bīrūnī is a more modern philologist
        than we are — Müller included."*

        Naṣr Manṣūr ibn ʿIrāq (al-Bīrūnī's tutor in
        mathematics), the young Ibn Sīnā (court physician,
        1004-1012), the philosopher-theologian Abū al-Khayr
        al-Khammār, the physicist Abū Sahl al-Masīḥī all worked
        at the same circle. What al-Bīrūnī learned at the court
        was, beyond technical knowledge, a *plurality of
        method*: to discuss the same question from the four
        separate frames of Greek philosophy, Indian astronomy,
        Iranian calendar systems, Syriac astrology and Arabic
        mathematics. This habit of multiple frames is the very
        core of the structure of the *Tahqīq*. The biography of
        Yāqūt al-Ḥamawī (in the *Muʿjam al-Buldān*, c. 1228),
        written two centuries after al-Bīrūnī's death, describes
        him so: *"He grew up in Khwārazm, then in Gurgān, then
        in Rayy, then in Ghazna; in each city he learned one
        more language; no city did he see as his own."* Yāqūt's
        sentence captures the psychological core of al-Bīrūnī's
        formation: *suspension of judgement* cannot be attached
        to a single method; it is born as *the caution of one
        who has grown accustomed to a plurality of places*.
        This is the source of the speed of al-Bīrūnī's
        acquisition of Sanskrit — a person who can learn a
        third language at forty-four has already learned a
        second as a child.

        Chapters 14-18 of the *Tahqīq* transmit the Indian
        number system directly from the Sanskrit source into
        Arabic: al-Bīrūnī examines for the first time
        systematically in Arabic the Sanskrit semantic layers
        of the term *śūnya* (empty, zero), and transmits
        Brahmagupta's rules of arithmetic with zero in the
        original Sanskrit terminology (in parenthesis). This
        channel parallels al-Khwārizmī's Baghdad *Sindhind*
        channel (c. 825), but the method is different:
        al-Khwārizmī uses a second-hand Sanskrit text (via
        earlier Pahlavi translations); al-Bīrūnī does first-hand
        Sanskrit-into-Arabic translation. The *Tahqīq* is the
        second main channel of the passage of the Hindu-Arabic
        numeral system into the Muslim world — *the channel of
        observation*, alongside *the channel of text*.
