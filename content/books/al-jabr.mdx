        In 1831 the German émigré Orientalist *Frederic August Rosen*
        published, through the Royal Asiatic Society, a parallel
        Arabic-English edition of al-Khwārizmī's *al-Jabr*. Rosen's
        base text was the only complete Arabic copy then known in
        Europe: Oxford, Bodleian Library, MS Huntington 214, a
        fourteenth-century manuscript. The edition was Europe's first
        serious philological encounter with the Arabic original of the
        text that named its discipline; until then, the Arabic source
        had been a known-but-unseen reference in the European history
        of mathematics.

        One hundred and seventy-six years later, in 2007, *Roshdi
        Rashed* issued in Paris a new critical edition: a parallel
        Arabic-French text drawn from collation of seventeen
        manuscripts, with a full philological apparatus. Rashed's
        edition for the first time made the work readable in its
        actual textual complexity — not as *one* original but as *a
        family scattered along the lines of transmission*.

        The 176-year gap between the two editions tells its own story:
        it marks the distance between the text's *conceptual* arrival
        in Europe (twelfth-century Latin translations) and its
        *philological* arrival (nineteenth- and twenty-first-century
        critical editions). For seven centuries, the European algebraic
        tradition had been building on this foundation without ever
        reading the foundation's text.

        By the early sixteenth century the word *algebra* stood, in
        the Italian mathematical lexicon, for an autonomous discipline
        — but the word had long since detached from the book and the
        author behind it. Cardano in his *Ars Magna* (1545) knew that
        algebraic techniques had Arabic origins, and mentions
        "*Mahomet*" once — but his "Mahomet" is most likely not
        al-Khwārizmī by clear identification, only a half-remembered
        shadow figure encountered through Latin translation. For
        Tartaglia, Ferrari, and the other Italian masters, algebra
        felt less like a discipline emerging out of another tradition
        than like one being rediscovered.

        When François Viète laid the foundations of symbolic algebra
        in his *In artem analyticen isagoge* (1591), al-Khwārizmī's
        name was wholly forgotten; only the words *algebra* and
        *algorismus* remained, two technical terms that no one any
        longer asked about, simply parts of Europe's mathematical
        tongue. This forgetting marks a kind of *success*: when a book
        is buried deep enough into a discipline that the discipline
        takes its name without remembering it, the book has, in one
        sense, won.

        But this same forgetting produced a *displacement of origin*.
        The European algebraic tradition began to locate its root in
        Greece (Diophantus), not in ninth-century Baghdad. For
        seventeenth- and eighteenth-century historians of mathematics,
        *algebra* was a European story running from Diophantus to
        Viète; the eight-century Arabic-Islamic link in the middle was
        passed over quickly or recorded merely as "transmission."

        sesi gibiydi. Yazarın siliklenmesi, ileride *algebra*'nın kim
        tarafından kurulduğu sorusunun nasıl unutulacağının habercisidir.

        Arabic text into Latin: *Liber algebrae et almucabola*. Twenty
        or thirty years later (~1170), Gerard of Cremona — the most
        prolific of the Toledo translators — re-translated the same
        text from scratch, with greater philological care. Within
        another generation, a third Latin version by William of Lunis
        was also in circulation.

        These three translations — particularly those of Robert and
        Gerard — circulated side by side in the European universities
        of the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries. Each gave a
        different reading: Robert's fuller but sometimes inaccurate,
        Gerard's more meticulous but partly abridged. The word itself
        — *algebra* — was now the Latin name for both the discipline
        and the book; "*almucabola*" travelled alongside as a secondary
        term (from Arabic *al-muqābala*, "balancing"). Over the
        centuries *almucabola* dropped away; only *algebra* survived.

        One feature of the translations is striking: *the author
        himself is barely visible in them*. Robert opens his version
        with "*Dixit Algorismi*" — "Algorismi (i.e. al-Khwārizmī) says"
        — but the rest of the work gives the Latin reader no further
        reference to the author. The book, for the Latin reader,
        sounded less like the voice of an author and more like that
        of a transmitter, an anonymous conduit. The author's effacement
        here forecasts the eventual forgetting, in the next stratum
        up, of who founded *algebra* in the first place.

        al-Khwārizmī wrote the book by command of Caliph al-Maʾmūn,
        and explicitly for *practical* needs. The list of applications
        he gives in the preface is in fact a portrait of life in an
        Islamic legal and administrative society: *mawārīth* (the
        partition of inheritance — Islamic inheritance law requires
        intricate fractional shares), *wasiyyāt* (legacies, governed
        by the one-third rule), *muʿāmalāt* (trade and debt),
        *misāḥa* (land surveying), *canal-digging* (the irrigation
        engineering of the Baghdad floodplain), and *geometry*.

        The practical orientation notwithstanding, the book's
        structural core is wholly conceptual. al-Khwārizmī reduces
        every algebraic equation to *six canonical forms* — in modern
        notation: *ax² = bx*, *ax² = c*, *bx = c*, *ax² + bx = c*,
        *ax² + c = bx*, and *bx + c = ax²*. Since the concept of
        negative numbers had not yet been settled, each type is
        treated as a distinct family of equations. For each type he
        then gives a solution by the operations of *al-jabr* (adding
        to both sides what is missing) and *al-muqābala* (cancelling
        like terms across both sides).

        An important detail: each algebraic rule is followed, in the
        body of the work, by a *geometric proof* — completing-the-
        square diagrams, the dissection and rearrangement of
        rectangles. This is the direct witness of how Arabic
        mathematics integrated its Greek geometric inheritance: the
        arithmetic rule is granted legitimacy by the geometric figure.
        The second half of the book, by contrast, is given over to
        the practical problems of inheritance calculation: real family
        configurations, real share ratios, real complications.
