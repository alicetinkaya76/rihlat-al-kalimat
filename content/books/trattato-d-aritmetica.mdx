      en: |
        Between c. 1340 and Dell'Abbaco's death in 1374 the
        *Trattato* was used as the principal textbook of the
        Florentine abbaco schools — particularly the main school
        near Santa Trinità. **Target audience**: the sons of
        wool-merchants, candidate bank-clerks, the apprentices of
        traders shipping goods, the prospective junior clerks of
        the tax office. The educational model: between the ages
        of eight and twelve the child attended the abbaco school
        for two or three years; studied the sections of the
        *Trattato* *from end to end*; then entered the family
        business or was apprenticed to a bank or trading house.
        The page-structure of the book matches this practical
        aim: each section first states the rule, then works
        through ten to twenty example problems (usually real-world
        scenarios — *"A Florentine merchant has sent two hundred
        florins of woollen cloth to the Levant, expecting a 12%
        profit; at Acre the cloth was sold for eight ducats of
        tarì; calculate his total earnings in ducats and
        florins"*). This practice-oriented pedagogy is the
        five-hundred-year ancestor of modern applied-mathematics
        education. As poetic and playful as the *Līlāvatī*'s
        problems are in modern India, so commercial and pragmatic
        are the Florentine *Trattato*'s.

        the *academic community* (the university, the monastery,

        practical content was of great value to the Tuscan
        merchants. From the last quarter of the 1200s onwards, at
        Pisa, then at Florence, Siena and Lucca, teachers began to
        rewrite the methods of the *Liber Abaci* as small
        notebooks in Tuscan vernacular Italian — the first
        movement of the *libri d'abbaco* (abacist notebooks).
        Modern historians of science count more than two hundred
        of these small manuscript *libri d'abbaco* between 1240
        and 1340; most are twenty to forty pages, anonymous or
        from teachers of little name, all of them
        local/pedagogical adaptations of Fibonacci's structure. In
        writing the *Trattato* Dell'Abbaco *synthesised in a single
        comprehensive book* this hundred-and-forty-year
        accumulation: thirteen sections, more than two hundred
        pages, each section first the rule, then the example.
        The *Trattato* is therefore neither an *invention* nor a
        *rewriting* — it is a *canon-making synthesis*. Another
        ancestor-form of the modern textbook: a single teacher
        gathering a tradition into a single book.
